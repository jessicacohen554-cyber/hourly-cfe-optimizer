1. The Question: What Happens When Corporations Go Hourly?

The convergence of three forces is about to reshape clean energy markets:

  • GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision (expected late 2027) will likely require hourly matching with ISO-level deliverability for corporate emissions reporting[6]
  • SBTi hourly matching requirements (starting 2030) will mandate time-matched clean energy procurement for ~8,000 companies with validated targets[7]
  • Corporate 24/7 CFE commitments from Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and other hyperscalers are already driving multi-GW procurement of firm clean energy[5]

Under annual matching, a corporation buys enough RECs to cover total annual consumption — timing doesn’t matter. Under hourly matching, every hour of consumption must be matched with clean generation in that same hour, in the same ISO region. This transforms the problem: daytime solar surplus becomes worthless for matching nighttime demand. The binding constraint shifts to evening and nighttime hours where only nuclear, geothermal, wind, storage, or CCS can deliver.

Why 10–25% participation, not 50%? Current voluntary clean energy procurement (CEBA data) represents ~5–8% of load in PJM and ~10–15% in ERCOT.[5] Even with GHG Protocol[6] and SBTi[7] driving adoption, realistic near-term participation is 10–25% of total ISO load. The analysis uses this range because it represents the plausible scenario where price signals emerge — not a hypothetical maximum.

Hourly matching ≠ matching every hour equally. A 90% hourly matching score means 90% of total annual load is matched hour-by-hour — but the remaining 10% can be concentrated in the hardest hours (winter nights, multi-day low-wind events). A buyer can match 100% during sunny hours and fall short overnight while still hitting 90% overall. This makes 90% more achievable than it first sounds, but it also means the last 10% (getting from 90% to 100%) is disproportionately expensive because it requires firm clean energy during the hardest hours.

2. Standard Supply Service: Everyone Starts Above Zero

The GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision[6] introduces Standard Supply Service (SSS) — the concept that every electricity consumer automatically receives a pro-rata share of their region’s existing clean energy. Under SSS, corporate buyers don’t start from zero. They start from the regional clean baseline and procure the incremental gap to reach their target.

What counts as SSS baseline?

  • Nuclear generation — allocated pro-rata to all load (where not locked into specific PPAs or state programs)
  • RPS-driven renewables — state mandated clean generation credited to the general supply mix[4]
  • Rate-base clean assets — utility-owned clean generation in regulated territories
  • Publicly owned assets — federal hydropower (BPA, TVA, WAPA), municipal utility generation

A PJM buyer targeting 95% hourly matching gets ~43% from SSS during the day and ~35% at night. Their incremental procurement need is ~52% during daytime and ~60% at night — the nighttime gap is the binding constraint.

3. EAC Scarcity by ISO Region

After SSS baseline allocation and state RPS demand is netted out, the remaining “uncommitted” clean supply is the pool available for voluntary corporate procurement. When corporate hourly EAC demand exceeds this pool in specific hours, the region experiences scarcity — and a clean premium emerges.

Likely Scarcity

PJM: The Tightest Market in the US

Clean supply: ~345 TWh/yr (nuclear ~275[8], wind ~35, solar ~25, hydro ~10) against ~800 TWh load.[1][9]

RPS demand: State mandates in NJ, VA, MD, IL collectively require ~120–150 TWh of RECs by 2030.[4] Critical nuance: most PJM state RPS programs require renewable energy certificates — nuclear does not qualify. Nuclear support comes through separate state programs (IL CEJA, NJ ZEC), several of which are transitioning or rolling off by late 2020s.

After SSS + RPS netting: The renewable-only RPS constraint means ~120–150 TWh of REC demand competes specifically for wind, solar, and hydro supply (~70 TWh total in PJM).[9] This creates a renewable-specific shortfall that state mandates will fill with new build. Meanwhile, nuclear (~275 TWh, ~90% capacity factor[8]) satisfies most of the SSS baseline but is being rapidly consumed by data center PPAs (Constellation Crane restart, Talen Susquehanna). Virtually no uncommitted clean supply remains for voluntary hourly matching.

The PJM squeeze: At just 10–15% corporate participation[5] with 90%+ hourly matching targets, PJM faces structural nighttime EAC scarcity. Nuclear PPAs lock up the only 24/7 clean supply. State RPS mandates claim all renewables.[4] New build faces 3–5 year interconnection queue delays.[10] PJM is where hourly matching creates the strongest price signal for CCS, new nuclear, and LDES.[2][3]

Empirical confirmation: PJM Tier I REC prices have already reached $37–40/MWh (historic highs).[11] PJM capacity market cleared at $333/MW-day in 2025 (10x increase).[11] These are real-market signals of emerging scarcity before hourly matching is even mandated.

Moderate Scarcity

ISO-NE: Transmission-Constrained, Aggressive Mandates

Clean supply: ~55 TWh/yr (nuclear ~27[8], hydro ~8, wind ~10, solar ~10[12]) against ~120 TWh load.[1][9]

RPS demand: MA, CT, RI, VT, ME mandates collectively require ~50–60 TWh[4] — consuming nearly the entire existing clean supply.

After SSS + RPS: Minimal surplus. Millstone and Seabrook (~27 TWh, ~90% capacity factor[8]) partially committed to state ZEC programs. Aggressive state mandates leave little room for voluntary procurement without new build.

Key constraint: Transmission limitations restrict Canadian hydro imports. Offshore wind faces permitting and interconnection delays[10] (Vineyard Wind 1 experience). Massachusetts Class I RECs at $39–40/MWh near ACP ceiling — empirical scarcity already visible.

Moderate — Evening Gap

CAISO: Daytime Abundance, Nighttime Deficit

Clean supply: ~130 TWh/yr (solar ~55, wind ~20, geothermal ~12, hydro ~25, nuclear ~18[8]) against ~280 TWh load.[1][9] Already ~62% clean on annual basis with 15+ GW battery storage deployed.

RPS demand: SB 100 mandates 60% RPS by 2030 = ~170 TWh.[4] Current clean supply falls short, driving aggressive procurement.

The duck curve problem under hourly matching: California has abundant daytime clean energy but faces a structural deficit from 5 PM to 8 AM.[1] Under hourly matching, this evening/night gap is the binding constraint. Even with 12+ GW of battery storage, multi-day low-wind events create persistent deficits requiring clean firm (geothermal, CCS) or imported clean energy.

CCS opportunity: California’s ~6 GW gas fleet could provide hourly-matched clean firm if equipped with CCS. With 45Q credits + state SB 905 incentives, this may be the fastest path to filling the evening gap.

Unlikely Scarcity

ERCOT: Abundant and Growing Supply

Clean supply: ~150 TWh/yr (wind ~95, solar ~35, nuclear ~20[8]) against ~470 TWh load.[1][9] Now ~65 GW of wind+solar installed — growing rapidly (15 GW solar interconnected in 2024 alone).[1] 14% load growth from data centers and industrial electrification.

RPS demand: Texas has no binding RPS.[4] 0 TWh mandatory demand. All corporate procurement is voluntary.[5]

After SSS: Even after pro-rata allocation (~32% baseline), ERCOT’s explosive renewable growth means abundant daytime EAC supply at low cost. South Plains wind produces heavily at night, partially filling the evening gap.

ERCOT may never see EAC scarcity for corporate buyers. No state RPS competing for EACs.[4] Rapid deployment driven by favorable economics.[2][3] RECs trade at just $1–15/MWh. The combination makes ERCOT the most cost-effective ISO for corporate hourly matching at the lowest clean premium. This is the strongest pull factor for corporate load placement decisions — and explains why 70% of recent corporate clean energy procurement concentrates in ERCOT/MISO/PJM.[5]

Moderate Scarcity

NYISO: Hydro-Anchored but Post-Indian-Point

Clean supply: ~75 TWh/yr (hydro ~30, nuclear ~30[8], wind ~8, solar ~7) against ~155 TWh load.[1][9]

RPS demand: CLCPA mandates 70% renewable by 2030, 100% zero-emission by 2040.[4] Current trajectory: ~60% clean by 2030.

Key dynamics: Champlain Hudson Power Express (CHPE) delivers ~10 TWh/yr Canadian hydro to NYC by 2026. But Indian Point closure (2021) removed ~17 TWh of zero-emission nuclear.[8] NYC load zone (Zone J) faces the tightest supply-demand balance. Offshore wind pipeline critical but faces cost escalation and permitting uncertainty.[10]

At 95% and 100% targets

Raising the hourly matching target from 90% to 95% or 100% shifts scarcity classifications upward by approximately one tier across all ISOs. The final 5–10% of matching requires clean firm energy during the absolute hardest hours (multi-day winter doldrums, overnight demand peaks) and carries disproportionate cost. This is the “last mile” problem that the Cost Optimizer quantifies in detail.

5. The Clean Premium by ISO

EAC scarcity translates directly into a clean premium — the additional cost above wholesale energy prices that corporate buyers pay for hourly-matched clean energy. The premium varies by ISO, time of day, and participation level:

PJM: $15–40/MWh

Highest clean premium in the US. Evening/night hours face structural deficit. Nuclear PPAs with data centers are removing existing supply from the voluntary market. At 15%+ participation[5], PJM buyers need new-build clean firm at premium prices.[2][3] CCS-CCGT and LDES earn their highest revenue in PJM.

Empirical: PJM Tier I RECs already at $37–40/MWh.[11] Capacity market at $333/MW-day (10x increase).[11]

ISO-NE: $20–55/MWh

Already-high MA Class I REC prices ($39–40/MWh near ACP ceiling) indicate existing scarcity. Hourly matching on top of aggressive state mandates[4] pushes premiums higher. Limited land and transmission mean new supply is expensive and slow.[10]

CAISO: $10–25/MWh

Daytime surplus keeps average premium low. Evening/night hours (5 PM–8 AM) face $20–40/MWh premiums.[1] 12+ GW battery storage partially mitigates but cannot cover multi-day events. Geothermal expansion could moderate nighttime premiums.[2]

ERCOT: $0–15/MWh

Lowest clean premium. Abundant and growing wind+solar.[1] No RPS competing for EACs.[4] Nighttime wind fills much of the evening gap. RECs at $1–15/MWh. Most cost-effective ISO for corporate hourly matching.[5]

NYISO: $15–35/MWh

Hydro provides strong daytime/seasonal baseline. Post-Indian Point nuclear gap increases nighttime scarcity.[8] CHPE Canadian hydro import helps but doesn’t fully close the gap. Zone J (NYC) faces the steepest premiums.

6. The Investment Signal

The regional variation in clean premiums creates a powerful signal for both corporate load placement and generator investment:

For Corporate Buyers

Companies choosing where to build data centers or manufacturing plants will factor in hourly matching costs. ERCOT and CAISO offer the lowest clean premiums; PJM and ISO-NE the highest. The clean premium becomes a location cost comparable to energy prices, tax incentives, and labor costs.

For Generators

Generators in PJM that invest in CCS or clean firm capacity now will capture the premium as corporate demand grows.[13] The hourly clean premium is the market mechanism that connects voluntary corporate ambition to physical generator decarbonization investment. First movers earn the scarcity premium; followers compete it away.

For Policymakers

The clean premium quantifies the cost of inaction on transmission and interconnection reform. PJM’s $15–40/MWh premium is partly a product of its 800+ GW interconnection queue backlog.[10] Streamlining permitting and building transmission directly lowers the corporate cost of hourly matching — and accelerates generator decarbonization.

The virtuous cycle: Corporate hourly matching demand → EAC scarcity in constrained ISOs → clean premium signal → generator investment in clean firm → new supply reduces scarcity → lower premiums attract more corporate participation. The clean premium is the market mechanism that makes voluntary corporate ambition translate into physical decarbonization.
