How Would Dispatchable CCS or Nuclear Change Wholesale Prices?

Market Design February 2026
Merit Order & LMP Formation: Current vs. With Dispatchable Clean Resources
Today's Merit Order
With Dispatchable Nuclear & CCS

Same demand level, different clearing price. Nuclear flexibility shifts the marginal generator from gas ($42/MWh) to nuclear ($8/MWh) in most hours.

The Merit Order Sets the Price

Wholesale electricity prices in organized U.S. markets (LMPs) are determined by the marginal generator — the most expensive unit dispatched to meet demand in any given hour. Today, natural gas combined-cycle plants set the clearing price in most hours across all five major ISOs, with marginal costs typically ranging from $35–50/MWh depending on regional gas prices and plant heat rates. This means every generator on the grid — including zero-marginal-cost renewables — gets paid the gas plant's price. The merit order is the foundation of price formation, and any resource that changes which generator is marginal fundamentally reshapes wholesale economics.

CCS on the Margin: Prices Go Up

Carbon capture equipment imposes a 25–30% energy penalty on a combined-cycle gas plant. The same fuel input produces less electricity, raising the effective heat rate and marginal cost per MWh. Add CO₂ transport and storage variable costs ($5–10/MWh), and a CCS-equipped CCGT sets LMPs roughly $10–25/MWh higher than an equivalent unabated plant at the same gas price. In markets with a carbon price (e.g., EU ETS at $60–100/ton), this premium narrows because unabated gas internalizes emission costs too. Above approximately $50–80/ton CO₂, CCS plants can actually set lower clearing prices than carbon-burdened gas. But in today's U.S. market with no federal carbon price, CCS unambiguously raises LMPs whenever it's the marginal unit.

Dispatchable Nuclear: Prices Get Crushed

Advanced nuclear designs (SMRs, load-following reactors) would bid into the market at $5–10/MWh marginal cost — comparable to renewables, but available in every hour regardless of weather or time of day. Unlike solar and wind, which suppress prices only during their production hours, flexible nuclear would push LMPs down during evening peaks, winter mornings, and overnight periods where gas currently commands premium prices. The result is a dramatically compressed peak-to-trough price spread: fewer high-price hours means less arbitrage revenue for battery storage and a deepening “missing money” problem for mid-merit gas plants that depend on peak margins for economic viability.

Both Together: A Bimodal Price World

If both resources dispatch flexibly, the supply curve reshapes into a steeper staircase. Nuclear displaces gas in most hours, pushing CCS-CCGT to the margins — needed only when demand exceeds nuclear plus renewable capacity. The resulting price distribution becomes bimodal: a dense cluster of hours near $0–10/MWh (when nuclear and renewables are sufficient) and a thinner tail of $55–80/MWh hours (when CCS or peakers are needed). For corporate buyers pursuing 24/7 carbon-free energy, this is largely good news. Average procurement costs drop substantially. But the last few percentage points of hourly matching (95% → 100% CFE) still depend on which resource fills the hardest hours, and those hours still carry premium pricing.

Implication for CFE Procurement

Dispatchable nuclear is unambiguously beneficial for corporate CFE buyers: lower LMPs across all hours reduce procurement costs. CCS is more nuanced — it raises LMPs when marginal, but provides the firm clean capacity needed to avoid the cost cliff at high matching thresholds. The optimal procurement portfolio shifts: less storage arbitrage, less renewable overbuilding, and the “last mile” to 100% hourly matching becomes more accessible. The resource that sets the price in the hardest-to-match hours determines whether the 95–100% climb is a gentle slope or a cost cliff.
