Least-Cost Hourly Clean Energy Matching:
A Multi-Regional Optimization Analysis

Optimal procurement portfolios for 75–100% hourly matching across five U.S. ISO regions using co-optimized resource mixes, battery storage, and blended pricing

Hourly CFE Optimizer Project • February 2026 • v2.0

← Back to Dashboard

Abstract

We present a co-optimization framework for determining the least-cost clean energy procurement portfolio to achieve specified levels of hourly matching — the fraction of electricity demand met by contemporaneous clean generation in each of 8,760 annual hours. Analyzing five major U.S. Independent System Operator (ISO) regions (CAISO, ERCOT, PJM, NYISO, NEISO) using 2025 EIA-930 hourly data, we find that achieving 90% hourly matching costs $47–70/MWh (effective), while 100% matching requires $83–120/MWh with 30–33% curtailment. The optimal resource mix shifts from solar- and wind-heavy at lower targets to clean-firm-dominant (nuclear, geothermal) above 90%, revealing a fundamental tradeoff between intermittent renewable cost advantages and the temporal reliability premium of dispatchable generation. These findings have direct implications for corporate 24/7 CFE procurement, ISO market design, and the economic case for preserving existing nuclear capacity.

1. Introduction

The electricity sector's transition to clean energy has historically relied on annual accounting: purchasers match total annual consumption with an equivalent volume of renewable energy certificates (RECs), regardless of whether clean generation coincides with actual load [1]. While this approach has successfully driven renewable deployment, it does not ensure that electricity is actually clean in any given hour. A data center running at 2 AM may claim 100% renewable coverage while consuming fossil-generated power, offset by midday solar certificates from a different time and often a different location.

The concept of 24/7 carbon-free energy (CFE) addresses this gap by requiring temporal matching between clean supply and demand [2]. Pioneered by Google's commitment to operate on carbon-free energy "every hour of every day" by 2030 [3], and formalized by the United Nations 24/7 Carbon-free Energy Compact [4], hourly matching represents a more rigorous standard for clean energy procurement. The Biden administration, Microsoft, and over 130 organizations have signed the compact, signaling broad momentum toward hourly accounting.

However, achieving high levels of hourly matching introduces significant economic complexity. Intermittent resources (solar, wind) produce energy in patterns that do not perfectly align with demand, creating surplus hours and deficit hours. Meeting demand in every hour requires either massive overproduction (with attendant curtailment costs) or dispatchable clean generation that can operate regardless of weather conditions. Understanding this cost-matching tradeoff is essential for organizations designing procurement strategies and for policymakers evaluating the feasibility of hourly clean energy standards.

This paper presents an optimization framework that co-optimizes cost and hourly matching across five U.S. ISO regions. By simultaneously searching across procurement levels, resource mixes (clean firm, solar, wind, hydro), and battery storage dispatch, we identify the lowest-cost portfolio for each matching target from 75% to 100%. Our results quantify the "last mile" cost escalation, reveal the critical role of clean firm power, and provide actionable data for procurement decision-making.

2. Literature Review

2.1 From Annual to Hourly Clean Energy Accounting

The inadequacy of annual renewable energy certificates (RECs) for verifying actual grid decarbonization has been widely documented. Xu et al. (2024) demonstrate that annual matching can overstate clean energy coverage by 15–40% in regions with high solar penetration, because midday surplus certificates mask nighttime fossil consumption [1]. The transition toward hourly or "granular" energy certificates is now supported by regulatory frameworks in the EU (via the delegated act on additionality and temporal correlation) and proposed SEC climate disclosure rules.

Google's 24/7 CFE program, the largest corporate hourly matching initiative, reported achieving 64% average hourly CFE across its global operations in 2022, with significant regional variation [3]. Their published methodology uses hourly generation data matched against facility-level consumption and has become a de facto industry standard. The UN 24/7 CFE Compact, launched at COP26, provides a multilateral framework for scaling hourly matching globally [4].

2.2 Clean Energy Cost Benchmarks

The cost landscape for clean energy technologies has evolved rapidly. The NREL Annual Technology Baseline (ATB) 2024 reports utility-scale solar PV LCOE of $24–42/MWh and onshore wind at $26–50/MWh (depending on resource class), with 4-hour lithium-ion battery storage at $115–165/MWh (LCOS) [5]. Lazard's Levelized Cost of Energy Analysis (v17.0) provides broadly consistent estimates, reporting unsubsidized solar LCOE of $24–96/MWh and wind at $24–75/MWh [6].

Regional cost variation is substantial. LBNL's Utility-Scale Solar report documents PPA prices ranging from $30–40/MWh in the Southwest to $60–90/MWh in the Northeast [7]. LBNL's wind market report shows similar geographic spread, with Great Plains wind PPAs at $20–35/MWh versus $55–80/MWh in New England [8]. These regional differentials are central to our optimization, as the cost-optimal resource mix varies significantly by ISO region.

2.3 The Role of Firm Clean Power

The Princeton Net-Zero America study (Larson et al., 2021) identifies firm, dispatchable clean generation as essential for achieving deep decarbonization cost-effectively [9]. Jenkins et al. (2018) demonstrate that removing firm low-carbon resources from the generation portfolio increases electricity system costs by 10–62% under high decarbonization scenarios [10].

Nuclear power, the largest existing source of firm clean generation, faces economic pressure from cheap natural gas and subsidized renewables. The EIA reports existing nuclear fleet operating costs of $28–35/MWh, making it cost-competitive with wholesale market prices in most regions [11]. New nuclear construction, however, faces significantly higher costs: the NREL ATB 2024 estimates new advanced nuclear at $60–100/MWh [5]. Our cost model uses $90/MWh for new-build clean firm across all regions, consistent with these estimates for advanced nuclear or enhanced geothermal systems.

2.4 Battery Storage and Grid Integration

Battery storage enables temporal shifting of clean energy from surplus to deficit hours. The NREL ATB 2024 reports 4-hour lithium-ion round-trip efficiency of 85% [5], with installed costs declining from $380/kWh in 2020 to approximately $250/kWh in 2024 [12]. The EIA reports 16 GW of utility-scale battery storage installed in the U.S. as of 2024, with the majority in California (CAISO) and Texas (ERCOT) [13].

However, 4-hour storage has limited ability to address multi-day deficits or seasonal mismatches. Dowling et al. (2020) show that storage alone cannot cost-effectively replace firm generation at high decarbonization levels, as the required storage duration scales nonlinearly with the matching target [14].

2.5 Curtailment Economics

Grid curtailment of renewable energy is a growing challenge at high penetration levels. CAISO data shows over 2.4 TWh of renewable curtailment in 2023, predominantly solar during midday hours [15]. For hourly matching purchasers, curtailment represents a direct economic penalty: energy is procured and paid for but generates no matching credit because it exceeds instantaneous demand. Our analysis quantifies this curtailment cost across matching targets and regions.

4. Methodology

4.1 Optimization Framework

The optimizer simultaneously searches across three dimensions: procurement level (70–300% of annual demand), resource mix (clean firm, solar, wind, hydro shares summing to 100%), and battery storage dispatch (0–50% of annual demand). Two complementary strategies ensure both cost-efficiency and matching completeness.

Figure 2. Dual optimization framework. Strategy A (sweep) produces the matching frontier; Strategy B (threshold) produces cost-optimal portfolios. Both use three-phase refinement from coarse numpy-accelerated screening to fine-grained precision optimization.

4.2 Hourly Matching Calculation

Hourly Match Score For each hour h (h = 1 to 8,760):
  Matchedh = min(Demandh, TotalSupplyh)
  Surplush = max(0, TotalSupplyh − Demandh)
  Gaph = max(0, Demandh − TotalSupplyh)

Hourly Match Score = SUM(Matchedh) / SUM(Demandh) × 100%

4.3 Cost Model

Our cost model distinguishes between existing and new-build resources. Existing clean generation (up to the current grid mix share) is priced at regional wholesale electricity market prices [17], reflecting contract or market-rate procurement of already-operating capacity. Any procurement above the existing share triggers new-build LCOE pricing [5][6].

4.5 Storage Model

Battery storage is modeled as 4-hour lithium-ion with 85% round-trip efficiency [5], operating on daily charge/discharge cycles. The storage dispatch level (as a percentage of annual demand) is itself optimized, with the model testing levels from 0% to 50%. Storage charges exclusively from clean surplus and discharges only into demand gaps, using a greedy dispatch algorithm that prioritizes the largest imbalances first.

5. Results

5.1 Cost Escalation by Matching Target

The effective cost of hourly matching increases nonlinearly across all five ISO regions. The cost escalation is modest from 75% to 85% matching, accelerates from 85% to 95%, and becomes steep from 99% to 100% — the "last mile" premium.

5.3 Resource Mix Evolution

The optimal resource mix undergoes a structural transformation as the matching target increases. At 75% matching, the cheapest portfolios are dominated by intermittent renewables (solar + wind account for 50–80% of procurement). By 99–100%, clean firm power (nuclear, geothermal) constitutes 40–78% of the portfolio across all regions.

Figure 4. Average resource mix evolution across matching targets (5-region mean). Clean firm share doubles from 27% at 75% matching to 60% at 100%, while wind drops from 34% to 9%. This structural shift drives the cost escalation — clean firm at $90/MWh replaces wind at $40–73/MWh.

5.4 Curtailment Analysis

Curtailment — clean energy generated but not matched to demand — increases dramatically at high matching targets. At 100% matching, all five regions require 30–33% curtailment, meaning roughly one-third of all procured clean energy goes unused.

Figure 5. Curtailment percentage by matching target. NYISO shows the lowest curtailment below 95% due to its hydro/clean-firm-heavy mix. All regions converge at 30–33% curtailment for 100% matching — roughly one-third of procured energy is wasted.

5.5 Procurement Requirements

Achieving 100% hourly matching requires 143–149% procurement of annual demand, meaning organizations must procure 43–49% more clean energy than they consume. The procurement multiplier at 90% matching is much more modest: 90–105% of demand.

6. Discussion

6.1 The Clean Firm Imperative

Our results strongly support the finding of Jenkins et al. (2018) that firm clean generation is essential for deep decarbonization [10]. The resource mix shift toward clean firm above 90% matching is not a modeling artifact but a physical consequence of intermittent generation's inability to reliably cover nighttime, low-wind, and seasonal deficit hours. This has profound implications for energy policy: preserving existing nuclear capacity and investing in next-generation firm clean resources (advanced nuclear, enhanced geothermal, long-duration storage) is economically critical.

6.2 Regional Differentiation

Cost and mix outcomes vary substantially by region, driven by three factors: (1) existing clean grid mix, (2) renewable resource quality, and (3) wholesale market prices. ERCOT benefits from excellent wind resources ($40/MWh new-build) but lacks hydro, resulting in a wind-dominant strategy that becomes unsustainable above 95%. NYISO's combination of significant hydro (25% cap) and nuclear (18.4% grid share) enables the lowest curtailment across matching targets.

6.3 The "Last Mile" Premium

Moving from 99% to 100% matching costs an additional $18–32/MWh across regions, roughly equal to the entire cost increase from 75% to 90%. This "last mile" premium reflects the fundamental challenge: the final 1% of hours with unmet demand are the most expensive to cover because they represent rare combinations of low wind, no solar, and peak demand. Organizations targeting 100% hourly matching should carefully evaluate whether the last-mile premium justifies the incremental environmental benefit, or whether 95–99% matching represents a more cost-effective target [2].

6.4 Implications for Procurement Strategy

For corporate buyers pursuing 24/7 CFE commitments [3][4], our results suggest a staged approach: (1) achieve 80–85% matching through cost-competitive solar and wind PPAs, (2) add clean firm contracts (nuclear PPAs, geothermal) to reach 90–95%, and (3) deploy storage and additional clean firm for the final push to 99%+. Attempting to reach high matching targets using only intermittent resources leads to excessive curtailment and cost overruns.

7. Limitations & Future Work

  • No transmission modeling: Results assume clean energy can be delivered within the ISO region without congestion costs. Real-world transmission constraints would increase effective costs [18].
  • Perfect nuclear availability: Planned outages and forced outages are not modeled, potentially overstating clean firm's hourly contribution.
  • Single weather year: Results use 2025 EIA-930 data only. Multi-year analysis would capture interannual variability in solar irradiance, wind patterns, and hydro availability.
  • 4-hour storage only: Longer-duration storage (8h+, pumped hydro, compressed air, hydrogen) could improve matching at lower cost for high targets [14].
  • Static pricing: LCOE and wholesale prices are fixed. Dynamic pricing, merit-order effects, and solar price cannibalization are not captured.
  • No demand-side flexibility: Load shifting, demand response, and flexible consumption patterns could reduce matching costs significantly.
  • Five regions only: MISO, SPP, and non-ISO regions are not covered. Future work should extend to all U.S. balancing authorities.
  • Grid-level proxies: EIA-930 regional generation profiles may not represent specific project-level assets. Actual solar/wind farm output can differ from regional averages.

8. Conclusion

This analysis provides the first comprehensive, multi-regional cost optimization of hourly clean energy matching across five major U.S. ISO regions. Our key findings are:

  1. 90% hourly matching is achievable at moderate cost ($47–70/MWh effective), representing a $17–28/MWh premium above wholesale grid electricity.
  2. The cost curve inflects sharply above 95%, with the 99–100% increment costing $18–32/MWh alone — equivalent to the entire 75–90% range.
  3. Clean firm power (nuclear, geothermal) is essential for cost-effective deep matching, growing from ~27% of the portfolio at 75% matching to ~60% at 100%.
  4. Curtailment reaches 30–33% at 100% matching across all regions, roughly doubling the effective cost per useful MWh.
  5. Regional resource endowments create 2× cost differences (ERCOT at $95/MWh vs. NEISO at $120/MWh for 100% matching), underscoring the importance of location-specific procurement strategies.

These results argue for a strategic, staged approach to 24/7 clean energy procurement and highlight the critical importance of preserving and expanding firm clean generation capacity. The transition from annual to hourly matching is technically and economically feasible — but only if procurement strategies evolve beyond intermittent renewables to include the dispatchable clean resources that the hardest-to-match hours demand.
