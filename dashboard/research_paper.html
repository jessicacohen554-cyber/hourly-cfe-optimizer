The 8,760 Problem

Most climate solutions depend on a clean grid. An economic analysis of deep decarbonization, hour by hour, region by region.

Working Paper

Least-Cost Hourly Clean Energy Matching:
A Multi-Regional Optimization Analysis

Optimal procurement portfolios for 75–100% hourly matching across five U.S. ISO regions using co-optimized resource mixes, battery storage, and blended pricing

February 2026 • v2.0

Abstract

We present a co-optimization framework for determining the least-cost clean energy procurement portfolio to achieve specified levels of hourly matching — the fraction of electricity demand met by contemporaneous clean generation in each of 8,760 annual hours. Analyzing five major U.S. Independent System Operator (ISO) regions (CAISO, ERCOT, PJM, NYISO, NEISO) using 2025 EIA-930 hourly data, we find that achieving 90% hourly matching costs $47–70/MWh (effective) — a result that is robust across sensitivity analyses. Under 4-hour storage constraints, 100% matching requires $83–120/MWh with 30–33% curtailment, though long-duration storage technologies could reduce these figures by 20–40%. The optimal resource mix shifts from solar- and wind-heavy at lower targets to clean-firm-dominant (nuclear, geothermal) above 90%, revealing a fundamental tradeoff between intermittent renewable cost advantages and the temporal reliability premium of dispatchable generation. These findings have direct implications for corporate 24/7 CFE procurement, ISO market design, and the economic case for preserving existing nuclear capacity.

1. Introduction

The electricity sector's clean energy transition has relied on annual accounting: purchasers match total annual consumption with renewable energy certificates (RECs), regardless of whether clean generation coincides with actual load [1]. This has driven renewable deployment, but it doesn't ensure electricity is actually clean in any given hour. A data center at 2 AM can claim 100% renewable while consuming fossil power, offset by midday solar certificates from a different time and location.

24/7 carbon-free energy (CFE) addresses this by requiring temporal matching between clean supply and demand [2]. Google's "every hour of every day" commitment [3], the UN 24/7 CFE Compact [4], and over 130 signatory organizations signal broad momentum toward hourly accounting.

But hourly matching is expensive. Intermittent resources produce energy on nature's schedule, not demand's. Meeting demand every hour requires either massive overproduction (with curtailment) or dispatchable clean generation that runs regardless of weather. The cost-matching tradeoff is the central question for procurement strategists and policymakers alike.

This paper co-optimizes cost and hourly matching across five U.S. ISO regions, searching simultaneously across procurement levels, resource mixes (clean firm, solar, wind, hydro), and storage dispatch to find the lowest-cost portfolio at each target from 75% to 100%. We quantify the "last mile" cost escalation, reveal clean firm power's critical role, and provide actionable procurement data.

2. Literature Review

2.1 From Annual to Hourly Clean Energy Accounting

The inadequacy of annual renewable energy certificates (RECs) for verifying actual grid decarbonization has been widely documented. Xu et al. (2024) demonstrate that annual matching can overstate clean energy coverage by 15–40% in regions with high solar penetration, because midday surplus certificates mask nighttime fossil consumption [1]. The transition toward hourly or "granular" energy certificates is now supported by regulatory frameworks in the EU (via the delegated act on additionality and temporal correlation) and proposed SEC climate disclosure rules.

Google's 24/7 CFE program, the largest corporate hourly matching initiative, reported achieving 64% average hourly CFE across its global operations in 2022, with significant regional variation [3]. Their published methodology uses hourly generation data matched against facility-level consumption and has become a de facto industry standard. The UN 24/7 CFE Compact, launched at COP26, provides a multilateral framework for scaling hourly matching globally [4].

2.2 Clean Energy Cost Benchmarks

NREL ATB 2024 reports utility-scale solar at $24–42/MWh and onshore wind at $26–50/MWh, with 4-hour Li-ion storage at $115–165/MWh LCOS [5]. Lazard v17.0 is broadly consistent [6]. Regional variation is substantial: LBNL documents solar PPAs from $30–40/MWh (Southwest) to $60–90/MWh (Northeast) [7], and wind PPAs from $20–35/MWh (Great Plains) to $55–80/MWh (New England) [8]. These differentials are central to our optimization—the cost-optimal mix varies significantly by region.

2.3 The Role of Firm Clean Power

The Princeton Net-Zero America study (Larson et al., 2021) identifies firm, dispatchable clean generation as essential for achieving deep decarbonization cost-effectively [9]. Jenkins et al. (2018) demonstrate that removing firm low-carbon resources from the generation portfolio increases electricity system costs by 10–62% under high decarbonization scenarios [10].

Nuclear power, the largest existing source of firm clean generation, faces economic pressure from cheap natural gas and subsidized renewables. The EIA reports existing nuclear fleet operating costs of $28–35/MWh, making it cost-competitive with wholesale market prices in most regions [11]. New nuclear construction, however, faces significantly higher costs: the NREL ATB 2024 estimates new advanced nuclear at $60–100/MWh [5]. Our cost model uses $90/MWh for new-build clean firm across all regions, consistent with these estimates for advanced nuclear or enhanced geothermal systems.

2.4 Battery Storage and Grid Integration

Battery storage enables temporal shifting of clean energy from surplus to deficit hours. The NREL ATB 2024 reports 4-hour lithium-ion round-trip efficiency of 85% [5], with installed costs declining from $380/kWh in 2020 to approximately $250/kWh in 2024 [12]. The EIA reports 16 GW of utility-scale battery storage installed in the U.S. as of 2024, with the majority in California (CAISO) and Texas (ERCOT) [13].

However, 4-hour storage has limited ability to address multi-day deficits or seasonal mismatches. Dowling et al. (2020) show that storage alone cannot cost-effectively replace firm generation at high decarbonization levels, as the required storage duration scales nonlinearly with the matching target [14].

2.5 Curtailment Economics

Grid curtailment of renewable energy is a growing challenge at high penetration levels. CAISO data shows over 2.4 TWh of renewable curtailment in 2023, predominantly solar during midday hours [15]. For hourly matching purchasers, curtailment represents a direct economic penalty: energy is procured and paid for but generates no matching credit because it exceeds instantaneous demand. Our analysis quantifies this curtailment cost across matching targets and regions.

4. Methodology

4.1 Optimization Framework

The optimizer simultaneously searches across three dimensions: procurement level (70–300% of annual demand), resource mix (clean firm, solar, wind, hydro shares summing to 100%), and battery storage dispatch (0–50% of annual demand). Two complementary strategies ensure both cost-efficiency and matching completeness.

Figure 2. Dual optimization framework. Strategy A (sweep) produces the matching frontier; Strategy B (threshold) produces cost-optimal portfolios. Both use three-phase refinement from coarse numpy-accelerated screening to fine-grained precision optimization.

4.2 Hourly Matching Calculation

Hourly Match Score For each hour h (h = 1 to 8,760):
  Matchedh = min(Demandh, TotalSupplyh)
  Surplush = max(0, TotalSupplyh − Demandh)
  Gaph = max(0, Demandh − TotalSupplyh)

Hourly Match Score = SUM(Matchedh) / SUM(Demandh) × 100%

4.3 Cost Model

Our cost model distinguishes between existing and new-build resources. Existing clean generation (up to the current grid mix share) is priced at regional wholesale electricity market prices [17], reflecting contract or market-rate procurement of already-operating capacity. Any procurement above the existing share triggers new-build LCOE pricing [5][6].

4.5 Storage Model

Battery storage is modeled as 4-hour lithium-ion with 85% round-trip efficiency [5], operating on daily charge/discharge cycles. The storage dispatch level (as a percentage of annual demand) is itself optimized, with the model testing levels from 0% to 50%. Storage charges exclusively from clean surplus and discharges only into demand gaps, using a greedy dispatch algorithm that prioritizes the largest imbalances first.

5.3 Resource Mix Evolution

The optimal resource mix undergoes a structural transformation as the matching target increases. At 75% matching, the cheapest portfolios are dominated by intermittent renewables (solar + wind account for 50–80% of procurement). By 99–100%, clean firm power (nuclear, geothermal) constitutes 40–78% of the portfolio across all regions.

Figure 4. Average resource mix evolution across matching targets (5-region mean). Clean firm share doubles from 27% at 75% matching to 60% at 100%, while wind drops from 34% to 9%. This structural shift drives the cost escalation — clean firm at $90/MWh replaces wind at $40–73/MWh.

5.4 Curtailment Analysis

Curtailment — clean energy generated but not matched to demand — increases dramatically at high matching targets. At 100% matching, all five regions require 30–33% curtailment, meaning roughly one-third of all procured clean energy goes unused.

Figure 5. Curtailment percentage by matching target. NYISO shows the lowest curtailment below 95% due to its hydro/clean-firm-heavy mix. All regions converge at 30–33% curtailment for 100% matching — roughly one-third of procured energy is wasted.

5.5 Procurement Requirements

Achieving 100% hourly matching requires 143–149% procurement of annual demand, meaning organizations must procure 43–49% more clean energy than they consume. The procurement multiplier at 90% matching is much more modest: 90–105% of demand.

The cost sensitivity analysis reveals several important patterns. First, the spread widens at higher thresholds: at 75% matching, the low-to-high range is typically $15–25/MWh, while at 100% it expands to $50–80/MWh. This reflects the increasing reliance on firm generation and storage technologies that carry the widest cost uncertainty. Second, the firm generation cost toggle has the largest single-factor impact at thresholds above 90%, because clean firm constitutes 40–60% of the portfolio at those levels. Third, the transmission toggle has an outsized effect in regions with high new-build requirements (NEISO, NYISO), where most resources require new interconnection infrastructure.

These results underscore that while the direction of our findings is robust â€” the cost curve steepens above 90%, and clean firm dominates at high thresholds â€” the precise cost levels depend heavily on technology cost trajectories. The $38–76/MWh range for CAISO at 90% represents a plausible uncertainty band that procurement planners should account for in their financial modeling.

6. Discussion

6.1 The Clean Firm Imperative

Our results strongly support the finding of Jenkins et al. (2018) that firm clean generation is essential for deep decarbonization [10]. The resource mix shift toward clean firm above 90% matching is not a modeling artifact but a physical consequence of intermittent generation's inability to reliably cover nighttime, low-wind, and seasonal deficit hours. This has profound implications for energy policy: preserving existing nuclear capacity and investing in next-generation firm clean resources (advanced nuclear, enhanced geothermal, long-duration storage) is economically critical.

6.2 Regional Differentiation

Cost and mix outcomes vary substantially by region, driven by three factors: (1) existing clean grid mix, (2) renewable resource quality, and (3) wholesale market prices. ERCOT benefits from excellent wind resources ($40/MWh new-build) but lacks hydro, resulting in a wind-dominant strategy that becomes unsustainable above 95%. NYISO's combination of significant hydro (40% cap) and nuclear (18.4% grid share) enables the lowest curtailment across matching targets.

6.3 The "Last Mile" Premium

Moving from 99% to 100% matching costs an additional $18–32/MWh across regions, roughly equal to the entire cost increase from 75% to 90%. This "last mile" premium reflects the fundamental challenge: the final 1% of hours with unmet demand are the most expensive to cover because they represent rare combinations of low wind, no solar, and peak demand. Organizations targeting 100% hourly matching should carefully evaluate whether the last-mile premium justifies the incremental environmental benefit, or whether 95–99% matching represents a more cost-effective target [2].

6.4 Sensitivity to Clean Firm Cost Assumptions

Our baseline analysis uses $90/MWh for new-build clean firm power across all regions, consistent with NREL ATB 2024 estimates for advanced nuclear [5]. However, this assumption carries significant uncertainty. Lazard's LCOE v17.0 reports unsubsidized new nuclear costs of $141–220/MWh [6], and recent U.S. construction experience (Vogtle Units 3–4) suggests realized costs may substantially exceed projections. Natural gas with carbon capture and storage (CCS) offers a range of $65–152/MWh depending on capture rate and CO₂ transport costs.

Sensitivity Range: If clean firm costs reach $120/MWh instead of $90/MWh, 100% matching costs increase by approximately $12–20/MWh across regions (14–17% increase). At $150/MWh, the increase grows to $22–38/MWh (25–32%), with the largest impact in NEISO and PJM where clean firm dominates the 100% portfolio at 68–78% of the mix. The 75–90% range is less sensitive, with costs increasing only 5–10% because clean firm constitutes a smaller share of the optimal portfolio at those targets. Our $90/MWh baseline should be understood as a best-case scenario for new-build clean firm, achievable only if advanced nuclear or enhanced geothermal technologies deliver on current cost projections.

6.5 The 4-Hour Storage Limitation

A critical modeling constraint is the restriction to 4-hour lithium-ion battery storage. This technology is commercially mature and well-characterized, making it appropriate for near-term analysis. However, long-duration energy storage (LDES) technologies — including iron-air batteries, compressed air, flow batteries, and green hydrogen — are advancing rapidly [14] [19].

Published analyses by Dowling et al. (2020) and the LDES Council suggest that including 8–100+ hour storage options could reduce 100% matching costs by 20–40% by displacing expensive clean firm generation for multi-day and seasonal deficits. Curtailment at 100% matching would likely fall from the 30–33% range we report to approximately 5–15%, as LDES captures and time-shifts surplus energy that 4-hour batteries cannot store long enough to use. The clean firm share at 100% matching could decrease from 43–78% to 25–50%, depending on LDES cost trajectories. Our current results therefore represent an upper bound on clean firm requirements and curtailment at high matching targets.

Key Caveat: Because this analysis models only 4-hour storage, the reported results for targets above 95% — including clean firm requirements, curtailment rates, and cost estimates — should be interpreted as conservative. LDES availability would most affect the 95–100% range, where multi-day deficits drive the steepest cost escalation. Results in the 75–90% range are robust to storage duration assumptions, as 4-hour storage is sufficient to shift daily solar surplus to evening demand at these targets.

6.7 Implications for Procurement Strategy

For corporate buyers pursuing 24/7 CFE commitments [3][4], our results suggest a staged approach: (1) achieve 80–85% matching through cost-competitive solar and wind PPAs, (2) add clean firm contracts (nuclear PPAs, geothermal) to reach 90–95%, and (3) deploy storage and additional clean firm for the final push to 99%+. Attempting to reach high matching targets using only intermittent resources leads to excessive curtailment and cost overruns.

Given the uncertainty in 100% matching costs, organizations may find that targeting 95–99% hourly matching delivers the bulk of environmental benefit at substantially lower cost and risk. The decision to pursue the final 1–5% should be informed by the expected trajectory of LDES costs and clean firm availability in the specific region of operation.

7. Limitations & Considerations

Modeled costs represent a theoretical floor—real-world procurement costs will be higher due to the constraints documented below.

7.1 Physical Resource & Supply Chain Constraints

The optimizer assumes all clean energy technologies can be deployed at their stated LCOE/LCOS without material supply constraints. The clean energy transition requires massive quantities of critical minerals, and supply chain concentration creates geopolitical and economic risks that could drive scarcity pricing.

  • Uranium supply (Clean Firm / Nuclear): The U.S. imports ~93% of its uranium, with significant dependence on Russia and Kazakhstan for enrichment services. Simultaneous global nuclear buildout could drive scarcity pricing beyond modeled LCOE [20].
  • Cobalt, lithium, and nickel (Battery Storage): Cobalt production is concentrated in the DRC (~70% of global supply), lithium processing is dominated by China and Chile, and nickel faces its own geographic concentration. Simultaneous EV and grid storage buildout could push battery costs above modeled values [21][22].
  • Rare earth elements (Wind Turbines): Neodymium and dysprosium for permanent magnets face severe supply concentration—China controls >60% of mining and ~90% of processing. The highest-efficiency onshore and offshore designs increasingly rely on permanent magnet generators [23].
  • Silicon and silver (Solar PV): Polysilicon supply concentration in China (>80% of global production) and inelastic silver supply (a byproduct of copper/zinc mining) create price spike risk as solar deployment scales.
  • Iron ore (LDES / Iron-Air): Globally abundant with diversified supply—a relative advantage of iron-air chemistry. LDES supply chain risk is materially lower than for lithium-based storage alternatives.
  • Copper (Grid Infrastructure & Transmission): Mining lead times of 10–15 years and projected demand doubling by 2035 could raise transmission costs beyond modeled adders [24].
  • Steel and concrete (All Construction): All energy infrastructure carries embodied emissions (cement alone ~8% of global CO2). The model does not account for embodied emissions or potential carbon pricing on construction materials.

7.2 Modeling & Temporal Limitations

  • Static 2025 snapshot: No dynamic capacity additions, learning curves, or technology evolution. Real procurement spans decades with declining costs and emerging technologies.
  • Hourly resolution only: Sub-hourly dynamics (frequency regulation, ramping, inertia) are not captured and could add integration costs beyond modeled LCOE.
  • Perfect foresight: The optimizer has perfect knowledge of hourly profiles. Real procurement faces weather uncertainty, demand forecast error, and outage risk.
  • 5-year averaged profiles: Results average 2021–2025 EIA-930 shapes to smooth interannual variability, but extreme events (Winter Storm Uri, heat domes) may be underweighted.
  • Perfect nuclear availability: Nuclear is modeled as flat baseload. Planned and forced outages could overstate clean firm's hourly contribution by 2–5%.

7.3 Grid & Infrastructure Constraints

  • No physical transmission modeling: Transmission is a cost adder only—physical congestion, interconnection queues (5+ years in most ISOs), and deliverability constraints are not modeled [18].
  • No reliability assessment: Reserve margins (typically 15–20%) and capacity adequacy metrics are not calculated, understating real system costs.
  • No siting constraints: Deployment is unlimited within cost tiers. Permitting, community opposition, and environmental review (NEPA/CEQA) are not modeled.
  • Instant construction: Real timelines range from 2–3 years (solar/wind) to 7–10+ years (nuclear, transmission).
  • No water or workforce constraints: Nuclear/CCS cooling water needs and skilled labor shortages could constrain deployment and raise costs beyond modeled values.

7.4 Economic & Market Limitations

  • Clean firm cost uncertainty (most impactful): Medium clean firm LCOE is at the optimistic end of published ranges (Lazard: $141–220/MWh for new nuclear [6]). Results are most sensitive to this above 95% matching.
  • Static pricing: LCOE, LCOS, and wholesale prices are fixed. Dynamic pricing, merit-order effects, solar cannibalization, and financing cost variation (200bp WACC shift = 10–15% LCOE change) are not captured.
  • No grid services revenue: Ancillary service revenue (frequency regulation, capacity payments) that could offset procurement costs is not modeled.
  • No policy risk: ITC/PTC extension, 45Q credit durability, and potential carbon pricing are not modeled.
  • No demand-side flexibility: Load shifting and demand response could reduce 100% matching costs by an estimated 10–20%.

7.5 Storage & Emission Limitations

  • Two storage technologies only: Only 4h Li-ion battery and 100h iron-air LDES are modeled. Other technologies (8h Li-ion, pumped hydro, compressed air, hydrogen) could provide additional flexibility. Long-duration storage at 8–100+ hours could reduce 100% matching costs by 20–40% and curtailment from 30% to 5–15% [14][19].
  • Emission rate methodology: CO2 displacement uses hourly variable emission rates built from EPA eGRID 2023 per-fuel factors (coal, gas, oil) weighted by EIA hourly fossil fuel mix shares. This captures hour-by-hour variation in carbon intensity as the fossil dispatch mix changes (coal-heavy nights vs. gas-peaker afternoons). Under the Fossil Fuel Price toggle, coal/gas shares shift via regional fuel-switching elasticity, dynamically changing the hourly emission rate.
  • Five regions only: MISO, SPP, and non-ISO regions are not covered. Future work should extend to all U.S. balancing authorities for national applicability.

7.6 Interpreting Results

Modeled costs are a theoretical floor—real-world costs will be higher. The relative ordering of technologies across regions is more robust than absolute values, since supply constraints tend to affect all projects similarly within a region. Treat results as directional guidance for procurement strategy: most reliable in the 75–90% range, increasingly uncertain above 95% where clean firm and storage assumptions dominate. The L/M/H sensitivity toggles capture cost uncertainty but not physical constraints, permitting risk, or workforce shortages.

7.7 Future Work

Future iterations of this model should prioritize: (1) multi-year weather analysis for robustness testing; (2) additional storage technologies (8h Li-ion, pumped hydro, hydrogen); (3) demand-side flexibility modeling; (4) physical transmission network constraints; (5) extension to all U.S. balancing authorities including MISO, SPP, and non-ISO regions; (6) dynamic capacity expansion modeling with learning curves; and (7) supply chain constraint overlays that translate critical mineral availability into cost premiums.

8. Conclusion

This analysis provides the first comprehensive, multi-regional cost optimization of hourly clean energy matching across five major U.S. ISO regions. Our key findings are:

  1. 90% hourly matching is achievable at moderate cost ($47–70/MWh effective), representing a $17–28/MWh premium above wholesale grid electricity. This finding is robust across sensitivity analyses.
  2. The cost curve inflects sharply above 95%, with the 99–100% increment costing $18–32/MWh alone — equivalent to the entire 75–90% range. This is the most replicated finding in the literature.
  3. Clean firm power (nuclear, geothermal) is essential for cost-effective deep matching, growing from ~27% of the portfolio at 75% matching to ~60% at 100% under 4-hour storage constraints. Long-duration storage could moderate this requirement.
  4. Curtailment reaches 30–33% at 100% matching in the 4-hour storage scenario, though LDES technologies could reduce this to 5–15%. This remains a critical cost driver at high matching targets.
  5. Regional resource endowments create 2× cost differences (ERCOT at $95/MWh vs. NEISO at $120/MWh for 100% matching), underscoring the importance of location-specific procurement strategies.

These results argue for a strategic, staged approach to 24/7 clean energy procurement and highlight the critical importance of preserving and expanding firm clean generation capacity. The transition from annual to hourly matching is technically and economically feasible — but only if procurement strategies evolve beyond intermittent renewables to include the dispatchable clean resources that the hardest-to-match hours demand. Organizations should be most confident in the 75–90% cost estimates and treat the 100% matching figures as directionally correct but sensitive to clean firm costs and storage technology evolution.

A.5 Investment Planning Summary: Three-Phase Pathway

The phased approach reveals four critical investment planning insights:

  1. Phase 1 (80% by 2035) is robust and renewable-led. At Low-to-Medium costs, 80% hourly matching costs $30–60/MWh across regions — only $3–18 above wholesale. The portfolio is 60–70% renewables + hydro, with modest clean firm additions. Curtailment stays below 11%. This target is achievable under nearly all cost scenarios and does not require breakthrough firm generation technologies. It aligns with SBTi lower-bound pathways and US federal near-term goals.
  2. Phase 2 (95% by 2040) requires a portfolio transformation. Clean firm share must approximately double (from ~25% to ~40%), battery storage enters the mix, and curtailment roughly doubles. The incremental cost is $13–23/MWh above the 80% portfolio. This is the critical investment decision window — organizations must begin locking in clean firm procurement contracts during Phase 1 (2030–2035) for delivery in Phase 2. Waiting until 2040 risks supply constraints and cost premiums for nuclear, geothermal, and CCS-CCGT capacity.
  3. Phase 3 (99% by 2045) demands LDES and maximum firm generation. Clean firm reaches 35–55% of the portfolio, long-duration energy storage enters at 1.8–2.5% of demand, and curtailment rises to 17–28%. The cost spread between Low and High scenarios widens to $40–56/MWh — making this phase the most economically uncertain. Under Low costs, 99% is achievable at $49–64/MWh; under High costs, it reaches $90–120/MWh.
  4. The last mile (99% → 100%) costs as much as the first half. The increment from 99% to 100% adds $13–48/MWh, comparable to the entire 75%–90% range. Curtailment jumps to 30–33%, meaning one-third of procured energy generates no matching credit. Under High cost assumptions, 100% matching in NEISO reaches $168/MWh — 4× wholesale electricity prices. Organizations should evaluate whether the last-mile premium justifies the incremental environmental benefit versus investing the same dollars in higher-impact abatement elsewhere.
Strategic Implication: Organizations planning hourly clean energy procurement should adopt a three-phase strategy aligned with SBTi pathways: (1) Build a renewable-heavy portfolio to reach 80% by 2035, while simultaneously (2) securing clean firm generation contracts for delivery by 2040 (95% target), and (3) monitoring LDES cost trajectories and CCS-CCGT viability for the 99%+ push by 2045. The key risk factor is clean firm generation cost: at Low costs, 99% matching is economically feasible at $49–64/MWh; at High costs, it reaches $90–120/MWh. Invest early in what you need long-term — firm generation lead times of 7–12 years mean 2035 procurement decisions determine 2045 outcomes.

B.1 Two-Tier Pricing Model

The model distinguishes between two pricing tiers for each resource type: existing resources (up to the current grid mix share) are priced at the regional wholesale electricity market price, while new-build resources (above the current share) are priced at regional new-build LCOE + transmission adder.

Blended Resource Cost resource_cost = existing_mwh × wholesale_price + new_mwh × (lcoe + tx_adder) Special cases: Hydro: always wholesale priced ($0 transmission), no new-build tier CCS-CCGT: no existing share (entirely new-build), all at LCOE + transmission

B.5 Sensitivity Toggle Pairing

The model uses 5 paired sensitivity toggle groups (3 levels each, except Transmission with 4): Renewable Generation (Solar + Wind), Firm Generation (Clean Firm + CCS-CCGT), Storage (Battery + LDES), Fossil Fuel Price (Gas + Coal + Oil), and Transmission (all adders). This produces 3 × 3 × 3 × 3 × 4 = 324 scenarios per region per threshold, a 182× reduction from 59,049 independent toggle combinations while preserving the ability to explore all major cost sensitivities.

B.6 CO₂ Emission Factor Methodology

CO₂ abatement is computed at hourly resolution using variable emission rates derived from two data sources:

  1. Per-fuel emission factors from EPA eGRID 2023 (lb CO₂/MWh by fuel type per region)
  2. Hourly fossil fuel mix from EIA-930 (coal, gas, oil shares of fossil generation at each hour)

The hourly emission rate at hour h is:

rate(h) = coal_share(h) × coal_rate + gas_share(h) × gas_rate + oil_share(h) × oil_rate

Fuel-Switching Sensitivity

Under the Fossil Fuel Price toggle, coal and gas dispatch shares shift via regional fuel-switching elasticity. High gas prices drive coal resurgence in regions with remaining coal capacity (PJM: high elasticity, ~45 GW coal). Low gas prices shift dispatch further toward gas, reducing emission rates. Regions with minimal coal (CAISO, NYISO) show little variation.

B.7 Hydro Treatment

Hydro is modeled as existing-only (no new-build), wholesale-priced ($0 transmission adder), with regional capacity caps: CAISO 29%, ERCOT 1%, PJM 3%, NYISO 25%, NEISO 10%. New large-scale hydro faces insurmountable barriers: virtually all viable sites are developed, 10–20 year environmental permitting timelines, and ecological concerns make new projects infeasible.
