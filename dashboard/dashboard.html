The Bottom Line: Plan Ahead for Clean Firm Power

Your resource mix today determines your decarbonization costs tomorrow

This analysis reveals a fundamental strategic insight: the resource mix that is cheapest at lower matching targets is not the resource mix you need at higher ones. As hourly matching ambitions rise from 75% to 90% to 99%, the optimal portfolio undergoes a dramatic transformation — from wind and solar dominance to an increasingly critical role for clean firm power (nuclear, geothermal, and CCS-equipped gas).

75% Matching

Wind and solar dominate. Cheap renewables cover most hours cost-effectively. The easy path.

90% — The Decision Point

Cost-per-point doubles. The mix pivots toward clean firm. Decisions made here lock in your trajectory.

99%+ — The Cliff

The last 1% costs as much as the entire 75–99% journey combined. Only firm generation survives here.

The overbuilding trap: Overinvesting in wind and solar may appear cost-effective in the short term, but it creates a costly problem at higher matching targets. Excess intermittent capacity leads to massive curtailment — energy you pay for but cannot use. At 99% matching, curtailment can exceed 40% of total procurement. Every dollar spent on capacity that will later be curtailed is a dollar not invested in the clean firm resources you will inevitably need.

Short-duration storage alone won’t close the gap. In this analysis (4-hour Li-ion), battery deployment remains near zero below 95% matching. Four-hour storage can shift solar surplus to evenings but struggles with multi-day wind droughts and long winter nights. Longer-duration storage technologies (iron-air, compressed air, hydrogen) may expand storage’s role significantly — but firm, dispatchable generation will remain essential for the hardest hours regardless.

Existing clean firm is irreplaceable at any cost. PJM (32% nuclear), NEISO (24%), and NYISO (18%) benefit enormously from already-built nuclear fleets that deliver clean baseload at wholesale prices. Losing these assets to economic shutdown — as nearly happened in the 2010s — would add $5–18/MWh to every matching target. These plants should be valued not by today’s wholesale price, but by the cost of replacing them.

Organizations serious about deep decarbonization should secure and procure clean firm power early — whether through nuclear PPAs, geothermal contracts, or investments in CCS-ready gas infrastructure. Waiting to address the firm power gap until you are already targeting 95%+ matching means competing for scarce resources in a seller’s market, at significantly higher costs.

Plan for where you’re going, not just where you are today.

Resource Strategy

How the Optimal Mix Evolves

At lower matching targets, solar and wind dominate because they are the cheapest per MWh. As matching targets rise, the mix shifts toward clean firm power (nuclear, geothermal) — the only resources that generate reliably regardless of weather.

Regional Variation: Wind-rich ERCOT sustains wind-heavy mixes to 95%+, while PJM and NEISO require a pivot to nuclear/clean firm at lower thresholds.

The Trilemma

From Two Dimensions to Three

For decades, energy planning balanced two priorities: reliability and affordability. Keep the lights on, and keep bills manageable. Hourly clean energy matching introduces a third dimension: clean.

This three-sided optimization — the energy trilemma — creates new tensions. Pursuing maximum cleanliness can strain both affordability (through exponential cost curves above 95%) and reliability (by depending on weather-variable resources). The optimal strategy must navigate all three corners simultaneously.

Our optimization results reveal this trilemma in action: at 75% matching, affordability dominates (cheap wind and solar suffice). By 95%, reliability concerns force the mix toward costly clean firm power. At 99%+, all three dimensions are stressed — clean, reliable energy at affordable cost requires the full toolkit: renewables, firm generation, storage, and potentially dispatchable low-carbon sources.

Strategic Takeaway: The energy trilemma means there is no single "right" threshold. Each buyer must determine their own balance between cleanliness, reliability, and cost based on their specific goals, budget, and timeline.
Transition Pathway

The Role of Efficient Gas & Carbon Capture

The exponential cost of the last few percent of hourly matching points to a practical question: what fills the gap when renewables and nuclear can’t? In many regions, efficient natural gas combined-cycle gas turbines (CCGTs) serve as a critical transition fuel.

Modern CCGTs achieve 60%+ thermal efficiency and can ramp quickly to complement variable renewables. While they produce CO₂, they emit roughly half the carbon per MWh of older coal and simple-cycle gas plants. In a portfolio targeting 95%+ hourly matching, CCGTs can efficiently serve the 5% of hours that clean resources cannot economically reach.

The analysis highlights why dispatchable low-carbon power matters: the hardest hours to match are typically winter evenings when solar is absent, wind may be calm, and demand peaks from heating loads. These are precisely the hours where dispatchable generation — whether nuclear, geothermal, or gas with CCS — provides the greatest value.

From Transition Fuel to Clean Firm: The CCS Pathway

Investing in efficient CCGTs today is not just a near-term compromise — it is a strategic foundation for future clean firm power. Modern CCGTs are designed to be “CCS-ready,” meaning carbon capture equipment can be retrofitted onto existing plants. By building efficient gas infrastructure now, organizations create assets that can transition from backup generation to dispatchable low-carbon power as CCS technology matures and carbon pricing strengthens the business case.

This transition path solves the most expensive part of the hourly matching challenge. At a capture rate of 90%+, a CCGT with CCS delivers firm, flexible generation at roughly 35–50 kg CO₂/MWh — comparable to lifecycle estimates for solar and wind when accounting for manufacturing. The key advantage: unlike renewables, CCS-equipped CCGTs dispatch on demand, covering the exact hours that drive the exponential cost increase above 95%.

The strategic logic is clear: build CCGTs today as transition backup, retrofit with CCS tomorrow for clean firm power. This avoids stranded assets — instead of building gas plants that become obsolete, you build gas plants that become clean. The same infrastructure that provides dispatchable backup at 95% matching today can deliver dispatchable clean power at 99%+ matching in the future, directly addressing the steep cost cliff this analysis reveals.

Bridge to Full Decarbonization: Efficient CCGTs serve as a pragmatic bridge technology. In the near term, they provide the dispatchable backup that enables higher renewable penetration. With CCS retrofits, they transition to dispatchable low-carbon generation — addressing the cost cliff above 95% hourly matching without the full premium of 100% clean firm power. Building CCS-ready CCGTs today is an investment in tomorrow’s clean firm capacity.
Critical Asset

Existing Nuclear & Geothermal: The Foundation We Can’t Afford to Lose

Existing nuclear and geothermal plants are the most cost-effective clean firm generation we have. Unlike new-build options, they carry no upfront capital expenditure or steep financing costs. Keeping these resources online is not just beneficial — it is essential to achieving deep decarbonization.

Yet these assets face real economic threats. The wave of cheap natural gas and subsidized renewables that drove several nuclear plant shutdowns in the 2010s has not fully abated. When wholesale power prices fall below operating costs, even well-run plants face pressure to shut down permanently. Once retired, a nuclear plant cannot economically restart — the clean firm capacity is gone forever, and replacing it requires new-build at 2–3x the cost.

The math is straightforward: existing plants provide clean firm power at wholesale market rates ($27–42/MWh) because their capital costs are already paid. Every megawatt of existing clean firm that retires prematurely is a megawatt that must eventually be replaced at far higher cost if deep decarbonization goals are to be met. These assets should be valued not just by their wholesale power price, but by the cost that would be incurred to replace them.

CCS Retrofits: Adding New Clean Firm to the Stack

Where existing nuclear and geothermal provide the foundation, CCS-retrofitted gas plants can build on it. Current estimates put CCS retrofit LCOE at $70–100/MWh before incentives, driven by capture equipment CapEx, a 10–15% parasitic efficiency penalty, and CO₂ transport/storage costs.

At $85/ton, the 45Q credit meaningfully narrows the gap. A CCGT capturing ~0.3 tons CO₂/MWh at 90% capture rate receives ~$25/MWh in credits, pushing net LCOE toward $55–80/MWh — competitive with new nuclear and well below the cost of the last percentage points of hourly matching. To maximize captured tons and 45Q value, CCS plants would likely operate at high capacity factors resembling baseload dispatch, conveniently filling the exact role the grid needs: steady, dispatchable clean firm generation.

Protect What You Have, Build What You Need: Existing nuclear and geothermal provide irreplaceable clean firm generation at a fraction of new-build cost. Losing these assets to economic pressure would increase decarbonization costs by $5–18/MWh depending on region — a penalty that compounds at every matching target. Meanwhile, CCS retrofits on existing gas infrastructure offer the most viable near-term pathway to add new clean firm capacity at costs competitive with new nuclear, especially with 45Q incentives driving baseload-like operation.
Important Context

What This Analysis Does Not Capture

This optimization models energy procurement and resource costs but does not include transmission, distribution, or interconnection costs. In practice, these costs can materially change the economics — and may strengthen or weaken the case for specific resource types depending on geography and grid constraints.

For remote renewables, transmission adds significant cost. The best wind and solar resources are often far from load centers, requiring new high-voltage lines that can add $15–40/MWh or more to delivered cost. Interconnection queue backlogs often exceed 5 years, delaying projects regardless of resource quality. This hidden cost narrows the apparent LCOE advantage of renewables over firm resources located closer to demand.

For existing nuclear plants, the transmission advantage is significant. These facilities are already interconnected, often at high-voltage nodes near population centers. They require no new transmission investment and face no queue delays. This further strengthens the case for preserving existing clean firm assets.

For CCS retrofits on existing gas plants, the same advantage applies — the electrical interconnection exists. However, CCS adds a new infrastructure requirement: CO₂ transport pipelines to sequestration sites, which carry their own permitting, cost, and timeline challenges.

For longer-duration storage, siting flexibility could reduce transmission needs by locating storage near load centers, effectively substituting storage investment for transmission investment. This is an area where future analysis incorporating T&D costs could meaningfully shift the optimal resource mix.

Net Impact: Including transmission and interconnection costs would likely strengthen the case for existing nuclear (already connected), increase the true delivered cost of remote renewables, and add complexity to CCS deployment (CO₂ pipelines). Our cost estimates for wind and solar should be considered optimistic relative to fully delivered costs in many regions.
