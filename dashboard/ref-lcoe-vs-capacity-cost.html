How the Metrics Connect: From Plant Cost to Electricity Price

Every electricity cost metric is built from a few primitive inputs. CapEx flows through CRF and capacity factor to become LCOE, which adds transmission to become delivered cost. LMP is set by the merit order, not by any individual plant’s LCOE.

LCOE $/MWh Levelized Cost of Energy

The lifetime average cost of producing one MWh of electricity from a generator. Spreads all costs — capital, financing, fixed O&M, variable O&M, and fuel — over all the energy the plant produces across its economic life.

LCOE = (CapEx × CRF + FOM) / (CF × 8,760) + VOM + Fuel

What it captures: Capital costs (amortized), operating costs, fuel costs, and implicitly the plant’s utilization via capacity factor. Enables apples-to-apples comparison across technologies that have fundamentally different cost structures (capital-heavy nuclear vs. fuel-heavy gas).

What it doesn’t capture: Transmission costs, integration costs, backup/reliability value, curtailment risk, or the temporal value of generation (a MWh at 3 PM is worth more than at 3 AM). LCOE treats all MWh as equal.

Hidden assumptions: Capacity factor is embedded as a fixed input, but in reality it’s a market outcome (a gas plant’s CF depends on dispatch order). Policy incentives (ITC, PTC, 45Q) may or may not be included — always check. Financing assumptions (WACC, plant life) materially affect the result.

LCOS $/MWh Levelized Cost of Storage

The all-in cost per MWh discharged from a storage system, including capital, O&M, the cost of charging energy lost to round-trip efficiency, and degradation over the system’s life.

LCOS differs from LCOE because storage doesn’t generate — it shifts energy in time. The “fuel cost” is the electricity consumed during charging, inflated by round-trip efficiency losses. A 4hr Li-ion battery at 85% RTE loses 15% of every MWh it stores; a 100hr iron-air system at 50% RTE loses half.

Depends heavily on: Cycle count (how many charge/discharge cycles per year), duration (hours of storage), round-trip efficiency, and the cost of charging energy. A battery cycled once per day has a very different LCOS than one cycled twice per week.

Capacity Cost $/kW Overnight Capital Cost, Installed Cost, CapEx

The cost to build one kilowatt of generating capacity, measured as if the plant could be built “overnight” (no construction interest). The most market-neutral metric — it measures physical construction cost independent of dispatch, fuel, or policy.

“Overnight” vs. “All-in”: Overnight CapEx excludes financing costs during construction. For a solar farm built in 12 months, the difference is small. For a nuclear plant taking 7–10 years, construction interest can add 30–50% to the overnight cost. Always check which version is being quoted.

Why it misleads across technologies: Solar at $1,200/kW looks cheaper than nuclear at $8,000/kW, but solar runs 25% of hours while nuclear runs 93%. Per MWh of output, the gap narrows dramatically. $/kW is useful for comparing the same technology across projects or tracking cost trends, but meaningless for comparing generation cost across technologies without a capacity factor adjustment.

Annualized form ($/kW-yr): CapEx × CRF converts the one-time cost to an annual charge. This is used for resource adequacy costs (gas backup) where you’re paying for availability, not energy.

Marginal Cost $/MWh Short-Run Marginal Cost, SRMC

The cost of producing one additional MWh from a plant that’s already built. Excludes capital costs entirely — only fuel and variable O&M. This is what generators bid into wholesale markets.

For renewables and nuclear, marginal cost is near $0/MWh (no fuel). For gas plants, it’s $25–60/MWh depending on heat rate and gas price. This is why renewables always dispatch first in the merit order — their bid is $0, so they run whenever available. The marginal cost of the most expensive plant running in any hour sets the wholesale price (LMP) for everyone.

Marginal Abatement Cost $/ton CO₂ MAC

The incremental cost of avoiding one ton of CO₂ emissions by substituting a clean resource for a fossil one. Measures the cost-effectiveness of decarbonization strategies.

MAC = (Costclean − Costfossil) / CO₂ Avoided per MWh

MAC depends on both the clean resource cost and the fossil resource it displaces. Replacing coal (high emissions) yields a lower MAC than replacing gas (lower emissions) for the same clean cost. Common benchmarks: Social Cost of Carbon ~$51–185/ton, EU ETS ~$60–100/ton, Direct Air Capture ~$250–600/ton.

LMP $/MWh Locational Marginal Price

The wholesale price of electricity at a specific grid node in a specific hour. Set by the marginal generator — the most expensive plant dispatched to meet demand. Every generator on the grid gets paid this price, regardless of their own costs.

LMP has three components: energy (marginal generation cost), congestion (transmission constraint premium), and losses (electrical losses on the wire). In most hours, the energy component dominates and equals the marginal gas plant’s fuel + VOM cost.

Key distinction from LCOE: LCOE is a plant-level lifetime average. LMP is a system-level hourly clearing price. A plant with $40/MWh LCOE might receive $25/MWh LMP in some hours and $80/MWh in others. Profitability depends on whether average LMP exceeds LCOE over enough hours.

LCOE vs. $/kW: The Capacity Factor Bridge

Each step from left to right adds a market-dependent assumption. $/kW is pure construction cost. LCOE bakes in capacity factor, fuel, financing, and sometimes policy incentives.

Capacity Factor % CF, Utilization Rate

The fraction of a plant’s maximum possible output that it actually produces over a period. A 100 MW plant at 30% CF produces 30 MW on average, or 262,800 MWh per year.

CF = Actual Generation / (Nameplate Capacity × Hours in Period)

This is the single most important variable in translating $/kW to $/MWh. It varies by technology, region, and market conditions:

  • Nuclear: 90–93% — nearly constant across regions (baseload, must-run)
  • Wind: 25–42% — highly regional (ERCOT best, CAISO weakest)
  • Solar: 18–29% — latitude-driven (ERCOT/CAISO best, NYISO weakest)
  • Gas CCGT: 40–85% — market-dependent (dispatch order determines utilization)
  • Gas CT (peaker): 5–15% — only runs during peak hours

The trap: CF is often treated as a fixed property of the technology, but for dispatchable resources (gas, CCS), it’s a market outcome. A gas plant in a market with heavy renewables might achieve 40% CF instead of the 85% assumed in its LCOE calculation — effectively doubling its per-MWh cost.

Heat Rate BTU/kWh Thermal Efficiency

The amount of fuel energy (in BTU) needed to produce one kWh of electricity. Lower is better. Converts fuel price ($/MMBtu) directly to generation fuel cost ($/MWh).

Fuel Cost ($/MWh) = Heat Rate (BTU/kWh) × Fuel Price ($/MMBtu) / 1,000

A modern CCGT at 6,400 BTU/kWh is roughly 53% efficient. An older steam turbine at 10,000 BTU/kWh is ~34% efficient. At $3/MMBtu gas, the CCGT spends $19.2/MWh on fuel while the steam turbine spends $30/MWh — a $10.8/MWh advantage from efficiency alone.

CCS penalty: Carbon capture equipment raises a plant’s effective heat rate by 25–30% because the capture process consumes energy. A 6,400 BTU/kWh CCGT becomes ~8,300 BTU/kWh with CCS.

Round-Trip Efficiency % RTE

The percentage of energy put into a storage system that comes back out. A battery with 85% RTE returns 0.85 MWh for every 1.0 MWh stored. The 15% loss is heat, friction, and conversion inefficiency.

RTE directly affects the effective cost of stored energy. If charging electricity costs $40/MWh and RTE is 85%, the energy cost component of the discharge is $40/0.85 = $47/MWh before any capital or O&M costs.

Typical values: Li-ion battery 85–90%, Pumped hydro 75–82%, Iron-air (LDES) 45–55%, Hydrogen 30–40%.

Capacity Credit % ELCC, Effective Load Carrying Capability

The fraction of a resource’s nameplate capacity that can be counted toward meeting peak demand for resource adequacy purposes. A 100 MW solar farm with 30% capacity credit contributes 30 MW toward the system’s reliability requirement.

Capacity credit reflects how reliably a resource produces during the hours when the grid most needs it (typically summer afternoon peaks or winter morning peaks). Thermal plants get high credits (90–100%) because they can dispatch on demand. Variable renewables get lower credits because their output depends on weather.

Typical values: Nuclear ~100%, Gas CCGT 90%, Battery 90–95% (duration-limited), Solar 30%, Wind 10%, Hydro 50%.

Storage Economics: LCOS Components

Storage costs combine the capital cost of the hardware, the cost of charging electricity (inflated by efficiency losses), and degradation from repeated cycling.

Capital Recovery Factor ratio CRF, Annuity Factor

The fraction of total capital cost that must be recovered each year to repay investment plus interest over the plant’s economic life. Converts a one-time $/kW cost into an annual $/kW-yr stream.

CRF = r(1+r)n / [(1+r)n − 1]

Where r = discount rate (WACC) and n = economic life in years. Typical values: ~8% for a 25-year plant at 7% WACC, ~6.5% for a 30-year plant. A higher CRF (shorter life or higher WACC) means each year must recover more capital, raising LCOE.

WACC % Weighted Average Cost of Capital, Discount Rate

The blended cost of debt and equity financing for a project. Reflects the minimum return investors require to put capital at risk. Feeds directly into CRF and thus into LCOE.

Typical values: 5–8% nominal for utility-scale renewables in developed markets, 8–12% for nuclear or emerging markets. A 1 percentage point increase in WACC raises LCOE by roughly 8–12% for capital-intensive technologies (solar, wind, nuclear) but has minimal effect on fuel-intensive technologies (gas).

Fixed O&M $/kW-yr FOM, Fixed Operating Costs

Annual costs that don’t vary with how much the plant runs. Includes maintenance crews, insurance, land lease, property taxes, administrative costs. You pay these whether the plant generates 0 MWh or 8,760 MWh.

FOM is added to annualized capital (CapEx × CRF) before dividing by energy output to get LCOE. For capital-light technologies, FOM can be a meaningful share of total cost. Typical: Solar $12–18/kW-yr, Wind $25–40/kW-yr, Nuclear $100–130/kW-yr, Gas CCGT $13–17/kW-yr.

Variable O&M $/MWh VOM, Variable Operating Costs

Costs that scale with generation. Consumables, wear-and-tear, water treatment, emissions allowances. Excludes fuel (which is tracked separately). Added directly to the per-MWh LCOE calculation.

Typically small relative to capital and fuel: Solar $0–2/MWh, Wind $0–3/MWh, Gas CCGT $2–4/MWh, Nuclear $2–3/MWh. VOM is part of what generators bid into the market (along with fuel cost) to form the merit order.

Curtailment MWh or %

Clean energy that’s available but intentionally wasted because the grid can’t absorb it. Happens when renewable output exceeds demand minus must-run generation and there’s no storage capacity left.

Curtailment is an integration cost that LCOE doesn’t capture. A solar farm with $40/MWh LCOE that’s curtailed 20% of the time effectively costs $50/MWh for the energy that actually gets used. CAISO curtailed ~2.4 million MWh of solar in 2023 — about 5% of total solar production.

Transmission Adder $/MWh Wheeling Cost, Delivery Cost

The cost of moving electricity from where it’s generated to where it’s consumed. Includes new line construction (amortized), congestion charges, and line losses. Added on top of LCOE to get delivered cost.

Ranges from $0 (on-site generation) to $20+/MWh for remote renewables requiring long-distance transmission. This is a key hidden cost that makes “cheapest LCOE wins” misleading — a wind farm with $30/MWh LCOE plus $15/MWh transmission costs more delivered than a gas plant with $50/MWh LCOE and $0 transmission.

Resource Adequacy MW or % RA, Capacity Requirement, Reserve Margin

The regulatory requirement to have enough generating capacity available to meet peak demand plus a safety margin (typically 15%). Ensures the grid doesn’t black out during extreme demand events.

RA is priced in $/kW-yr, not $/MWh, because you’re paying for the plant to be available, not to generate. A peaker gas plant that runs 200 hours/year at a terrible $/MWh LCOE can still be the cheapest way to provide RA because its $/kW-yr availability cost is low. This is the metric where $/kW capacity cost is the right framing — you’re buying insurance, not energy.
