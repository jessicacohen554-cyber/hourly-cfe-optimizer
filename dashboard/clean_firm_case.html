The Case for Clean Firm Power

Why every ISO needs dispatchable clean generation — and why the gas alternative is more expensive than it looks

Build clean firm today.
It's cheaper in the long run.

High matching targets need clean firm power in most regions. Without it, the gap between demand and variable renewable supply must be filled by gas backup — and every new gas plant built today becomes a stranded asset tomorrow.

Clean firm is expensive today because it's first-of-a-kind. Vogtle Units 3&4 came in at ~$15,000/kW; NuScale's SMR reached ~$20,000/kW before cancellation. Lazard (2025) puts unsubsidized new nuclear at $141–220/MWh. But every energy technology follows Wright's Law: costs decline with deployment. Solar achieved a 24% learning rate per doubling. Wind: 15%. NREL ATB 2024 models nuclear learning at 8–9.5% per doubling; the DOE's Liftoff report projects ~40% total reduction after 5–10 reactors per design, reaching $3,600/kW and ~$66/MWh. MIT's 2024 analysis puts AP1000 NOAK at $4,625/kW ($66/MWh unsubsidized).

Meanwhile, gas gets more expensive. Fuel prices rise (EIA AEO2025: $3.50→$4.95/MMBtu by 2050). Capital costs have tripled — market quotes are $2,400/kW vs. EIA's $1,200/kW estimate (GridLab 2025). As clean energy scales, gas plants run fewer hours, spreading fixed costs over declining output. Add carbon pricing — EU ETS already at €77/ton, heading to €126+ by 2030 — and the true cost of gas rises relentlessly. Carbon Tracker estimates $3.5 trillion in stranded fossil assets globally under IEA net-zero scenarios.

CCS follows a parallel trajectory. DOE OCED projects ~25% CAPEX reduction from FOAK to NOAK, with learning rates of 3–5% per doubling (NETL). The 45Q tax credit ($85/ton for saline storage) reduces CCS-CCGT LCOE by over 25%, bringing near-term costs to ~$92/MWh (Frontiers 2022). As both nuclear and CCS descend their learning curves, the clean firm portfolio converges on $68–75/MWh by the late 2030s.

Diversifying beyond VRE + storage derisks grid decarbonization and prevents wasted investment solidifying the future of fossil fuels via redundant gas backup. The FOAK premium is temporary. The gas trap is permanent.

Nuclear NOAK target: $68–75/MWh (vs. FOAK $135–170) Gas true cost by 2040: $85–112/MWh (and rising)

The FOAK premium is an investment, not a cost

Lazard (2025) puts unsubsidized new nuclear at $141–220/MWh — consistent with Vogtle ($15,000/kW) and NuScale ($20,000/kW) experience. But DOE’s Liftoff report targets $3,600/kW after 5–10 deployments per design; MIT (2024) models AP1000 NOAK at $66/MWh unsubsidized. NREL ATB 2024 applies 8–9.5% learning rates per doubling. Geothermal is moving even faster — Fervo cut drilling costs 50% in 3 years. CCS follows suit: NETL documents 3–5% learning per doubling, with DOE OCED projecting 25% CAPEX reduction to NOAK. The gas cost it displaces never declines.
