PJM Wholesale Market Analysis

Clean energy halves wholesale prices in PJM — but the path is narrower than you think

Across 151,944 simulated PJM scenarios, wholesale prices decline from $35/MWh at 50% clean energy to $17/MWh at 95%. But the spread between best- and worst-case outcomes peaks in the middle of the transition — precisely where most utilities are planning today.

Fuel prices explain nearly everything until 100% clean, where the rules change entirely and renewable costs, transmission, and policy levers take over.

Price Drivers

Fuel prices explain 88–100% of wholesale cost variation — until they don't

From 50% to 95% clean energy, the single biggest determinant of wholesale prices is the fossil fuel price assumption. At 50% clean, fuel level explains 100% of the variance in average LMP. At 80%, it still accounts for 88%.

The gap between Low and High fuel scenarios in PJM is massive: at 80% clean, Low fuel yields $17/MWh while High fuel yields $37/MWh — a 2.2× difference driven entirely by what marginal gas plants cost to run.

Low fuel: $17/MWh at 80% · High fuel: $37/MWh at 80%

At 100% clean, the game changes completely

When clean energy reaches 100%, fuel prices become irrelevant — there are no fossil plants left to set the price. Variance fragments across multiple levers: renewable costs (14%), fuel level (14%), transmission (11%), 45Q policy (7%), and CO₂ price (5%).

This is the structural break in the wholesale market. Everything that mattered before 100% stops mattering. Everything that didn't — transmission buildout, tax policy, clean generation costs — becomes decisive.

At 100%: No single factor explains >15% of price variance

Market Structure

PJM zero-price hours explode from 265 to 7,980 — and the market flips inside out

At 50% clean, PJM sees about 265 zero-or-negative-price hours per year — roughly 3% of the time, typically overnight with must-run nuclear surplus. By 95%, that figure hits 4,268 hours — nearly half the year. At 100%, it's 7,980 hours: 91% of all hours produce free or negatively-priced electricity.

This isn't a gradual shift. The curve accelerates sharply past 75%, as each incremental percentage of clean energy adds more zero-marginal-cost generation to hours where fossil was already barely dispatching.

265 → 7,980 zero-price hours: a 30× increase

Scarcity events decline but never disappear

Hours above $200/MWh drop from a median of 68 at 50% clean to 10 at 100% — but they persist even in a fully clean grid. Clean firm generation and storage smooth the worst supply crunches, but extreme weather and demand spikes still produce scarcity pricing in the tail.

The P90 of scarcity hours (worst 10% of scenarios) remains above 40 even at 100% clean — a reminder that resource adequacy risk doesn't vanish, it transforms.

Price Structure

The peak premium persists — and that's the arbitrage signal

Off-peak prices crash faster than peak: at 95% clean, off-peak hits $14/MWh while peak holds at $22/MWh. The median spread between peak and off-peak actually widens from 80% to 97.5% before collapsing only at 100%.

For storage operators and demand-response providers, this is the signal: the economic case for time-shifting energy strengthens through most of the clean energy transition, even as absolute prices fall.

Peak–offpeak spread: $13–$16/MWh from 80% to 97.5%

Off-peak prices go negative first — creating the duck curve in price space

At 100% clean, off-peak median is −$12/MWh while peak is still −$2/MWh. Even in a fully decarbonized grid, demand-supply timing mismatch creates a $10 spread. This is the price manifestation of the duck curve — too much clean energy when demand is low, and storage arbitrage is the only remedy.

At 100%: peak −$2, off-peak −$12 — $10 spread remains
PJM Nuclear Economics

The Clean Energy Paradox: Decarbonization Destroys the Price Signal That Keeps Nuclear Alive

PJM's 32 GW existing nuclear fleet is the cheapest firm clean energy on the grid at $34/MWh wholesale. But the very renewable buildout needed to decarbonize the grid collapses wholesale prices below nuclear operating costs — creating a retirement risk for the resource you need most at high clean energy targets.

Retirement Threshold

The breakeven line: ~85% clean energy

Nuclear plants need $25–30/MWh to cover fuel ($6–8), fixed O&M ($15–20), and variable O&M ($3–5). PJM's median wholesale price crosses below $28/MWh at 85% clean and drops to $20/MWh by 90%.

Under low fuel prices — the scenario where cheap renewables are most likely — LMP drops to $16/MWh at 90% clean. Nuclear loses $9–14 on every MWh sold.

At 90% clean, Low fuel: $16/MWh LMP vs. $25–30 nuclear cost

Off-peak is where nuclear bleeds

Nuclear is baseload — it runs 24/7 and can't economically cycle. Off-peak hours are ~60% of the year. At 90% clean, off-peak LMP averages $19/MWh (P10 = $10/MWh). At 95%, it falls to $14/MWh. At 99%, off-peak hits $5–7/MWh — well below even fuel cost alone.

Simultaneously, zero-price hours explode: 2,660 hours at 90% (30% of the year), 4,270 at 95% (49%), 5,610 at 99% (64%). Nuclear earns nothing or pays to produce during these hours.

At 95%: off-peak $14/MWh, 4,270 zero-price hrs (49% of year)

Fuel price is the survival toggle

At 90% clean energy, the fuel price assumption separates nuclear survival from retirement:

High fuel ($45/MWh wholesale base): LMP averages $35/MWh. Nuclear clears with a $5–10 margin. Plants remain economic.

Medium fuel ($34/MWh): LMP averages $20/MWh. Nuclear loses $5–10/MWh. Retirement pressure mounts.

Low fuel ($28/MWh): LMP averages $16/MWh. Nuclear is deeply underwater. Fleet-wide retirement becomes rational.

The Replacement Cost Paradox

If nuclear retires due to price depression, replacing its firm generation capacity costs $105–163/MWh for new nuclear or $79–131/MWh for CCS-CCGT — 3–5× the wholesale price that caused the retirement.

This is the central tension: the grid needs nuclear at 90%+ clean targets, but the market doesn't pay enough to keep it running. Without capacity payments, clean energy standards, or nuclear production credits, the economic signal and the system need diverge completely.

High demand growth (PJM: up to 3.6%/yr) provides some price support by absorbing renewable output — but the LMP data shows it's insufficient. The wholesale price collapse is structural, not cyclical.

The uncertainty paradox: widest where it matters most

The P10–P90 spread in wholesale prices peaks at 75–80% clean energy — exactly the threshold range where most corporate buyers and utilities are currently planning. At 80%, the gap between the best- and worst-case outcome is $31/MWh. That's the difference between wholesale power cheaper than natural gas alone and wholesale power more expensive than today's average.

The implication: forecasting wholesale prices at 75–85% clean requires getting fuel cost assumptions right. Every other input is noise until you approach 100%.

$31/MWh spread at 80% · $0.4/MWh spread at 100%

Three eras of the wholesale market

Era 1 (50–75%): Fossil sets the price. Fuel costs dominate. Wholesale markets function as they do today, just at modestly lower levels. Zero-price hours are a nuisance, not a structural feature.

Era 2 (75–95%): The transition zone. Price uncertainty is at its peak. Zero-price hours become a defining feature (half the year by 95%). Storage and flexibility gain premium value.

Era 3 (95–100%): The structural break. Fossil no longer sets marginal price. All hours converge toward zero or negative. Revenue for all generators collapses. The market requires fundamental redesign.

50–75%: business as usual · 75–95%: transition · 95%+: new paradigm
