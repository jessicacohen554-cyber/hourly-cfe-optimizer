Three Ways to Count Carbon: Which One Actually Decarbonizes the Grid?

The GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision has sparked a three-way debate about how corporations should account for electricity emissions. The answer determines where hundreds of billions in clean energy capital flows — and whether it actually decarbonizes anything.

Annual RECs are the status quo. The proposed revision adds hourly matching with deliverability constraints — still attributional, but aligned to grid physics. A third camp argues for pure consequential accounting: chase the cheapest $/tCO₂ anywhere, regardless of whether it matches your load in time or space. Our analysis of 7 ISO/RTO regions shows that at just 20–30% C&I participation, pure consequential creates capital flight from gas-dependent grids toward coal displacement — leaving the hardest decarbonization problems permanently underfunded.

Key Findings — Top Down
20–30%
The participation threshold where consequential accounting breaks: just 20–30% of C&I load
Across 7 ISO/RTOs (~2.7 million GWh), pure consequential drives capital toward coal-heavy grids (SPP, MISO) at ~$15–25/tCO₂ and away from gas-dependent grids (NEISO, NYISO, CAISO) where displacement costs $40–80/tCO₂. At 25% C&I participation, gas-only grids receive less investment than their load share — the free-rider dynamics dominate and nobody funds firm clean power where it's needed most.
2–5×
The last 10% costs more — and that's exactly where innovation happens
Hourly matching from 90% → 100% costs 2–5× more per ton. Consequential accounting would tell you to stop and spend that dollar elsewhere. But these hard hours drive the technology development (firm clean power, LDES) that deep decarbonization requires.
Both
Hourly attribution is attributional AND has strong consequential impact
Hourly matching with deliverability creates accurate inventory accounting aligned to grid physics — while simultaneously driving real emissions displacement. It's not one or the other. The best attributional method also produces the best real-world outcomes.
3 Methods
Annual, hourly, and consequential are distinct frameworks
Annual attribution (current Scope 2) and hourly attribution (proposed revision) are both inventory accounting. Consequential is a separate paradigm — estimating marginal impact rather than tracking consumption. The GHG Protocol is consulting on both tracks simultaneously.
Free Rider
Consequential creates free-rider dynamics
If everyone chases the cheapest $/tCO₂ regardless of location, buyers cluster on the same low-hanging fruit while their own grids remain dirty. The data center in PJM claims abatement from a MISO wind farm while running on coal.
Counterfactual
Marginal impact modeling is inherently unverifiable
Consequential accounting depends on estimating what would have happened without the intervention. Marginal emissions rates shift hourly and vary by model assumptions. This creates massive scope for cherry-picking baselines and gaming results.

Three Methods, Three Philosophies

The debate isn't just "attributional vs. consequential." It's a three-way contest between fundamentally different theories of how carbon accounting should drive decarbonization — and each creates radically different investment incentives.

1. Annual Attribution Current

The status quo. Buy RECs equal to your annual consumption. No requirement for when, where, or whether the clean energy was actually delivered to your grid.

  • GHG Protocol Scope 2 market-based method (2015)
  • Annual matching: total RECs ≥ total consumption
  • No temporal or locational granularity required
  • A New England company buys Texas wind RECs at 2 AM to cover a winter 6 PM gas peak
  • Near-zero consequential emissions impact (Xu et al. 2024, WattTime 2024)

2. Hourly Attribution Proposed

The proposed Scope 2 revision. Match consumption with CFE hour-by-hour within deliverability regions. Still attributional inventory accounting — but aligned to grid physics.

  • Proposed GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision (expected ~2027)
  • Hourly matching: CFE supply = demand each hour
  • Deliverability constraints: same grid region / market boundary
  • Reveals need for firm clean power (nuclear, CCS, geothermal) + storage
  • Strong consequential impact as a byproduct of physics-aligned accounting

3. Consequential / Impact Advocated

Championed by WattTime and the Emissions First Partnership. Estimate marginal emissions impact of procurement decisions. Optimize for cheapest $/tCO₂ abated — anywhere, anytime.

  • Separate GHG Protocol consultation track (parallel to Scope 2 revision)
  • No requirement to match load temporally or spatially
  • Metric: tCO₂ displaced per dollar, measured via marginal emissions rates
  • Philosophy: pursue the cheapest abatement wherever it exists on the grid
  • Intellectual appeal but potential perverse incentives when decoupled from load

The Critical Distinction

Hourly attribution is attributional — it tracks what you consume and what you procure. But because it aligns accounting to grid physics and deliverability, it produces strong consequential impact as a natural byproduct. You can't match every hour without deploying resources that actually serve your grid. Pure consequential accounting, by contrast, explicitly doesn't require matching your load. It says: forget your consumption profile — just find the cheapest ton of CO₂ to displace anywhere. The question is whether that philosophy, scaled across thousands of corporate buyers, creates the right incentives for deep grid decarbonization.

The Evolving Landscape

The GHG Protocol is running two parallel consultations: one revising Scope 2 attributional accounting (proposing hourly matching), another developing a new consequential framework. 800+ stakeholders have weighed in. The outcome will reshape corporate clean energy investment globally.

2020
Google Commits to 24/7 CFE by 2030
First major company to commit to matching electricity consumption with carbon-free energy every hour of every day, on every grid where it operates. Published the CFE% metric for hourly tracking.
2021
UN 24/7 CFE Compact Launched
140+ signatories including governments, corporates, and NGOs committed to advancing hourly carbon-free energy. The U.S. Government joined at COP28 in 2023, directing federal facilities toward 50% 24/7 CFE by 2030.
2022–23
EnergyTag & Granular Certificates
EnergyTag develops standards for hourly energy attribute tracking. Time-based Energy Attribute Certificates (T-EACs) enable granular trading — not just "how much" but "when and where" clean energy was generated.
2024–26
GHG Protocol Dual-Track Consultation
Two parallel consultations (Oct 2025 – Jan 2026): Scope 2 revision proposing hourly matching + deliverability for attributional accounting, and a separate consequential accounting framework for impact-based disclosure. 800+ stakeholder responses. WattTime and Emissions First Partnership advocate for the consequential track; EnergyTag and 24/7 CFE supporters push for hourly attribution.
~2027
New Standards Expected
The revised Scope 2 standard and consequential guidance are scheduled for publication. Whether hourly attribution, consequential impact, or both become the corporate reporting default will determine the trajectory of hundreds of GW in clean energy procurement.
