Three Ways to Count Carbon: Which One Actually Decarbonizes the Grid?

The GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision has sparked a three-way debate about how corporations should account for electricity emissions. The answer determines where hundreds of billions in clean energy capital flows — and whether it actually decarbonizes anything.

Annual RECs are the status quo. The proposed revision adds hourly matching with deliverability constraints — still attributional, but aligned to grid physics. A third camp argues for pure consequential accounting: chase the cheapest $/tCO₂ anywhere, regardless of whether it matches your load in time or space. Each approach creates different incentives. Not all of them are good.

Three Methods, Three Philosophies

The debate isn't just "attributional vs. consequential." It's a three-way contest between fundamentally different theories of how carbon accounting should drive decarbonization — and each creates radically different investment incentives.

1. Annual Attribution Current

The status quo. Buy RECs equal to your annual consumption. No requirement for when, where, or whether the clean energy was actually delivered to your grid.

  • GHG Protocol Scope 2 market-based method (2015)
  • Annual matching: total RECs ≥ total consumption
  • No temporal or locational granularity required
  • A New England company buys Texas wind RECs at 2 AM to cover a winter 6 PM gas peak
  • Near-zero consequential emissions impact (Xu et al. 2024, WattTime 2024)

2. Hourly Attribution Proposed

The proposed Scope 2 revision. Match consumption with CFE hour-by-hour within deliverability regions. Still attributional inventory accounting — but aligned to grid physics.

  • Proposed GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision (expected ~2027)
  • Hourly matching: CFE supply = demand each hour
  • Deliverability constraints: same grid region / market boundary
  • Reveals need for firm clean power (nuclear, CCS, geothermal) + storage
  • Strong consequential impact as a byproduct of physics-aligned accounting

3. Consequential / Impact Advocated

Championed by WattTime and the Emissions First Partnership. Estimate marginal emissions impact of procurement decisions. Optimize for cheapest $/tCO₂ abated — anywhere, anytime.

  • Separate GHG Protocol consultation track (parallel to Scope 2 revision)
  • No requirement to match load temporally or spatially
  • Metric: tCO₂ displaced per dollar, measured via marginal emissions rates
  • Philosophy: pursue the cheapest abatement wherever it exists on the grid
  • Intellectual appeal but potential perverse incentives when decoupled from load

The Critical Distinction

Hourly attribution is attributional — it tracks what you consume and what you procure. But because it aligns accounting to grid physics and deliverability, it produces strong consequential impact as a natural byproduct. You can't match every hour without deploying resources that actually serve your grid. Pure consequential accounting, by contrast, explicitly doesn't require matching your load. It says: forget your consumption profile — just find the cheapest ton of CO₂ to displace anywhere. The question is whether that philosophy, scaled across thousands of corporate buyers, creates the right incentives for deep grid decarbonization.

The Evolving Landscape

The GHG Protocol is running two parallel consultations: one revising Scope 2 attributional accounting (proposing hourly matching), another developing a new consequential framework. 800+ stakeholders have weighed in. The outcome will reshape corporate clean energy investment globally.

2020
Google Commits to 24/7 CFE by 2030
First major company to commit to matching electricity consumption with carbon-free energy every hour of every day, on every grid where it operates. Published the CFE% metric for hourly tracking.
2021
UN 24/7 CFE Compact Launched
140+ signatories including governments, corporates, and NGOs committed to advancing hourly carbon-free energy. The U.S. Government joined at COP28 in 2023, directing federal facilities toward 50% 24/7 CFE by 2030.
2022–23
EnergyTag & Granular Certificates
EnergyTag develops standards for hourly energy attribute tracking. Time-based Energy Attribute Certificates (T-EACs) enable granular trading — not just "how much" but "when and where" clean energy was generated.
2024–26
GHG Protocol Dual-Track Consultation
Two parallel consultations (Oct 2025 – Jan 2026): Scope 2 revision proposing hourly matching + deliverability for attributional accounting, and a separate consequential accounting framework for impact-based disclosure. 800+ stakeholder responses. WattTime and Emissions First Partnership advocate for the consequential track; EnergyTag and 24/7 CFE supporters push for hourly attribution.
~2027
New Standards Expected
The revised Scope 2 standard and consequential guidance are scheduled for publication. Whether hourly attribution, consequential impact, or both become the corporate reporting default will determine the trajectory of hundreds of GW in clean energy procurement.

Hourly Attribution: Accurate Accounting That Drives Real Impact

Our 8,760-hour optimization model demonstrates what happens when you constrain procurement to match load hour-by-hour within deliverability regions. The accounting is attributional. The impact is profoundly consequential — because physics-aligned constraints force investment in the resources the grid actually needs.

The Cost Curve of Hourly CFE: Marginal Abatement Cost by Threshold

Hourly attribution reveals what annual accounting hides. When you require hour-by-hour matching within a deliverability region, the optimizer discovers that you need firm, dispatchable clean power (nuclear, CCS-CCGT, geothermal) alongside variable renewables and storage. Solar and wind alone can't cover the 8,760.

This is an attributional framework — but look at the consequential impact. By constraining procurement to match your actual load in your actual grid region, hourly attribution forces investment in the resources that grid physically needs. The resulting deployment directly displaces fossil generation in real time. You don't need a counterfactual model to see the impact — it's visible in the dispatch stack.

The MAC curve is relatively flat through 90%, then steepens dramatically. Those final hours — winter evenings, multi-day low-wind periods — are exactly where the value lies.

The Last 10% Problem is a feature, not a bug. Getting from 90% → 100% hourly CFE costs 2–5× more per ton. A pure consequential framework would tell you to stop at 90% and spend that marginal dollar displacing coal in a different grid. But those hard hours are precisely what drives innovation in firm clean power and long-duration storage — the technologies deep decarbonization requires everywhere.

Hourly attribution is the framework that says: invest in solving the hard problem on your grid, not the easy problem on someone else's.

The Consequential Trap: When Cheapest $/tCO₂ Creates Perverse Incentives

Pure consequential accounting has intellectual appeal — who wouldn't want to maximize emissions reduction per dollar? But when thousands of corporate buyers all optimize for the same metric without temporal or spatial constraints, the real-world dynamics get ugly.

1. The Free-Rider Problem

If every buyer chases the cheapest $/tCO₂ anywhere on the grid, they all cluster on the same low-hanging fruit — displacing coal with wind in coal-heavy grids. Meanwhile, the grids they actually operate on remain dirty. Nobody invests in decarbonizing their own consumption.

Example: A data center in PJM (40% coal) buys wind credits that displace MISO coal. PJM never decarbonizes because everyone's abatement dollar goes elsewhere.

2. Moral Hazard

A company can claim massive emissions reductions while its actual electricity consumption pattern — and the grid it operates on — never improves. The 24/7 load keeps running on fossil, but the balance sheet looks green because cheap abatement was purchased from a different grid.

Example: Tech company runs 100MW in NEISO (winter gas dependency) but claims carbon neutrality via Wyoming wind displacing Montana coal at $15/tCO₂. NEISO's winter gas problem persists.

3. No Signal for Firm Clean Power

Hourly matching forces the market to develop nuclear, geothermal, CCS, and long-duration storage — because you can't cover winter nights with solar. Pure consequential directs capital toward the cheapest marginal displacement, which is almost always more solar/wind in coal-heavy grids. The hard technologies never get the demand signal.

Result: Solar/wind overbuild in favorable regions; zero investment signal for the dispatchable clean resources that deep decarbonization requires in every region.

4. Counterfactual Gaming

The entire consequential method depends on modeling what would have happened without the intervention. Marginal emissions rates shift hourly, vary by model, and are inherently unverifiable. Sophisticated buyers will optimize for favorable baselines rather than genuine impact.

Problem: Two models of the same grid can produce marginal rates differing by 2-3×. Which one is "right"? Whoever picks the methodology picks their abatement number.

5. Race to the "Easy" Grids

Capital flows to grids where displacement is cheapest — typically coal-heavy, wind-rich regions. Expensive-to-decarbonize grids (like NEISO with winter gas pipeline constraints, or island grids dependent on diesel) get less investment, not more, because the $/tCO₂ is unfavorable.

Outcome: Coal-heavy grids get over-invested in wind; natural gas-dependent grids — which need the most innovative solutions — get ignored.

6. The "Last Mile" Gets Abandoned

Getting from 90% → 100% CFE on any grid is expensive. Pure consequential says: stop at 90%, spend the marginal dollar elsewhere. But the last 10% is where technology learning curves advance — it's the demand signal that makes firm clean power and long-duration storage commercially viable. Abandoning it everywhere guarantees deep decarbonization never arrives.

Paradox: Individually rational (spend elsewhere) but collectively catastrophic (nobody solves the hard hours, ever).

The Tragedy of the Carbon Commons

Pure consequential accounting is individually optimal and collectively suboptimal. Each buyer maximizes their own $/tCO₂ metric — but when every buyer optimizes for the cheapest ton anywhere, nobody decarbonizes their own grid. It's a classic tragedy of the commons: the shared resource (each grid's need for investment in firm clean power and storage) is neglected because each actor is incentivized to free-ride on the cheapest global abatement. Hourly attribution avoids this by tethering procurement to the buyer's actual load and grid — ensuring every dollar invested addresses the physical reality of that grid's decarbonization challenge.

6

What This Means for Clean Energy Buyers

The three-method landscape creates different strategic imperatives. The smart move: anchor on hourly attribution for your own grid, and supplement with consequential analysis to understand broader impact.

For Corporates & Data Centers

  • Adopt hourly matching now — don't wait for the 2027 standard. Match consumption to CFE hour-by-hour in your grid region
  • Invest in firm clean power — sign PPAs for nuclear, geothermal, or CCS-backed resources that cover the hard hours
  • Use consequential analysis as a supplement — measure displacement impact, but don't let $/tCO₂ optimization override grid-specific investment
  • Demand T-EACs — hourly certificates from counterparties, not annual bundles

For Policy & Standard-Setters

  • Anchor on hourly attribution — it's the framework that produces accurate accounting AND drives real grid investment
  • Be cautious with pure consequential — recognize the perverse incentives at scale. If adopted, require a "home grid" constraint to prevent free-riding
  • Build T-EAC infrastructure — regional granular certificate registries are the backbone of credible hourly matching
  • Allow consequential disclosure alongside — let companies show their impact, but don't replace inventory accounting with it

Hourly attribution reveals it directly: in ERCOT, 90% hourly CFE costs ~$52/tCO₂ — well below every major carbon benchmark. Even at 95%, it's competitive. The cost curve steepens above 95%, but even there it competes favorably with DAC ($150–400/t) and SAF ($136–500/t).

Pure consequential would say: stop at 90% in ERCOT and spend the marginal dollar displacing coal in MISO at $25/t instead. Individually rational. But if every buyer does this, ERCOT's last 10% — which needs firm clean power and LDES — never gets funded. And MISO gets oversaturated with cheap wind while nobody solves the hard-to-decarbonize hours anywhere.

Hourly attribution forces the hard investment. That's the point.
