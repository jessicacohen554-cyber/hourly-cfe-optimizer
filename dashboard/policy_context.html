Policy & Research Context

How hourly clean energy matching fits into the global landscape of climate policy, corporate commitments, and academic research on grid decarbonization.

Why Hourly Matching Matters Now

A convergence of policy revisions, corporate commitments, and academic research is transforming how organizations account for clean energy. The shift from annual to hourly matching isn't an incremental refinement — it's a fundamental change in what "clean energy" means, with direct implications for the resource mix, cost, and emissions impact of corporate procurement.

This analysis — modeling the cost of hourly clean energy matching across 10 thresholds, 5 US grid regions, and 324 cost scenarios — arrives at a moment when these questions are moving from academic theory to binding policy. The frameworks below define the landscape.

The Accounting Revolution: Annual to Hourly

The entire policy ecosystem is converging on a single structural change: replacing annual renewable energy certificate (REC) matching with hourly, locational matching. This shift has cascading implications for technology deployment, grid planning, and corporate procurement strategy.

The GHG Protocol Scope 2 Revision — The Linchpin

Every corporate climate framework ultimately depends on the GHG Protocol for its accounting foundation. The ongoing Scope 2 Guidance revision is the single most consequential policy process for hourly matching:

Sept 2024
Scope 2 Technical Working Group convened
July 2025
ISB approves TWG proposals for public consultation
Oct 2025 – Jan 2026
First public consultation period (now closed)
2026
Phase 2 development + second public consultation
2027
Final Scope 2 standard published

Proposed changes:

  • Hourly matching: All contractual instruments (RECs/GOs) must be issued and redeemed for the same hour as consumption
  • Deliverability: Instruments must be sourced from the same market boundary as the reporting entity's operations
  • Exemptions: Companies under ~5 GWh aggregate in a single market boundary exempt from hourly matching; phased implementation for existing contracts
The competing camps: Google, EnergyTag, and the Climate Group advocate hourly + deliverability as the only credible path. Amazon, Meta, and the Emissions First Partnership prefer "impact accounting" focused on marginal emissions avoided. The Center for Resource Solutions warns hourly requirements could collapse the voluntary REC market that drove $60B+ in clean energy investment.

Corporate & Voluntary Frameworks

Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)

Transitioning to Hourly

Founded 2015 • Partnership of CDP, UNGC, WRI, WWF • 8,000+ companies with validated targets

The most consequential framework because it sets binding targets with validation and enforcement. The draft Net-Zero Standard V2.0 (second consultation draft Nov 2025, final expected Spring 2026) introduces a phased hourly matching requirement:

  • 2030: 50% hourly matching required for large consumers (≥10 GWh in a single region)
  • 2035: Intermediate milestone (specific % TBD)
  • 2040: 90% hourly matching required; 100% low-carbon electricity
  • Additionality: Generation facilities must be commissioned or repowered within 10 years (tightens to 5 years by 2035)
Why this matters for our analysis: When SBTi requires hourly matching starting in 2028–2030, it will force a massive shift in corporate procurement strategy from "buy the cheapest annual RECs" to "build/contract for temporal and locational matching." Our optimizer quantifies exactly what that transition costs across different grid regions and technology scenarios.

RE100

Annual Matching

Launched 2014 • Climate Group + CDP • 450+ companies • ~500 TWh collective demand

RE100 drove enormous corporate renewable procurement — an estimated 17–60% of non-RPS renewable development in the US since 2014. But annual matching allows temporal mismatch: a company can claim "100% renewable" while running on fossil fuels at night.

  • Matching is annual: RECs purchased anywhere within a broad market boundary during the same calendar year count
  • Starting 2025: only generation facilities built within the last 15 years fully count (additionality push)
  • No announced timeline for RE100 to adopt hourly matching requirements
  • Climate Group launched the 24/7 Coalition as a separate, more ambitious initiative alongside RE100

The core tension: RE100 created real demand. The fear is that tightening to hourly matching too fast could collapse this demand. The counterargument: annual matching has become an accounting exercise that lets companies claim clean energy status while grid emissions remain unchanged during peak fossil hours.

UN 24/7 Carbon-Free Energy Compact

Hourly Matching

Launched Sept 2021 • SEforALL + Google • 170+ signatories • US joined at COP28 (Dec 2023)

Voluntary commitments to advance 24/7 CFE procurement practices, focusing on the "dirtiest hours" when the most fossil fuel is burned. Signatories include governments (US, Iceland, Kenya), tech companies (Google, Microsoft), and cities (Copenhagen, Paris, London via C40).

  • Every kWh matched with carbon-free generation in the same hour, on the same grid
  • Annual reporting on progress; no binding enforcement mechanism
  • Emphasis on demand signals for clean firm generation, storage, and demand flexibility

Climate Group 24/7 Carbon-Free Coalition

Hourly Matching

Launched Sept 2024 • The Climate Group • Technical Criteria V1.0 published June 2025

The most operationally specific corporate framework. Three guiding principles:

  • Time-matched: CFE generation matched to consumption each hour
  • Locally-matched: Procured on the same local grid where consumption occurs
  • Decarbonizing: Prioritize actions with greatest and fastest decarbonizing impact

Technical Criteria V1.0 is explicitly referenced by SBTi Net-Zero Standard V2.0 for defining "grid region." Intended as the implementation framework for the GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision. Founding partners: Google, Vodafone, AstraZeneca, AirTrunk, Iron Mountain, Shree Cement.

CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project)

Moving Toward Hourly

Founded 2000 • 24,000+ companies disclosing • 740+ financial institutions ($142T AUM) use CDP data

CDP's real power is investor pressure. When CDP scoring rewards hourly matching, capital allocation follows. Currently uses annual EAC matching per GHG Protocol, but already encourages temporal and geographic matching as a best practice. Will adopt hourly requirements once GHG Protocol finalizes them (expected post-2027).

CDP explicitly references EnergyTag's Granular Certificate Scheme Standard as a mechanism for hourly matching in its current guidance.

EnergyTag — Granular Certificate Standard

Market Infrastructure

Founded 2020 • First accredited issuers June 2025 • GC Scheme Standard V2 published

EnergyTag is the plumbing that makes all other frameworks operational. Without verifiable hourly certificates, the UN Compact, Climate Group Coalition, SBTi requirements, and GHG Protocol revisions are unenforceable aspirations.

  • Defines the technical standard for Granular Certificates (GCs) with hourly or sub-hourly resolution
  • First two accredited issuers: Energinet (Danish TSO) and Granular Energy
  • Referenced by CDP, SBTi V2.0 draft, Climate Group, and EU CBAM
  • EU CBAM connection: From Jan 2026, companies importing goods into the EU must demonstrate time-matched clean electricity supply for embedded emissions

Academic & Research Foundations

This project's methodology and findings are grounded in a substantial body of peer-reviewed research on grid decarbonization, clean firm power, and the system-level impacts of procurement strategies.

Princeton ZERO Lab — Clean Firm Power

Sepulveda, Jenkins, de Sisternes, Lester • Joule, November 2018

The foundational paper on clean firm power. Evaluated ~1,000 cases across varying CO₂ limits, technology costs, and geographic conditions:

  • Across all cases, least-cost decarbonization includes one or more firm low-carbon resources (nuclear, CCS, bioenergy)
  • Firm resources reduce electricity costs by 10–62% in fully decarbonized scenarios
  • Without firm resources, costs rise steeply and nonlinearly as CO₂ limits approach zero
  • Going "all-in" on wind/solar/batteries means "betting the planet on achieving very low costs for all of these resources" plus continental-scale transmission

Princeton — System Impacts of 24/7 CFE Procurement

Xu, Manocha, Patankar, Jenkins • 2022–2024

First study to model grid system impacts of hourly CFE using capacity expansion planning. Three procurement strategies compared:

  • Annual matching achieves zero or negligible system-level carbon emissions reductions — it displaces other renewables, not fossil generation
  • Hourly matching does reduce system-level emissions through both volume and timing effects
  • Cost premium for hourly matching: $13–$30/MWh above annual matching
  • With only current technologies, 24/7 costs 139% more than annual matching; with a full portfolio (CCS, H₂ turbines, advanced geothermal, nuclear), the premium drops to 54%
  • 24/7 CFE drives early deployment of advanced clean firm resources and long-duration storage that otherwise lack a market
The critical finding: Annual matching "almost entirely ineffective" at reducing emissions. It cannibalizes capacity that would have been built anyway. Only hourly matching creates demand signals for the technologies needed to fully decarbonize.

24/7 CFE Accelerates Advanced Clean Energy Technologies

Riepin, Jenkins, Swezey, Brown • Joule, January 2025 • TU Berlin + Princeton + Google

Even a small number of corporate 24/7 CFE buyers can catalyze innovation. Case study (Germany): if companies representing just 3% of C&I electricity demand committed to 24/7 CFE, it would quadruple expected deployment of long-duration iron-air batteries and achieve 25% cost reduction by 2030.

IPCC AR6 Working Group III — Energy Systems

2022 • Chapter 6: Energy Systems

  • Electricity sector decarbonization is the most cost-effective energy transition pathway (high confidence)
  • Renewable and low-carbon power generation: 74–82% of electricity by 2050 under 1.5°C pathways
  • Beyond ~80% VRE penetration, storage requirements escalate: 0.2–6 TWh/yr in the US
  • Nuclear, CCS, and geothermal identified as important complements, especially for the "last mile"
  • System costs increase nonlinearly as clean targets approach 100%
  • Solar PV identified as having the single greatest impact of any technology in any sector

IEA Net Zero by 2050 & World Energy Outlook

Net Zero Roadmap updated 2023 • World Energy Outlook 2025

  • Global electricity sector must reach net-zero by 2040
  • Renewables: >60% of generation by 2030; annual capacity additions ~1,200 GW
  • Solar PV and batteries alone are "not sufficient" — also required: nuclear, CCS, low-emissions fuels, massive grid expansion
  • 2024 global electricity CO₂: ~13.8 Gt; emissions intensity declined a record 3%
  • IEA projects global CO₂ emissions may peak around 2025 under current policies
  • Grid investment and permitting reform identified as the primary bottleneck

NREL — 100% Clean Electricity by 2035 & LA100

DOE-commissioned study, August 2022 • LA100 Study, March 2021

100% Clean by 2035 Study (ReEDS model):

  • Multiple technically feasible pathways exist; wind and solar provide 60–80% of generation
  • Additional power system costs: $330B–$740B; health benefits: $390–$400B; climate benefits: $1.2T+
  • Net societal benefit across all scenarios: $920B–$1.2T
  • Up to 5% of 2035 generation still from fossil fuels (offset via negative emissions)

LA100 Study (Los Angeles):

  • 100% renewable achievable by 2035 while maintaining high reliability
  • All scenarios follow a similar trajectory up to 80–90% renewable
  • Pathways diverge for the last ~10% — this is where the hard problem lives
  • For the final 10%, all scenarios rely on some form of dispatchable clean firm power

The Central Debate: Three Competing Visions

⏱

Hourly Matching

Every MWh consumed must be matched with a carbon-free MWh generated in the same hour, on the same grid. Aligns incentives with physical reality. Drives demand for storage, clean firm, and demand flexibility.

Advocates: Google, EnergyTag, Climate Group, Princeton ZERO Lab

📈

Emissions First / Impact Accounting

Focus on marginal emissions avoided, not temporal matching. A battery shifting solar from 2pm to 8pm may have more impact than a wind farm producing during surplus hours. Measurement challenge: marginal vs. average emissions.

Advocates: Amazon, Meta, Intel, Emissions First Partnership

⚠

Annual Matching (Status Quo)

Annual RECs drove real investment — $60B+ in clean energy. Hourly matching dramatically increases costs and could reduce total procurement volume. The perfect may be the enemy of the good.

Advocates: Center for Resource Solutions, REC market participants

What our optimizer reveals about this debate: The cost data across 16,200 scenarios shows that the "hourly is too expensive" argument depends heavily on the target threshold. At 85–90% hourly matching, costs remain manageable with modest resource diversification. It's the push from 95% to 100% where costs escalate nonlinearly — confirming IPCC and Princeton findings on the "last mile" problem. The answer isn't "hourly or annual" but "how many hours, and at what cost?"

How This Project Fits In

This optimizer sits at the intersection of all these policy frameworks and research streams. It answers the practical question they all raise but none fully quantify:

For any given grid region, with any given set of technology cost assumptions, what is the least-cost resource portfolio to achieve a specified level of hourly clean energy matching — and how does that cost and mix change as you push from 75% to 100%?
  • For SBTi-aligned companies: What will the 2030 (50% hourly) and 2040 (90% hourly) targets actually cost across CAISO, ERCOT, PJM, NYISO, and NEISO?
  • For RE100 members: What's the cost premium of moving from annual to hourly matching, and how does it vary by region and technology availability?
  • For policymakers: How do state RPS targets interact with hourly matching requirements? What additional resources are needed beyond what annual RPS compliance provides?
  • For the Emissions First debate: What resource mixes emerge from hourly cost optimization, and how do they differ from what annual optimization would produce?
  • For grid planners: Where are the inflection points — the thresholds where the required resource mix shifts dramatically and costs escalate?

The 16,200-scenario optimization (10 thresholds × 5 regions × 324 cost scenarios) provides granular, region-specific answers that complement the broader research from IPCC, IEA, NREL, and Princeton with actionable procurement-level data.
