About This Project

A systems-level analysis of how grid economics, corporate procurement, policy frameworks, and accounting rules interact — and where the gaps create both risk and opportunity for affordable decarbonization.

The Big Picture

Decarbonizing Electricity Is a Systems Problem

The power sector sits at the center of global decarbonization. But the path from today’s fossil-dependent grid to a clean one isn’t just an engineering challenge — it’s an economic puzzle where physics, policy, markets, and accounting rules collide. Most analyses treat these layers separately. This project connects them.

Annual clean energy targets — buying enough renewable energy certificates (RECs) to “match” total consumption over a year — hide a critical gap: the hundreds of hours when the sun isn’t shining and the wind isn’t blowing. A company can claim 100% renewable on paper while its actual consumption is powered by gas at 2am. The emerging global consensus is that hourly matching is the standard that actually drives decarbonization. But hourly matching is far more expensive than annual, and the cost depends on where you are, what resources are available, and what assumptions you make about future technology costs.

This project models that cost — hour by hour, across 8,760 hours per year, in five major U.S. grid regions, under hundreds of cost scenarios — to answer the question: what does it actually take to get clean electricity in every hour, and what does it cost?

The Layers

Six Interconnected Layers Shape the Energy Transition

Grid decarbonization isn’t driven by a single force. It’s shaped by overlapping layers of physics, economics, policy, and corporate action. Understanding how they interact — and where they conflict — is essential for making smart investment decisions.

01

⚡ System-Level Grid Economics

The physical grid operates on marginal cost dispatch: the cheapest available generator runs first. Adding renewables pushes fossil plants off the margin during sunny/windy hours, but the last 5–20% of hours (evenings, winters, low-wind weeks) are the hardest and most expensive to decarbonize. This creates a steep cost curve — the “last-mile” premium that dominates procurement economics.

02

🏭 Power Generation & Corporate Decarbonization

Utilities and IPPs face their own transition: retiring coal, extending nuclear, building renewables, and deciding whether to invest in CCS, long-duration storage, or next-gen firm power. Their fleet decisions shape the supply side of the equation. A region with 30 GW of nuclear (PJM) faces a very different clean energy future than one dominated by gas (ERCOT) or hydro imports (NYISO).

03

💼 Voluntary Corporate Buyers

Tech giants (Google, Microsoft, Amazon), data center operators, and Fortune 500 companies are the primary demand signal for new clean energy. Their procurement commitments — virtual PPAs, 24/7 CFE targets, hourly matching goals — shape which resources get built, where, and at what price. When corporate demand exceeds available clean supply in specific hours, Energy Attribute Certificate (EAC) scarcity emerges, driving up premiums.

04

🏛 State & National Policy

Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS), clean energy mandates, tax credits (ITC, PTC, 45Q for CCS), and capacity market designs set the floor for clean energy deployment. But policies designed for annual compliance create different incentive structures than those designed for hourly matching. A state that mandates 50% renewables annually may not incentivize the firm clean power needed to fill the hardest hours.

05

📋 Accounting & Reporting Frameworks

The GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision (expected ~2027) will likely require hourly matching and deliverability for emissions reporting. SBTi’s power sector pathways are moving toward hourly targets by 2030. EPRI’s SMARTargets framework offers an alternative approach. These frameworks define what counts as “clean” — and their design determines whether corporate spending actually drives real-world emissions reductions or just shuffles paper credits.

06

🌎 Global & National Goals

The Paris Agreement, IEA Net Zero by 2050, US federal clean electricity targets, and EU climate law set the destination. But goals without mechanisms are aspirational. The gap between “net zero by 2050” and “here’s how to affordably match clean energy in every hour of 2025” is enormous — and it’s where this analysis operates.

Systems Map

How These Forces Interact

These six layers don’t operate in isolation. Policies shape corporate behavior. Corporate demand drives generator investment. Accounting rules determine which investments “count.” And grid physics constrains what’s actually possible. The interactions can be catalytic — accelerating affordable decarbonization — or perverse, channeling dollars toward paper compliance rather than real impact.

The Scope 2 Revision Is the Inflection Point

The GHG Protocol’s Scope 2 guidance revision — expected around 2027 — will redefine what “counts” for corporate emissions reporting. If it mandates hourly temporal matching with deliverability requirements (as drafted), it will create an entirely new demand signal for firm clean resources. Combined with SBTi’s 2030 hourly matching trajectory for ~8,000 companies, this will reshape clean energy markets.

But if the revision is watered down to preserve annual matching, the current system of paper compliance persists — and the hardest hours stay dirty.

Research Gaps

What This Project Addresses

Existing research on clean energy procurement tends to focus on one layer at a time: grid planning models that ignore corporate procurement dynamics, corporate sustainability analyses that ignore grid physics, or policy assessments that don’t model cost sensitivity. This project bridges those silos.

Gap 1: Cost is treated as fixed, not as a variable

Most hourly matching analyses use a single cost assumption for each technology. But technology costs are deeply uncertain — solar LCOE ranges from $40 to $120/MWh depending on region and assumptions. This project co-optimizes the resource mix with costs: different cost assumptions produce different optimal portfolios. That’s not a sensitivity check — it’s the core finding. What you need depends on what things cost.

Gap 2: Regional variation is underappreciated

A 90% hourly clean energy target costs very different amounts in ERCOT (abundant wind, no state RPS competition) vs. PJM (nuclear-heavy but under data center pressure) vs. ISO-NE (transmission-constrained, high REC prices). National averages obscure the procurement decisions that actually face buyers. This project models five ISOs independently, each with real 2025 hourly generation profiles, regional cost tables, and local grid mix baselines.

Gap 3: The “last 5%” cost inflection is under-studied

Going from 75% to 90% hourly matching is relatively affordable. Going from 95% to 100% is dramatically more expensive — sometimes 5–10x the marginal cost. This inflection zone (90–100%) is where the most consequential procurement decisions happen, and it’s where this project concentrates its analytical resolution with 2.5% threshold granularity.

Gap 4: EAC scarcity is emerging but unquantified

As corporate demand for hourly-matched clean energy grows, some regions will run out of available clean supply during peak demand hours. PJM Tier I REC prices already hit $37–40/MWh — historic highs — before hourly matching is even mandated. This project quantifies the “clean premium” by ISO region, modeling how corporate participation rates drive scarcity.

Gap 5: Policies and frameworks aren’t evaluated against physical constraints

The 45Q CCS tax credit incentivizes gas plants to run at maximum capacity factor — even when the grid doesn’t need the power. Annual REC standards don’t create demand for the firm clean resources needed for the hardest hours. This project evaluates policy mechanisms and accounting frameworks against the actual hourly physics of the grid, revealing where incentive structures diverge from decarbonization goals.

Novel Insights

What This Analysis Reveals

By connecting grid physics, cost uncertainty, regional variation, and policy incentives into a single model, this project produces insights that single-layer analyses miss.

INSIGHT 01

Cost Drives the Optimal Resource Mix

Different cost assumptions don’t just change the price — they change what you should build. High renewable costs push toward nuclear; high firm costs push toward solar + storage. 16,200 independent co-optimizations reveal that the “right” portfolio depends on cost assumptions as much as on the target threshold.

INSIGHT 02

The Inflection Zone Is Steeper Than Expected

Between 90% and 100% hourly matching, marginal costs escalate non-linearly. The resources needed for the last 5–10% of hours (winter evenings, multi-day low-wind periods) are the most expensive and least utilized, creating a cost curve that punishes perfection.

INSIGHT 03

Region Determines Strategy, Not Just Cost

ERCOT’s optimal portfolio is fundamentally different from PJM’s — not just cheaper. Wind-rich regions need less storage; nuclear-heavy regions need less new clean firm. The “what you need depends on where you are” finding has direct procurement implications.

INSIGHT 04

Existing Clean Assets Are Undervalued

Nuclear uprates, existing hydro, and operating geothermal provide the cheapest clean firm capacity. But they’re being consumed by data center PPAs and state mandates, creating scarcity for voluntary corporate buyers who need them for hourly matching.

INSIGHT 05

45Q Creates a Gas Plant Persistence Incentive

The $85/ton 45Q credit for CCS makes gas+CCS plants economically incentivized to run at maximum capacity factor to maximize capture credits — regardless of grid need. This is a policy-designed perverse incentive that this model captures by treating CCS as baseload.

INSIGHT 06

EAC Scarcity Is Already Emerging in PJM and ISO-NE

Before hourly matching mandates even take effect, REC prices in PJM and ISO-NE have reached historic highs. Modeling corporate participation at 10–50% penetration reveals severe EAC scarcity in these regions, with clean premiums of $20–55/MWh — costs that will be borne by buyers or passed to ratepayers.
