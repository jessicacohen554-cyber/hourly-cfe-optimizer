Consequential Accounting in a Vacuum: Five Failure Modes at Scale

Pure consequential accounting — optimizing for cheapest $/tCO₂ displaced anywhere on any grid — is intellectually compelling in isolation. But what happens when you evaluate it as a system-wide procurement framework, not just a single buyer's decision? The math turns adversarial.

This analysis examines five mechanisms through which consequential accounting, adopted at scale, undermines the decarbonization outcomes it claims to optimize. Each mechanism is grounded in grid physics, market dynamics, and empirical data from 7 ISO/RTO regions.

1

The Marginal Signal Degrades With Every Buyer

Consequential accounting relies on marginal emissions rates — the emissions intensity of the generator that would have run without the intervention. But marginal rates aren't fixed properties of the grid. They shift as procurement volume scales.

At low volumes, marginal rates are meaningful. A single 100 MW wind PPA in MISO displaces the marginal gas or coal unit on each hour's merit order. The marginal emissions rate captures this displacement accurately — it's just reading the dispatch stack.

At 5–15% C&I participation, the signal begins to degrade. Each new PPA doesn't displace the same marginal unit. The first PPA displaces coal at 2,200 lb/MWh. The second displaces the next coal unit at 2,100 lb/MWh. By the tenth, you're displacing efficient CCGT at 850 lb/MWh. The marginal emissions rate that justified the first buyer's investment has halved by the time the tenth buyer arrives — but every buyer is still reporting the original marginal rate if they use static annual estimates.

This isn't theoretical. It's a direct consequence of merit-order economics: each unit of renewable generation displaces the next most expensive (and typically next most carbon-intensive) fossil generator. As more renewables enter a region, the marginal fossil unit shifts from coal → inefficient gas → efficient CCGT → the last peaker. The displacement curve is concave — steep at first, then rapidly flattening.

Double-Counting at the Margin

If 50 buyers each claim they displaced coal at 2,200 lb/MWh, but the grid only had enough coal to be displaced by the first 10, the other 40 are claiming reductions that don't physically occur. No registry prevents this because consequential accounting doesn't use certificate-based tracking — it uses models.

Related: Bjørn et al. (2024) show that buyer-level consequential claims can sum to 2–3× actual grid emissions reductions when aggregated across all participants.

Temporal Instability

Marginal rates vary hour-to-hour, season-to-season, and year-to-year. A buyer who contracts based on 2024 marginal rates may find their 2026 displacement rate is 40% lower because grid mix shifted, new gas plants came online, or coal retirements already happened. The "impact" they purchased doesn't exist three years later.

WattTime's own marginal rate data shows intra-year variation of 30–60% for the same hour across years in PJM and MISO.

Model Dependence

There is no single "correct" marginal emissions rate. Different dispatch models (statistical regression vs. structural economic dispatch vs. machine learning) produce rates that differ by 30–200% for the same grid-hour. A buyer choosing one model over another isn't measuring impact — they're selecting a narrative.

Comparison of WattTime, REsurety, and Singularity Energy marginal rates for PJM shows regular 2× divergence on the same hours.

The Measurement Paradox

Consequential accounting claims superiority because it measures "actual impact." But the measurement instrument — marginal emissions rates — becomes less accurate as more buyers use it. At low adoption, the signal is clean. At scale, it's noise. The framework is most reliable precisely when it's least needed (few buyers, small volumes) and least reliable when it matters most (widespread adoption, system-level decisions).

2

Saturation: When Cheap Displacement Runs Out

Every grid has a finite stock of "cheap abatement" — the coal and inefficient gas units that offer high displacement per dollar. Consequential accounting concentrates capital on this stock until it's exhausted, then the economics collapse.

Marginal Displacement Rate vs. Cumulative Renewable Penetration by ISO

The displacement curve is concave for every grid — steep at first, then flattening. In coal-heavy SPP and MISO, the first 10% of renewable penetration displaces coal at 1,800–2,200 lb CO₂/MWh. By 25% penetration, you've exhausted the marginal coal and are displacing gas at 850–1,100 lb/MWh. The "cheap tons" disappear faster than consequential buyers can contract for them.

The saturation problem is worse than the curve suggests because consequential capital from all seven ISOs competes for the same "attractive" coal displacement in SPP and MISO. A NEISO buyer and a CAISO buyer are both chasing the same MISO coal retirement. The available stock of cheap displacement doesn't scale with demand — it depletes.

After saturation: consequential accounting produces the same $/tCO₂ as hourly matching — because the marginal unit everywhere is now efficient gas. But the framework has already directed a decade of capital away from gas-dependent grids, starving them of the firm clean power investment they needed. The window for those grids' decarbonization has narrowed.

3

Fossil Lock-In: How Cheap Abatement Preserves Gas Infrastructure

The most insidious failure mode: by directing all marginal clean energy capital to coal displacement, consequential accounting extends the economic life of natural gas infrastructure in gas-dependent grids by decades.

The mechanism is straightforward. Gas-dependent grids (NEISO, NYISO, CAISO) need firm clean power — nuclear, geothermal, CCS-CCGT, or long-duration storage — to displace their fossil fleet. These technologies require large, long-term demand signals (PPAs, capacity contracts) to get financed and built. Under consequential accounting, corporate buyers in these grids direct their clean energy capital elsewhere because coal displacement in SPP/MISO offers better $/tCO₂.

The result: gas plants in NEISO, NYISO, and CAISO face no competitive pressure from firm clean alternatives. Without demand signals for nuclear/CCS/LDES in these grids, no developer builds them. Without new firm clean capacity, the gas fleet runs indefinitely — not because gas is cheap, but because nothing is being built to replace it.

NEISO: The Pipeline Trap

New England's grid is 70%+ gas, constrained by pipeline capacity from Appalachian shale fields. Winter gas prices spike 5–10× as heating and power generation compete for the same pipeline capacity. The grid needs 5–8 GW of firm clean power (offshore wind + nuclear + CCS) to escape gas dependence. Under consequential accounting, NEISO corporate buyers fund MISO wind instead — leaving the winter gas constraint permanently unfunded.

ISO-NE Winter Reliability Program (2024–2028) acknowledges 3+ GW of gas retirement risk with no committed firm clean replacement.

CAISO: The Duck Curve Deepens

California already has massive solar penetration. What it needs is dispatchable clean power for the evening ramp (4–9 PM) and multi-day low-solar events. Under consequential accounting, CAISO buyers purchase MISO coal displacement instead of contracting for CAISO geothermal, battery storage, or CCS — because the $/tCO₂ is more favorable elsewhere. The duck curve deepens, gas peakers remain essential, and CAISO's 2045 goals slip further.

CAISO 2024 Summer Assessment: 5.2 GW of gas peakers ran >1,000 hours, with no committed clean firm replacement in the interconnection queue.

NYISO: Peaker Retirement Stalls

New York City runs on gas peakers — old, inefficient, disproportionately located in environmental justice communities. Retiring them requires firm clean alternatives: offshore wind, battery storage, and demand response at scale. Consequential accounting diverts the capital that would fund these replacements, because displacing Midwest coal offers 40–60% better $/tCO₂ than replacing NYC gas peakers.

NYC peaker plants run 500–1,500 hours/year at heat rates of 10,000–12,000 Btu/kWh — 30% less efficient than modern CCGT. Environmental justice organizations have documented their disproportionate health impact.

The Lifespan Extension

Under hourly matching, a NEISO corporate buyer must contract firm clean power to cover winter peak hours — creating demand signals for offshore wind, nuclear, or CCS. Under consequential accounting, that same buyer funds MISO wind instead. Every year of delayed firm clean investment in gas-dependent grids is another year those gas plants run. At current gas fleet ages (median 15–20 years in NEISO/NYISO), consequential accounting could extend gas dependence by 10–20 years — exactly the timeframe in which deep decarbonization must occur.

Geographic Clustering: Capital Concentration Across 7 ISOs

When corporate buyers across all major U.S. grids optimize for cheapest $/tCO₂, capital doesn't distribute evenly. It concentrates in coal-heavy grids with the highest displacement rates — creating winners, losers, and infrastructure distortions that persist for decades.

The chart makes the capital concentration visible. Coal-heavy grids (SPP, MISO) pull capital from across the interconnection. Gas-only grids (NEISO, NYISO, CAISO) become net capital exporters.

The asymmetry is structural. SPP + MISO have 531 TWh of C&I load (~33% of total) but attract 45–55% of consequential clean energy capital at scale. NEISO + NYISO + CAISO have 294 TWh (~18%) but receive only 8–12% of capital — their own corporate buyers are funding abatement in other grids.

Participation Threshold: When Perverse Incentives Dominate

The 20–30% C&I participation band is the tipping point. Below 20%, capital allocation roughly matches each grid's share of C&I load — buyers invest where they operate. Above 30%, coal-heavy grids receive 40–55% of all clean energy investment while gas-only grids drop below their "fair share."

At 50%+ participation (~800+ TWh of consequential procurement), SPP's interconnection queue is saturated. MISO absorbs capital from PJM, NYISO, and NEISO buyers. Gas-dependent grids — which need the most innovative solutions — receive the least investment precisely because they're expensive to decarbonize.

The 60% threshold is existential: if more than ~60% of corporate load uses consequential accounting, the remaining hourly-matching buyers become too small a constituency to drive firm clean power investment in gas-dependent grids. Technology learning curves for nuclear, LDES, and CCS stall because the demand signal disappears.

5

The Tragedy of the Carbon Commons

Each failure mode reinforces the others. Marginal signal degradation makes displacement claims less credible. Saturation exhausts cheap abatement. Lock-in preserves fossil infrastructure. Geographic clustering starves the grids that need investment most. Together, they produce a collectively catastrophic outcome from individually rational decisions.

The game-theoretic structure is a textbook tragedy of the commons. Each buyer optimizing for their own cheapest $/tCO₂ is locally rational. But the shared resource — each grid's need for investment in firm clean power and long-duration storage — is neglected because every actor is incentivized to free-ride on cheaper abatement elsewhere.

Hourly attribution avoids this trap by construction. When procurement must match load hour-by-hour within a deliverability region, there is no mechanism for free-riding. A NEISO buyer must solve NEISO's winter gas problem. A CAISO buyer must cover the evening ramp. The constraint forces investment in the technologies each grid actually needs — not the cheapest global displacement.

The irony: hourly matching produces higher $/tCO₂ in the short run for any individual buyer — because it forces investment in firm clean power instead of cheap wind. But it produces lower total system emissions at scale — because it actually decarbonizes every grid rather than over-investing in coal displacement while leaving gas grids untouched.

Individual optimization vs. system outcome: Consequential accounting minimizes $/tCO₂ for each buyer. Hourly matching minimizes total emissions across the grid system. The GHG Protocol's choice between these frameworks isn't just accounting methodology — it's a choice between individual rationality and collective decarbonization.

Why This Isn't Just Theory

We're already seeing early signs: 70%+ of voluntary corporate renewable PPAs in 2023–2024 were for wind and solar in ERCOT, SPP, and MISO — the grids with the best $/MWh and $/tCO₂. NEISO and NYISO corporate PPA activity was minimal despite those grids having the highest marginal gas dependence. The capital flow pattern matches exactly what consequential optimization predicts — and it's happening under today's annual REC regime, which already has weak locational constraints.

Sources: BNEF Corporate PPA Tracker 2024; American Clean Power Association Market Report Q4 2024.

What Hourly Matching Solves

By requiring temporal and spatial matching, hourly attribution creates guaranteed demand signals for firm clean power in every grid. A 100 MW NEISO data center under hourly matching must contract ~30–40 MW of dispatchable clean capacity to cover winter peaks. Under consequential accounting, that same buyer could meet its full "impact" target with a MISO wind PPA — and NEISO's winter problem persists.

Our optimizer shows NEISO hourly matching at 90% CFE requires 15–20% firm clean power (nuclear/CCS). No consequential framework produces this demand signal.

The Bottom Line

Consequential accounting works as a niche analytical tool — measuring displacement impact alongside an hourly matching procurement strategy. It fails catastrophically as a primary procurement framework at scale. The five failure modes documented here aren't edge cases; they're the predictable, mathematically inevitable consequences of optimizing for cheapest $/tCO₂ across unconstrained geographies. The GHG Protocol's Scope 2 revision should recognize hourly attribution as the robust default — the framework that works at 1% participation and at 100%.
