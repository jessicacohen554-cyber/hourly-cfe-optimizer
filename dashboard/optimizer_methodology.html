The 8,760 Problem

Technical methodology behind the hourly clean energy matching optimizer.

Methodology & Technical Reference

This document details the optimization model, data sources, cost assumptions, and dispatch algorithms that power the analysis. The optimizer uses a three-phase sweep across 16,200 scenarios to co-optimize resource mix and cost at every threshold. All inputs are grounded in published data from NREL ATB, EIA, eGRID, and Lazard.

1. Overview

The Hourly Clean Energy Cost Optimizer determines the most cost-effective clean energy portfolio for achieving target levels of hourly matching across five major U.S. Independent System Operator (ISO) regions. Unlike annual matching—where a buyer procures certificates to cover total annual consumption regardless of timing—hourly matching requires that clean generation coincide with actual demand in each of the 8,760 hours of a year.

The tool co-optimizes cost and hourly matching simultaneously. For each target threshold (75%, 80%, 85%, 90%, 95%, 99%), the model searches across procurement levels (70% to 300% of annual demand), resource mix combinations (clean firm, solar, wind, hydro), and battery storage dispatch levels to find the lowest-cost portfolio that meets or exceeds the target.

Co-optimization is essential. A sequential approach (optimize matching first, then calculate cost) would fail to capture the cost-matching tradeoff: at lower thresholds, the cheapest solution may use a different procurement level or resource mix than the one that maximizes matching. For example, a solar/wind-heavy portfolio at 90% procurement may achieve 80% matching at lower cost than a clean-firm-heavy portfolio at 80% procurement, even though the latter uses less total energy.

The model also produces a sweep visualization showing the maximum achievable hourly match score at each procurement level from 70% to 300%+, illustrating the diminishing returns frontier as procurement increases.

2. Data Sources

2.1 EIA-930 Hourly Electric Grid Monitor

Demand and generation profiles are sourced from the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) Form 930 Hourly Electric Grid Monitor, which provides hourly balancing authority-level data for electricity demand and generation by energy source across U.S. ISOs.

Bulk CSV files (EIA930_BALANCE for demand, generation-by-source for supply profiles) covering the 2025 analysis year are combined from January–June and July–December files to produce full-year 8,760-hour datasets.

Source: EIA Hourly Electric Grid Monitor — https://www.eia.gov/electricity/gridmonitor/
About: https://www.eia.gov/electricity/gridmonitor/about

2.2 EIA Generation Profiles

Hourly generation profiles by fuel type (solar, wind, nuclear, hydro, geothermal) are extracted from EIA-930 data for each ISO region, capturing the actual temporal patterns of clean energy production—solar irradiance cycles, wind variability, and seasonal hydro dispatch.

Each profile is normalized so that the sum across all 8,760 hours equals 1.0, making the optimization scale-independent regardless of buyer load size.

Note on NYISO Solar: EIA does not report a separate solar generation series for NYISO. This tool uses NEISO's solar generation profile as a proxy for NYISO, labeled "solar_proxy" in the data. This is a reasonable approximation given the similar latitude and solar resource characteristics of the two regions.

3. Demand Profiles

Regional electricity demand profiles are extracted from EIA-930 balance data for each of the five ISO regions. The raw data provides hourly demand in megawatts (MW) for each balancing authority.

3.1 Chronological Ordering

EIA-930 CSV files use two columns for time identification: Data Date (the calendar date, e.g., "01/15/2025") and Hour Number (an integer 1–24 representing the hour ending within that date). The tool uses these two columns together to establish the correct chronological order of hourly observations. This pairing avoids ambiguity that could arise from timezone-shifted timestamps and ensures the demand vector aligns precisely with the generation profiles for accurate hourly matching.

3.2 Normalization

The demand profile is normalized so the sum across all 8,760 hours equals 1.0. Each hour's value represents that hour's fractional share of total annual demand. This normalization enables the optimization to operate on unitless ratios, making results independent of absolute load size.

Demand Normalization DemandNormh = DemandRawh / SUM(DemandRawh for all h) Where h = 1 to 8,760 and SUM(DemandNormh) = 1.0

3.3 Data Quality

EIA-930 data occasionally contains reporting gaps where all generation types report exactly zero for a given hour. These are identified as data anomalies (not real zero-generation events) and are excluded from peak gap analysis. Demand values below 100 MW are interpolated from nearest valid neighbors to prevent spurious results.

Outlier detection: Demand profiles are validated at load time using hour-of-day statistical screening. For each hour-of-day (0–23), if any single day's value exceeds 100× the median for that hour across the year, the entire year is excluded from the multi-year average. This catches EIA data entry errors (e.g., unit conversion errors that inflate individual hours by orders of magnitude). PJM 2021 was excluded due to a corrupt observation on October 19, 2021 where hours 03:00–05:00 UTC contained demand values approximately 20,000× normal levels. PJM demand shape is averaged over 2022–2025 (4 years). All other ISO-year combinations pass validation. Raw data is preserved unmodified for auditability.

4. Supply / Generation Profiles

The model uses four clean energy resource types, each with distinct hourly generation characteristics.

4.1 Clean Firm Profile

Clean firm generation (nuclear and geothermal) is modeled as a flat baseload profile: equal output in every hour (1/8760 of annual generation per hour). Nuclear capacity factors exceed 90% with minimal hourly variation; geothermal similarly provides constant output. Planned refueling and forced outages are not modeled.

4.2 Intermittent Profiles (Solar, Wind, Hydro)

Solar, wind, and hydro profiles are derived from actual 2025 EIA-930 hourly generation data for each ISO region. Each profile is normalized to sum to 1.0. When the optimizer allocates, for example, 30% of procurement to solar, the hourly solar supply equals:

Resource Supply Construction Supplytype,h = ProcurementFactor × ResourceSharetype × GenProfiletype,h Where: ProcurementFactor = ProcurementPct / 100 (e.g., 1.2 for 120% procurement) ResourceShare = resource percentage / 100 (e.g., 0.30 for 30% solar) GenProfiletype,h = normalized hourly generation shape (sums to 1.0)

4.3 Hydro Capacity Caps

Hydroelectric generation is constrained by regional availability. New hydro capacity is limited by geography and permitting, so the following caps reflect each region's realistic maximum share:

ISO RegionHydro Cap (%)Rationale
CAISO (California)29%Significant Sierra Nevada hydro resources
ERCOT (Texas)1%Very limited hydro resources
PJM (Mid-Atlantic)3%Limited Appalachian hydro
NYISO (New York)25%Substantial Niagara/St. Lawrence hydro
NEISO (New England)10%Moderate New England/Quebec hydro imports

These caps constrain the optimizer: the hydro share of any resource mix cannot exceed the cap for that region. The remaining portfolio (clean firm + solar + wind) must sum to at least (100% − hydro cap).

5. Optimization Model

The optimizer searches simultaneously across three dimensions: procurement level (70%–300% of annual demand), resource mix (clean firm / solar / wind / hydro percentages summing to 100%), and storage dispatch level, using two complementary strategies.

5.1 Dual Optimization Approach

Strategy A — Sweep (Max-Matching): For each procurement level (70%, 80%, 90%, 100%, 110%, 120%, 140%, 160%, ..., 300%), the model evaluates all resource mix combinations to find the mix that maximizes the hourly match score. This produces the sweep chart showing the matching frontier—the best possible matching at each procurement level.

Strategy B — Threshold (Min-Cost): For each target threshold (75%, 80%, 85%, 90%, 95%, 99%), the model searches simultaneously across procurement levels AND resource mixes to find the cheapest combination that meets or exceeds the target. This produces the threshold-specific results with cost estimates.

Key Insight — Co-Optimization: The co-optimization is essential because for lower matching targets (e.g., 75%), the cheapest solution might actually use higher procurement if the resource mix is cheaper. For example, 110% procurement with a wind-heavy mix in ERCOT (where wind is $40/MWh new-build) may cost less than 80% procurement with a clean-firm-heavy mix ($90/MWh new-build), even though the wind-heavy portfolio procures more total energy. A sequential approach would miss this.

5.2 Three-Phase Refinement

The optimizer uses a three-phase refinement strategy to balance computational speed with precision:

PhaseMix StepProcurement StepDescription
Phase 1: Coarse 5% 10% Evaluate all 5%-increment mix combinations across 10%-step procurement levels (70%–300%). Uses numpy-accelerated scoring without storage. Identifies top candidates within 15% of best cost.
Phase 2: Medium ±5% ±5% Refine top 8 candidates with ±5% mix adjustments and ±5% procurement adjustments. Tests storage at multiple dispatch levels (5%, 10%, 15%, 20% of annual demand).
Phase 3: Fine ±2% ±2% Fine-tune the top 5 finalists (within 5% of best cost) with ±2% adjustments. Tests storage in 2% steps for precise optimization.

5.3 Hourly Matching Calculation

For each candidate portfolio, the hourly match score is calculated by comparing total clean energy supply against demand in each of the 8,760 hours:

Hourly Matching Score For each hour h: Matchedh = min(Demandh, TotalSupplyh) Surplush = max(0, TotalSupplyh - Demandh) Gaph = max(0, Demandh - TotalSupplyh) Hourly Match Score = SUM(Matchedh) / SUM(Demandh) × 100%

Because demand is normalized to sum to 1.0, the match score equals the sum of matched values directly.

5.4 Numpy Acceleration

The coarse screening phase evaluates thousands of combinations using numpy vectorized operations. Supply profiles are pre-stacked into a (4, 8760) matrix and the hourly match score is computed in a single operation:

Vectorized Score Computation supply = procurement_factor × dot(mix_fractions, supply_matrix) matched = minimum(demand_arr, supply) score = sum(matched) Where supply_matrix is shape (4, 8760) for [clean_firm, solar, wind, hydro] and mix_fractions is a (4,) vector of resource shares

This reduces the coarse phase from minutes to seconds across all five ISO regions.

5.5 Mix Generation

Resource mix combinations are enumerated exhaustively: all combinations of clean firm, solar, wind, and hydro percentages summing to 100% (subject to hydro caps) at the given step size. At 5% steps this yields several hundred valid combinations per region.

6. Storage Model (Battery & LDES)

The model includes two distinct storage technologies with fundamentally different duration, efficiency, and dispatch characteristics. Both shift surplus clean energy from oversupply periods to deficit periods, improving hourly matching without additional generation procurement.

6.1 Technology Comparison

ParameterBattery (4hr Li-ion)LDES (100hr Iron-Air)
Duration4 hours100 hours
Round-Trip Efficiency (RTE)85%50%
Dispatch CycleDaily (24-hour)Multi-day / seasonal (7-day rolling window)
Primary Use CaseIntra-day shifting (solar peak → evening)Multi-day weather events, seasonal balancing
ChemistryLithium-ion (NMC/LFP)Iron-air (Fe-air)
MaturityCommercial, widely deployedPre-commercial / early deployment
SourceNREL ATB 2024NREL ATB 2024, Form Energy data
Why two storage types: 4-hour batteries excel at daily solar shifting but cannot bridge multi-day weather events (e.g., 3-day windless periods). LDES fills this gap with 100-hour duration, enabling week-scale energy shifting at the cost of lower round-trip efficiency. H2 storage is explicitly excluded from this model.

6.2 Battery Dispatch Algorithm (Daily Cycle)

Battery storage operates on a daily cycle. For each 24-hour period, the simulation:

  1. Identifies hours with surplus clean generation (supply > demand)
  2. Identifies hours with gaps (demand > supply)
  3. Charges from surplus hours, prioritizing largest surpluses first
  4. Discharges into gap hours, prioritizing largest gaps first
  5. State of charge resets at the start of each day
Battery Dispatch Derivation Daily Dispatch Target = (StorageDispatchPct / 100) / NumDays Power Rating (MW) = Daily Dispatch Target / 4 hours Required Charge = Dispatch / 0.85 (RTE) Actual Dispatch = min(DailyTarget, AvailableSurplus × 0.85, TotalGap)

6.3 LDES Dispatch Algorithm (Multi-Day / Seasonal)

LDES uses a fundamentally different dispatch algorithm designed for multi-day and seasonal energy shifting:

  1. Rolling 7-day window (fine phase uses full-year optimization)
  2. Identifies sustained multi-day surplus periods (spring wind runs, extended sunny stretches)
  3. Charges during surplus periods up to power rating (capacity / 100 hours), respecting energy capacity
  4. Identifies sustained multi-day deficit periods (winter evening doldrums, cloudy windless stretches)
  5. Discharges during deficit periods up to power rating, limited by stored energy × 50% RTE
  6. Captures week-to-week and seasonal patterns that 4-hour batteries cannot address
LDES Dispatch Derivation Power Rating (MW) = Energy Capacity (MWh) / 100 hours Usable Discharge = Stored Energy × 0.50 (RTE) Charge Window: Rolling 7-day surplus identification Discharge Window: Rolling 7-day deficit identification State of charge persists across days (no daily reset)

6.4 Operating Constraints (Both Technologies)

  • Charge only from surplus: Storage absorbs only excess clean energy, never grid power
  • Dispatch only into gaps: Storage discharges only during hours with unmet demand
  • Power limit: Charge and discharge rates capped by power rating (MW)
  • Battery efficiency loss: For every 1 MWh charged, 0.85 MWh is dispatched
  • LDES efficiency loss: For every 1 MWh charged, 0.50 MWh is dispatched
  • Battery daily reset: State of charge resets each day for even annual distribution
  • LDES persistent state: State of charge carries across days within rolling windows
  • Greedy dispatch: Largest surpluses/gaps addressed first in both technologies

6.5 Storage Optimization

The storage dispatch targets (as a percentage of annual demand) are optimized independently for battery and LDES. During the sweep (max-matching) phase, storage is swept from 0% to 50% in 5% steps, then refined to 1% around the best level. During threshold optimization (min-cost), storage is tested at discrete levels (5%, 10%, 15%, 20%) during Phase 1–2, and in 2% steps during Phase 3. Higher dispatch targets deploy more storage capacity, shifting more energy but incurring higher costs.

7. Cost Model

The cost model is integrated directly into the threshold optimization—cost and resource mix are co-optimized for every scenario. Different cost assumptions produce different optimal resource mixes. This co-optimization is the core scientific contribution: cost is never a secondary overlay applied post-hoc.

7.1 Two-Tier Pricing Model

The model distinguishes between two pricing tiers for each resource type:

  • Existing resources (up to the current grid mix share for that resource in that region) are priced at the regional wholesale electricity market price. This represents contracting existing clean generation capacity that already operates on the grid.
  • New-build resources (any procurement above the current grid mix share) are priced at the regional new-build LCOE + transmission adder. This represents financing and constructing new clean generation capacity plus interconnection costs.
Blended Resource Cost For each resource type r in region i: resource_mwh = procurement_factor × (resource_pct / 100) existing_mwh = min(resource_mwh, grid_mix_sharer,i / 100) new_mwh = max(0, resource_mwh - grid_mix_sharer,i / 100) resource_cost = existing_mwh × wholesale_pricei + new_mwh × (lcoer,i + transmission_adderr,i) Total blended cost = SUM(resource_cost for all r) + battery_cost + ldes_cost Special cases: • Hydro: always wholesale priced ($0 transmission), no new-build tier • CCS-CCGT: no existing share (entirely new-build), all at LCOE + transmission

7.6 Curtailment and Effective Cost

Surplus procurement results in curtailment: clean energy generated when supply already exceeds demand cannot be used for matching. The buyer pays for all procured energy but receives matching credit only for the portion that temporally coincides with demand.

Effective Cost Per Useful MWh Effective $/MWh = Total Procurement Cost / Matched Fraction of Demand Where: Total Procurement Cost = SUM(resource costs) + storage cost (in $/MWh-of-demand) Matched Fraction = Hourly Match Score / 100 Curtailment % = (Procurement Factor - Matched Fraction) / Procurement Factor × 100

At 200% procurement with 95% matching, nearly half of procured energy is curtailed, roughly doubling the effective cost per useful MWh. This curtailment penalty is the fundamental economic constraint on achieving high hourly match scores with intermittent resources alone.

7.7 Incremental Cost

Results include both the total effective cost and an incremental cost above the wholesale baseline. The baseline represents undifferentiated grid electricity at regional wholesale prices; the increment represents the premium for hourly matching.

Incremental Cost Incremental $/MWh = Effective $/MWh - Regional Wholesale Price

8. Compressed Day Visualization

For each threshold result, the tool generates a compressed day profile—a representative 24-hour shape aggregating all 8,760 hourly values by hour-of-day.

8.1 Aggregation Method

For each hour-of-day (0–23), corresponding values are summed across all 365 days. For example, the “hour 14” solar value represents cumulative 2:00 PM generation across the year, producing a representative daily shape that preserves total annual volumes.

8.2 Stack Composition

The compressed day is decomposed into matched and surplus components for each resource type. Resources are matched to demand in a priority order that places baseload resources first:

  1. Clean Firm (matched first)
  2. Hydro
  3. Wind
  4. Solar
  5. Storage Dispatch (fills remaining gaps)

Surplus cutting follows the reverse order: solar surplus is cut first, then wind, then hydro, then clean firm. Storage charging absorbs surplus in the same cut order, reducing the tradeable surplus shown in the visualization.

Important: The compressed day chart is an illustrative representation. Because it aggregates at the hour-of-day level, each hour shows either surplus or gap, never both simultaneously. The precise hourly match score is calculated from the exact hour-by-hour matching across all 8,760 hours and may differ slightly from what the compressed day implies.

8.3 Storage Visualization

Storage charge is shown as negative values (below zero) in the compressed day chart, while storage dispatch appears as a positive component filling gap hours. This visually represents the time-shifting of clean energy from surplus hours to deficit hours.

9. Assumptions, Limitations & Considerations

This section provides a comprehensive inventory of model limitations. Understanding these constraints is essential for interpreting results correctly. Results represent a cost-optimized theoretical floor assuming no supply constraints; real-world costs will likely be higher due to the factors documented below.

9.1 Physical Resource & Supply Chain Constraints

The model assumes all clean energy technologies can be deployed at their stated LCOE/LCOS without material supply constraints. In practice, critical mineral availability, processing bottlenecks, and supply concentration risks could drive scarcity pricing that raises real-world costs above modeled values.

  • Uranium supply (Clean Firm / Nuclear): The model assumes nuclear capacity can be built at LCOE without fuel supply constraints. Real-world uranium availability, enrichment capacity (limited global centrifuge infrastructure), and fuel fabrication bottlenecks could drive scarcity pricing—particularly if multiple nations pursue nuclear buildout simultaneously. The U.S. currently imports ~93% of its uranium, with significant dependence on Russia and Kazakhstan for enrichment services. (Sources: World Nuclear Association Supply Data; US DOE/EIA Uranium Marketing Annual Report)
  • Cobalt, lithium, and nickel (Battery Storage): Battery LCOS assumes stable material prices. Cobalt production is concentrated in the Democratic Republic of Congo (~70% of global supply), lithium processing is dominated by China and Chile, and nickel supply chains face their own geographic concentration. Simultaneous global EV and grid storage buildout could create scarcity pricing that raises battery costs above modeled values. (Sources: IEA Critical Minerals Market Review 2024; USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries)
  • Rare earth elements (Wind Turbines): Neodymium and dysprosium for permanent magnets in direct-drive wind turbines face severe supply concentration—China controls >60% of rare earth mining and ~90% of processing. While geared turbines can avoid rare earths, the highest-efficiency offshore and large onshore turbines increasingly rely on permanent magnet generators. (Source: DOE Critical Materials Assessment 2023)
  • Silicon and silver (Solar PV): Polysilicon supply chain is concentrated in China (>80% of global production), and silver demand for PV metallization contacts is growing rapidly. Silver supply is inelastic (primarily a byproduct of copper/zinc mining), creating potential for price spikes as solar deployment scales. (Sources: IRENA; BNEF Solar Supply Chain Analysis)
  • Iron ore (LDES / Iron-Air): Iron-air batteries (e.g., Form Energy) require significant iron, but iron ore is globally abundant with diversified supply (Australia, Brazil, India, Russia). This is a relative advantage of iron-air chemistry over lithium-based alternatives—supply chain risk for LDES is materially lower than for Li-ion batteries.
  • Copper (Grid Infrastructure & Transmission): Transmission buildout and all electrical infrastructure require massive quantities of copper. Mining lead times for new copper deposits are 10–15 years from discovery to production, and global copper demand from electrification (EVs, heat pumps, grid expansion) is projected to double by 2035. Copper scarcity could raise transmission costs beyond modeled adders. (Source: S&P Global “Future of Copper” Report)
  • Steel and concrete (All Construction): All energy infrastructure requires steel and concrete, which have their own embodied emissions (cement production alone accounts for ~8% of global CO2). The model does not account for embodied emissions or the cost impact of potential carbon pricing on construction materials.

9.2 Modeling & Temporal Limitations

  • Static 2025 snapshot: The model captures a single point in time with no dynamic capacity additions, learning curves, or technology evolution. Real-world procurement happens over decades, during which costs decline, new technologies emerge, and grid conditions change.
  • Hourly resolution only: The model uses hourly data; sub-hourly grid dynamics including frequency regulation, voltage support, ramping constraints, and inertia requirements are not captured. These constraints can limit instantaneous renewable penetration.
  • Perfect foresight: The optimizer has perfect knowledge of hourly generation and demand profiles for the full year. Real procurement decisions face significant weather uncertainty, demand forecast error, and generator outage risk.
  • Multi-year weather averaging: Generation and demand profiles are averaged over 2021–2025 EIA-930 data (with automatic outlier exclusion; see Â§3.3). While this smooths inter-annual variability, extreme weather events in any single year are diluted rather than captured as scenario variants. Year-to-year variability in solar irradiance, wind patterns, and hydro availability could shift results by several percentage points. Multi-year analysis would capture extreme weather events (e.g., Texas Winter Storm Uri, California heat domes).
  • Perfect clean firm availability: Nuclear/geothermal is modeled as perfectly flat baseload. Real nuclear plants have planned outages (refueling ~30 days every 18–24 months) and occasional forced outages, which may slightly overstate clean firm's contribution.
  • Proxy data: EIA-930 grid-level generation patterns are used as proxies for project-level assets. Actual generation profiles for a specific solar or wind farm may differ from the regional average due to microclimate, panel orientation, and turbine technology.
  • Daylight Saving Time: UTC offsets are applied as fixed shifts and do not account for DST transitions. This may introduce a 1-hour misalignment for portions of the year in affected regions.

9.3 Grid & Infrastructure Constraints

  • No physical transmission modeling: Transmission is modeled as a cost adder ($/MWh) on new-build resources, but physical congestion, curtailment from transmission limits, deliverability requirements, and interconnection queue delays (currently 5+ years in most ISOs) are not modeled. Results assume clean energy can be delivered to the load in any hour within the same ISO region.
  • No reliability / adequacy assessment: The model does not calculate loss-of-load probability (LOLP), reserve margins, or capacity adequacy metrics. Real power systems require reliability buffers (typically 15–20% reserve margin) that add cost beyond what the optimizer computes.
  • No land use constraints: Solar and wind deployment is assumed unlimited within cost tiers. Real siting faces environmental review (NEPA/CEQA), community opposition, wildlife concerns (avian impacts for wind, desert tortoise habitat for solar), and competing land uses (agriculture, conservation).
  • No construction timeline modeling: All resources are assumed instantly available. Real construction takes 2–3 years for solar/wind, 3–5 years for battery storage at scale, 5–7+ years for nuclear, and 7–10+ years for new transmission lines.
  • No water constraints: Nuclear and CCS-CCGT require cooling water. Drought conditions and water scarcity—particularly relevant in CAISO and ERCOT—could constrain siting options or require expensive dry cooling systems not reflected in modeled LCOE.
  • No workforce availability: Labor shortages for skilled trades (electricians, ironworkers, nuclear operators, specialized welders) could constrain deployment rates and raise costs. The nuclear industry in particular faces a decades-long workforce gap.

9.4 Economic & Market Limitations

  • Static cost assumptions: LCOE and wholesale price values are fixed. In reality, costs change over time as markets mature, supply chains develop, and technology improves.
  • No financing cost differentiation: LCOE/LCOS is assumed constant regardless of project size, developer creditworthiness, or interest rate environment. Real financing costs vary significantly with Federal Reserve policy, project risk profile, and developer balance sheet strength. A 200 bps change in cost of capital can shift LCOE by 10–15%.
  • No market price feedback: The model does not account for the price effects of large-scale clean energy deployment (merit order effects, price cannibalization of solar during midday hours, wholesale price depression from high renewable penetration).
  • No grid services revenue: Clean energy resources can earn ancillary service revenue (frequency regulation, spinning reserves, capacity payments) that offsets procurement costs. The model does not capture this potential revenue, which may make results appear more costly than real-world net costs.
  • No policy risk: ITC/PTC extension uncertainty, 45Q credit durability, state RPS changes, and potential carbon pricing are not modeled. Policy changes could significantly alter the relative economics of different technologies.
  • No demand response or efficiency: Demand is assumed fixed at historical hourly patterns. Real systems use demand response programs, energy efficiency measures, and flexible load shifting (e.g., EV charging, thermal storage) that can significantly reduce the cost of achieving high matching targets.
  • Scalability of procurement: At very high procurement levels (200%+), the model results are more theoretical than practical. Deploying that volume of clean energy in a single ISO region presents real-world market absorption and grid integration challenges.

9.5 Emission Accounting Limitations

  • CO2 hourly methodology: CO2 displacement uses hourly variable emission rates built from EPA eGRID 2023 per-fuel factors (coal, gas, oil) weighted by EIA-930 hourly fossil fuel mix shares. This captures hour-by-hour variation in carbon intensity as the fossil dispatch mix changes. Under fuel price sensitivity scenarios, coal/gas shares shift via regional fuel-switching elasticity.
  • No embodied emissions: Life-cycle emissions from manufacturing, construction, and decommissioning of clean energy infrastructure are not included. Solar panels, wind turbines, and batteries all have embodied carbon from mining, manufacturing, and transport.
  • 5 ISO regions only: The tool currently covers CAISO, ERCOT, PJM, NYISO, and NEISO. Other U.S. regions (MISO, SPP, etc.) are not yet included, limiting national applicability.

9.6 Storage Model Limitations

  • Two storage technologies only: Only 4h Li-ion battery and 100h iron-air LDES are modeled. Other storage technologies (8h Li-ion, pumped hydro, compressed air, hydrogen) could provide additional flexibility but are not included.
  • Simplified dispatch: The battery storage simulation uses a daily cycling model with greedy charge/discharge allocation. Real-world battery operation involves more sophisticated co-optimized dispatch that could achieve marginally better economic results.

9.7 Interpreting Results Given These Limitations

  • Results represent a cost-optimized theoretical floor assuming no supply constraints, no siting barriers, and no construction delays.
  • Real-world costs will likely be higher due to resource constraints, permitting timelines, supply chain friction, and financing variability.
  • The relative ordering of technologies (which is cheapest in each region) is more robust than absolute cost values, because supply constraints tend to affect all projects in a region similarly.
  • Users should treat results as directional guidance for procurement strategy, not as precise cost predictions for specific projects.
  • The sensitivity toggles (Low/Medium/High) partially capture cost uncertainty but do not capture all constraint dimensions listed above. Physical resource constraints, permitting risk, and workforce shortages represent additional cost uncertainty beyond the toggle ranges.

10. Sensitivity Toggle Pairing Methodology

10.1 Overview

The model uses 5 paired sensitivity toggle groups instead of 10 individual toggles. Related cost variables are grouped so they move in lockstep (all Low, all Medium, or all High), reducing dashboard complexity while preserving meaningful sensitivity analysis. Each toggle group controls two or more underlying cost parameters that share structural cost drivers, ensuring that the paired movement reflects real-world correlation rather than an arbitrary simplification.

10.2 The 5 Paired Groups

10.2.1 Renewable Generation Cost (Solar LCOE + Wind LCOE)

Solar and wind costs are driven by similar factors—manufacturing scale economies, global supply chain dynamics (polysilicon, rare earths, steel), installation labor markets, and federal incentive structures (ITC/PTC). Historical data from NREL ATB and Lazard shows these costs have moved in parallel over the past decade, typically within ±15% of each other’s year-over-year change rates. Pairing them reflects the structural correlation in their cost drivers.

10.2.2 Firm Generation Cost (Clean Firm LCOE + CCS-CCGT LCOE)

Nuclear/geothermal and CCS-CCGT share capital-intensive, long-lead-time cost structures. Both face similar financing cost sensitivity (high WACC impact on levelized cost), similar construction labor constraints, and similar regulatory/permitting timeline risks. The key cost uncertainty for both is construction duration and financing terms rather than fuel or operating costs.

10.2.3 Storage Cost (Battery LCOS + LDES LCOS)

Li-ion battery and iron-air LDES costs share upstream manufacturing/materials cost drivers. Both depend on mining output (lithium, iron, nickel), battery manufacturing scale, and balance-of-system installation costs. While they use different chemistries, the dominant cost factors—manufacturing learning curves, raw material prices, and project development costs—are correlated.

10.2.4 Fossil Fuel Price (Natural Gas + Coal + Oil)

Energy commodity prices are structurally correlated through substitution effects (fuel switching in power generation), macroeconomic conditions (GDP growth, industrial demand), and geopolitical supply factors. The EIA Annual Energy Outlook consistently models these as co-moving in Low/Reference/High scenarios. In the power sector specifically, gas-coal fuel switching creates a direct price linkage.

10.2.5 Transmission Cost (All Resource Adders)

Grid interconnection and transmission infrastructure costs are driven by region-specific factors—steel/conductor commodity prices, right-of-way acquisition costs, specialized labor availability, and permitting/regulatory timelines. These factors affect all new-build resources within a region roughly equally, making a single regional transmission cost toggle appropriate.

10.3 Paired Toggle Groups Summary

Toggle Group Paired Variables Levels Key Correlation Driver
Renewable Generation Cost Solar LCOE, Wind LCOE Low / Medium / High Shared supply chain, manufacturing scale, federal incentives
Firm Generation Cost Clean Firm LCOE, CCS-CCGT LCOE Low / Medium / High Capital-intensive construction, financing terms, permitting

10.4 Scenario Count Reduction

With 5 paired groups (3 levels each, except Transmission with 4 levels including None), the total cost scenario space is:

Scenario Space Comparison Paired toggles: 3 × 3 × 3 × 3 × 4 = 324 scenarios per region per threshold Independent toggles: 310 = 59,049 scenarios per region per threshold Reduction factor: 182× fewer scenarios

This reduction from 59,049 to 324 scenarios makes real-time dashboard interaction feasible while preserving the ability to explore meaningful cost sensitivities across all major cost categories.

10.5 Optimization Approach

Resource mix is optimized once at Medium costs. The dashboard applies Low/Medium/High cost overlays on cached resource mixes without re-running the optimizer, since cost toggle changes affect $/MWh calculations but not the optimal hourly resource dispatch pattern. The dispatch pattern depends on resource generation profiles (solar shape, wind shape, etc.) and the procurement level, which are independent of cost assumptions. Only the financial result—the total and effective cost per MWh—changes when toggles are adjusted.

Why this works: The hourly match score is a physical quantity determined by how much clean energy is available in each hour relative to demand. Changing the price of solar does not change how much solar is generated at 2 PM. Therefore, the same optimized resource mix and procurement level remain valid across all cost scenarios—only the cost metric shifts.

11.1 Marginal Emission Rates

Each MWh of clean energy procured displaces fossil generation at the regional marginal emission rate derived from EPA eGRID data. The marginal rate represents the CO2 intensity of the generation that would otherwise serve load—typically natural gas combined cycle or coal units at the margin of the dispatch stack.

RegionMarginal Emission Rate (tCO2/MWh)Dominant Marginal FuelSource

When gas prices rise in PJM, coal dispatch increases and the marginal emission rate climbs. In CAISO and NEISO, where coal has largely retired, the emission rate is less sensitive to gas price changes.

11.2 CCS-CCGT Partial Credit

CCS-CCGT receives a partial emission displacement credit. Unlike zero-emission resources (solar, wind, nuclear), CCS-CCGT has residual emissions due to the 90% capture rate:

CCS-CCGT CO2 Accounting Unabated CCGT emission rate: ~0.37 tCO2/MWh CCS capture rate: 90% Post-capture residual: 0.37 × (1 − 0.90) = ~0.037 tCO2/MWh Net displacement per MWh of CCS-CCGT: = Regional marginal rate − CCS residual rate = (e.g., 0.42 − 0.037) = ~0.383 tCO2/MWh displaced (ERCOT example) Compared to zero-emission resource displacing same marginal unit: = 0.42 tCO2/MWh displaced CCS-CCGT achieves ~91% of the displacement of a zero-emission resource

11.3 Abatement Cost Formulas

The model calculates two abatement cost metrics for each threshold and scenario:

Average Cost of Abatement Average $/ton CO2 = Total Incremental Cost / Total CO2 Abated Where: Total Incremental Cost = (Effective $/MWh − Wholesale $/MWh) × Annual Demand (MWh) Total CO2 Abated = SUM(Clean MWhtype × Displacement Ratetype)
Marginal Cost of Abatement Marginal $/ton CO2 = (CostX+1% − CostX%) / (CO₂X+1% − CO₂X%) Represents the cost of the next ton of CO2 abated at each threshold step. Exhibits hockey-stick behavior: cheap early tons, exponentially expensive last tons.

12. Hydro Treatment Rationale

Hydroelectric generation receives unique treatment in the model, distinct from all other resource types. This section documents the rationale and implementation of each special rule.

12.1 Existing-Only Constraint

Unlike solar, wind, clean firm, and CCS-CCGT, hydro is modeled as existing capacity only with no new-build option. New large-scale hydro construction in the United States faces insurmountable barriers: virtually all viable dam sites are already developed, environmental permitting for new dams requires 10–20 year timelines with uncertain outcomes, and ecological impact concerns (fish migration, river ecosystems) make new projects politically infeasible in most regions.

12.2 Wholesale Pricing (No New-Build Tier)

Because no new hydro capacity is assumed, all hydro generation is priced at the regional wholesale electricity price. There is no new-build LCOE tier for hydro. This reflects the reality of contracting existing hydroelectric output through PPAs or wholesale market purchases.

12.3 Regional Capacity Caps

The optimizer constrains hydro’s share of any resource portfolio to the following regional caps, reflecting each region’s realistic maximum available hydroelectric generation:

RegionHydro Cap (%)Cap (GW)Rationale
CAISO29%30Significant Sierra Nevada hydro resources (Hetch Hetchy, Shasta, Oroville)
ERCOT1%5Very limited hydro in Texas (Sam Rayburn, small run-of-river)
PJM3%15Limited Appalachian hydro (Conowingo, Bath County pumped storage)
NYISO25%40Substantial Niagara Falls and St. Lawrence River hydro
NEISO10%30Moderate New England hydro plus Quebec hydro imports (HQ interconnections)

12.4 Zero Transmission Adder

Hydro carries a $0 transmission adder at all toggle levels. Existing hydro facilities are already interconnected to the grid with established transmission infrastructure. No new transmission is required to deliver their output.

12.5 Optimizer Interaction

The hydro cap constrains the optimizer: the hydro share of any resource mix cannot exceed the regional cap. The remaining portfolio (clean firm + solar + wind + CCS-CCGT) must sum to at least (100% − hydro cap). This ensures the optimizer does not over-rely on hydro in regions where physical hydro capacity is limited.

13. EAC Scarcity Analysis & Clean Premium Curve Methodology

This section documents the analytical framework for assessing Energy Attribute Certificate (EAC) scarcity under hourly matching requirements and deriving clean energy premium curves by ISO region. The analysis extends the optimizer’s cost modeling to the voluntary corporate procurement market, answering: if corporations adopt hourly matching at scale, which ISOs face EAC scarcity and what premium will buyers pay?

The framework assumes 10–25% corporate load participation (realistic near-term range based on current CEBA volumes) at 90–100% hourly matching targets (reflecting GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision and SBTi trajectory). This combination concentrates scarcity in evening and nighttime hours where variable renewables cannot produce, making clean firm resources (nuclear, geothermal, CCS-CCGT) and long-duration storage the binding constraint.

For each ISO, we construct an annual clean energy supply inventory from the following data sources:

Total clean supply (TWh/yr) = Nuclear + Wind + Solar + Hydro + Geothermal. Biomass excluded (RPS eligibility varies by state).

13b. Step 2: Standard Supply Service (SSS) Baseline Computation

The SSS baseline represents the share of clean energy automatically allocated to all consumers before voluntary corporate procurement begins. This follows the GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision’s proposed “standard supply service” methodology:

SSS Clean Share (annual) = Total Regional Clean Generation ÷ Total Regional Load

Example: PJM clean supply ~345 TWh ÷ PJM load ~800 TWh = 43.1% SSS baseline

The SSS baseline is allocated hourly, not annually. Each hour, the SSS contribution equals actual clean generation in that hour divided by total load in that hour. This means SSS is not uniform across the day:

  • Midday (10 AM–3 PM): Highest SSS — solar + wind + nuclear + hydro all producing
  • Evening (5 PM–10 PM): SSS drops sharply as solar sets. Only nuclear + wind + hydro.
  • Night (10 PM–6 AM): Lowest SSS. Primarily nuclear + nighttime wind.

A corporate buyer’s incremental procurement need = (Target hourly match % − SSS baseline %) × hourly load. This incremental need is largest during evening/night hours when the SSS baseline is lowest.

13c. Step 3: State RPS/CES Demand Netting

State mandates create mandatory demand for EACs that is netted out before assessing voluntary market supply. Netting is applied at the state level (RPS compliance is state-jurisdictional), then aggregated to ISO:

  • PJM: NJ (50% by 2030), VA (VCEA 100% by 2045, ~30% by 2030), MD (50% by 2030), IL (CEJA 100% by 2045, ~40% by 2030), PA (8% Tier I). Combined ~120–150 TWh REC demand by 2030. Critical: Most PJM state RPS programs require renewable energy certificates, not generic clean energy. Nuclear does not qualify for standard RPS compliance (with limited exceptions for IL ZEC/CEJA and NJ ZEC programs, which are rolling off or transitioning).
  • ISO-NE: MA (50% Class I by 2030 + CES 80% by 2040), CT (zero-carbon by 2040), RI (100% renewable by 2033), VT (100% by 2035), ME (80% by 2030). Combined ~50–60 TWh.
  • CAISO: SB 100 (60% RPS by 2030, 100% clean by 2045). ~170 TWh RPS demand by 2030.
  • ERCOT: No binding RPS. 0 TWh mandatory demand.
  • NYISO: CLCPA (70% renewable by 2030, 100% zero-emission by 2040). ~110 TWh by 2030.

Uncommitted clean supply = Total clean supply − max(SSS allocation, RPS demand)

We use max(SSS, RPS) because they draw from overlapping pools: an RPS-obligated utility’s clean generation satisfies both its RPS mandate and contributes to the SSS baseline. Each MWh allocated once only.

13d. Step 4: Corporate Hourly Matching Demand Calculation

Corporate demand for hourly EACs is modeled as a function of two parameters:

  • Participation Rate (P): Fraction of total ISO load participating in voluntary hourly matching. Modeled at P = {10%, 15%, 20%, 25%}. Represents the share of C&I load that adopts hourly matching under GHG Protocol Scope 2 or SBTi requirements. Calibration: Current voluntary clean energy procurement (CEBA data) = ~5–8% of load in PJM, ~10–15% in ERCOT. P = 10% is the near-term floor; P = 25% represents ambitious but plausible adoption.
  • Hourly Matching Target (T): Percentage of each hour that participating buyers seek to match. Modeled at T = {90%, 95%, 100%}. These high targets reflect SBTi’s trajectory toward 90%+ matching by 2040 and Google/Microsoft 24/7 CFE commitments.

Hourly demand formula:

Corporate_EAC_demandh = P × Loadh × max(0, T − SSSh)

where h indexes each hour (1–8,760), P is participation rate, T is hourly matching target, Loadh is total ISO load at hour h, and SSSh is the standard supply service clean share at hour h.

Annual corporate EAC demand = Σh Corporate_EAC_demandh

Key insight: At high hourly match targets (90–100%), demand concentrates in evening/night hours where SSS baseline is lowest. A buyer targeting 95% hourly matching in PJM already gets ~43% from SSS during daytime (and ~35% at night), so incremental procurement need is ~52% during day and ~60% at night. The nighttime gap is the binding constraint, and only clean firm or stored energy can fill it.

13e. Step 5: Scarcity Assessment & Classification

For each ISO × participation rate × target combination, scarcity is assessed by comparing corporate EAC demand against uncommitted clean supply on an hourly basis:

  1. Compute hourly surplus/deficit: Surplush = Uncommitted_clean_supplyh − Corporate_EAC_demandh. Positive = surplus (low-cost EACs). Negative = deficit (scarcity pricing).
  2. Count deficit hours: Number of hours/year where Surplush < 0.
  3. Compute deficit volume: Total MWh of unmet hourly demand = Σ |min(0, Surplush)|.
  4. Classify scarcity:

Premium Curve Construction

  1. Sort uncommitted supply by marginal EAC cost (supply stack above).
  2. For each hour, compute corporate EAC demand using Step 4 formula.
  3. Match demand against supply stack, dispatching lowest-cost resources first. Marginal resource (last dispatched) sets the hourly clean premium.
  4. Apply temporal constraints: Solar daytime only. Wind follows regional CF profile. Nuclear/geothermal 24/7. Battery/LDES provide temporal shifting.
  5. Compute weighted-average premium across all hours: Clean Premium = Σ(Premiumh × Volumeh) ÷ ΣVolumeh.
  6. Report daytime vs. nighttime premiums separately to capture structural evening/night premium.

Key driver of premium variation: The clean premium is fundamentally determined by how far up the supply stack demand reaches. In ISOs with abundant uncommitted supply (ERCOT), demand stays at the bottom of the stack (existing renewables at $0–8/MWh). In scarcity ISOs (PJM, ISO-NE), demand exhausts cheap existing resources and reaches new-build clean firm ($25–65/MWh), creating the steep premium. Evening/night premiums are always higher than all-hours averages because daytime surplus solar pulls the average down.

13g. Step 7: Data Center & Large Load Demand Adjustment

Corporate demand is not uniformly distributed. Data centers, the largest single category of voluntary clean energy buyers, are concentrated in specific regions:

  • PJM — Northern Virginia Corridor: ~70% of US data center capacity. Dominion Virginia territory. 10–15 GW announced through 2030 (AWS, Google, Meta, Microsoft). Each GW = ~7–8 TWh/yr at 85% utilization. Existing nuclear PPAs (Constellation Crane restart, Talen Susquehanna) consuming 3–5 GW of nuclear from the uncommitted pool.
  • ERCOT — Texas: Second-largest market. ~5 GW announced through 2030. Direct access to abundant wind/solar without RPS competition. Corporate PPAs among the cheapest in the US.

The scarcity analysis applies a data center demand overlay that reduces uncommitted nuclear supply in PJM by 25–50 TWh (reflecting announced PPAs) before computing the voluntary market balance. This is the primary reason PJM faces scarcity at lower participation rates than other ISOs.

13h. Key Assumptions & Limitations

Assumptions

  • No incrementality constraint — buyers can claim existing clean resources. If incrementality required, scarcity increases dramatically everywhere except ERCOT.
  • ISO-level deliverability — clean energy sourced within buyer’s ISO. No inter-ISO EAC transfer.
  • Static supply (2025) — no new capacity additions modeled. Conservative for ERCOT; optimistic for PJM queue-constrained regions.
  • Flat corporate load — ~90% flat baseload profile. Data centers approximate this well.
  • RPS uses annual compliance — state RECs sourced from any hour; corporate hourly EACs time-matched.
  • PJM RPS = renewable-only — most PJM state RPS mandates require renewable energy (wind, solar, hydro); nuclear does not qualify except under specific state nuclear support programs (IL ZEC/CEJA, NJ ZEC), several of which are transitioning or rolling off by late 2020s.

Limitations

  • Price elasticity not modeled — scarcity would stimulate new supply and reduce demand in reality. Static model overstates scarcity at higher participation rates.
  • Cross-ISO effects ignored — PJM scarcity could drive imports from MISO/SPP or shift load to ERCOT.
  • Battery arbitrage simplified — 4hr Li-ion shifts ~20% of daily solar to evening but cannot address multi-day deficits.
  • GHG Protocol timeline uncertain — Scope 2 revision may not require hourly matching until 2028–2030 at earliest.
  • State policy evolution — RPS targets may increase; federal policy changes could shift landscape significantly.
  • Nuclear ZEC program rolloff — several state nuclear support programs expire in late 2020s, potentially freeing capacity for voluntary market or leading to early retirement.
