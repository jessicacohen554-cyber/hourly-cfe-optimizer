Climate Alignment Assessment

Are These Generators’ Targets Aligned with Climate Science?

Assessing the top 15 generators against SBTi Power Sector v2, IPCC AR6, IEA Net Zero, and U.S. federal targets.

The Climate Target Landscape

Multiple frameworks define what "adequate" power sector decarbonization looks like. They differ in metrics, timelines, and stringency—but all point in the same direction: deep, rapid reduction.

SBTi Power Sector v2

Science-based intensity pathway declining to approximately 138 gCO₂/kWh (~305 lbs/MWh) by 2030 and near-zero by 2040. Uses Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA). Allows CCS as complementary, not substitute for reduction. Requires both near-term (5–10yr) and long-term (2050) targets.11

IPCC AR6 (1.5°C)

Power sector must reach near-zero by 2035–2040 for 1.5°C. Requires 65–80% reduction by 2030 from 2019 levels. Coal phase-out by 2030 in OECD. Unabated gas decline by 2035. CCS has limited role.9

IEA Net Zero by 2050

Advanced economies: net-zero electricity by 2035. Global power intensity to ~130 gCO₂/kWh by 2030, near-zero by 2040. No new unabated coal after 2021. Coal phase-out in advanced economies by 2030. Gas phase-down by 2035.10

U.S. Federal Targets

Biden-era goal: 100% clean electricity by 2035. EPA power plant rules require 90% CCS on new NGCC by 2032 and existing coal by 2032 (face legal challenges). IRA provides $369B in clean energy incentives. Current administration policy status uncertain.12

Key pattern: SBTi commitment and CDP disclosure quality correlate with fleet archetype. Nuclear-led and renewable-heavy generators (Constellation, NextEra, AES, Xcel) tend to have the strongest commitments — they can afford ambition because their fleet already provides a clean foundation. Coal-heavy generators (BH Energy, Evergy, Vistra) are less likely to commit to SBTi targets, reflecting the greater economic challenge of their starting position.

The Metrics Debate

Intensity vs. Absolute Targets: Which Metric Matters More?

Intensity targets (gCO₂/kWh or lbs/MWh) measure carbon per unit of output. They're useful for comparing efficiency across different-sized generators. SBTi v2 uses intensity as its primary metric for the power sector.

Absolute targets (MT CO₂/year) measure total climate impact. A company can improve intensity while increasing absolute emissions if generation grows faster than efficiency improves.

The load growth complication: With electricity demand surging from data centers, EVs, and industrial electrification, many generators are growing their output significantly. Constellation's load is projected to grow 15–20% by 2030. If generation grows 20% but intensity stays flat, absolute emissions increase 20%. Intensity targets alone don't guarantee climate benefit in a growing market.

The analysis recommends both metrics simultaneously: intensity targets to drive efficiency, absolute caps to ensure genuine emissions reduction. This is consistent with SBTi v2's approach, which requires companies to set both near-term intensity and long-term absolute targets.
