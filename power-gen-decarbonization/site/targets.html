The Climate Target Landscape

Multiple frameworks define what "adequate" power sector decarbonization looks like. They differ in metrics, timelines, and stringency—but all point in the same direction: deep, rapid reduction.

SBTi Power Sector

Uses Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA) — company-specific intensity pathways converging to ~0 gCO₂/kWh. Requires ~85% intensity reduction by 2035 from base year. Power sector net-zero deadline is 2040 (a decade ahead of other sectors). Carbon offsets cannot count toward targets. CCS at the facility level may count, but the framework does not validate specific technologies. New draft standard (Sep 2025) expands scope to entire value chain and shifts from “renewable” to “zero-carbon” (includes nuclear).11

IPCC AR6 (1.5°C)

Power sector CO₂ must fall ~48% by 2030 and ~99% by 2050 vs 2019 (1.5°C, no/limited overshoot). Climate Action Tracker benchmark: 48–80 gCO₂/kWh by 2030, <5 by 2040. Developed countries reach net-zero electricity by 2035. Coal without CCS reduced 67–82% by 2030. Low-carbon share of electricity reaches 93–97% by 2050. BECCS enables net-negative emissions in some scenarios.9

IEA Net Zero by 2050

Power sector emissions decline ~60% by 2030 from 2019. OECD achieves 100% clean power by 2035. Global power sector reaches net-zero by 2040 — the only sector to do so a full decade before economy-wide. Intensity falls from ~460 gCO₂/kWh (2020) to ~140–170 by 2030, near-zero by 2040. 2025 update acknowledges slower near-term progress; temperature peaks ~1.65°C around 2050.10

U.S. Federal Targets

Biden-era 100% clean electricity by 2035 goal is effectively dead. EPA proposed full repeal (June 2025) of all power plant GHG standards under Section 111, including Obama- and Biden-era rules. No binding federal GHG target currently exists. The IRA ($369B clean energy incentives) remains law and is projected to drive 60–83% reduction below 2005 levels by 2040 through economics alone. State-level standards (CA, NY, etc.) continue independently.12

Climate Action 100+

World’s largest investor engagement initiative ($60T+ AUM). Net-zero in generation by 2040 globally, 2035 in advanced economies. 50%+ decarbonization by 2030. Explicitly says avoid carbon offsets and minimize CCUS reliance. No CA100+ power utility with coal capacity is currently Paris-aligned (Carbon Tracker).13

24/7 Carbon-Free Energy

Emerging standard pioneered by Google/Microsoft. Goes beyond annual RECs to require hourly matching of consumption with clean generation. Directly relevant to utility-scale procurement. This is the methodology used in our hourly CFE optimizer sister project.14

The Trajectory Question

Same Percentage, Wildly Different Costs: The SBTi Equity Problem

SBTi’s Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA) requires all power generators to achieve approximately the same percentage reduction from their baseline intensity — roughly 85% by 2035. This creates a fundamental equity problem: the same percentage reduction costs radically different amounts depending on fleet composition.

The cost asymmetry: A coal-heavy generator at ~500 kg CO₂/MWh needs an 85% reduction to ~75 kg/MWh. Its path: replace coal with wind at $25–35/MWh, which often undercuts coal operating costs. Marginal abatement cost: ~$17–28/tonne CO₂. Economically rational even without a carbon price.

A nuclear-led generator at ~180 kg/MWh also needs an ~85% reduction to ~27 kg/MWh. Its remaining emissions come from efficient gas CCGTs — the cheapest-to-operate fossil plants in the fleet. Replacing them requires CCS ($90–130/MWh), new nuclear ($120–180/MWh), or massive renewables+LDES overbuild. Marginal abatement cost: ~$88–165/tonne CO₂. Requires either a significant carbon price or extraordinary corporate demand-side pull.

The SDA framework doesn’t differentiate based on where a generator sits on the abatement cost curve. A coal-heavy generator achieves 85% reduction cheaply by harvesting low-hanging fruit; an already-clean generator must spend 5–10× more per tonne to achieve the same percentage because the remaining emissions are harder and more expensive to abate.

This is the core tension of percentage-based targets: Generators that decarbonized proactively face a steeper marginal abatement curve for their remaining emissions than those that delayed action. The SDA framework was designed for economy-wide fairness (higher-emitting sectors do more), but within the power sector, it creates asymmetric outcomes where the cleanest generators face the most expensive per-tonne reductions. EPRI’s SMARTargets initiative attempts to address this by incorporating company-specific circumstances, regional constraints, and multiple objectives (affordability, reliability, equity) into target-setting — though critics argue this flexibility risks diluting climate ambition.

Alternative approaches: An absolute intensity threshold (everyone must reach X kg/MWh by year Y) would treat already-clean generators more favorably. SBTi’s v2 draft includes convergence elements, but the near-term pathway still relies on percentage reductions. EPRI’s SMARTargets methodology proposes company-specific pathways grounded in scenario analysis, though Ceres and other investor groups have raised concerns that this could undermine sector-specific accountability. The debate reflects a genuine tension between rigorous, uniform climate targets and practical, differentiated pathways.

The Metrics Debate

Intensity vs. Absolute Targets: Why Both Metrics Matter

Intensity targets (gCO₂/kWh or kg/MWh) measure carbon per unit of output. SBTi v2 uses intensity as its primary metric for the power sector. But intensity can mask growing absolute emissions.

Absolute targets (Mt CO₂/year) measure total climate impact. A company can improve intensity while increasing absolute emissions if generation grows faster than efficiency improves.

The load growth complication: With electricity demand surging from data centers, EVs, and industrial electrification, many generators are growing their output significantly. Some nuclear-led and gas-heavy generators project 15–20% load growth by 2030 driven by data center contracts. If generation grows 20% but intensity stays flat, absolute emissions increase 20%. Intensity targets alone don’t guarantee climate benefit in a growing market.

The analysis recommends both metrics simultaneously: intensity targets to drive efficiency, absolute caps to ensure genuine emissions reduction. This is consistent with SBTi v2’s approach, which requires companies to set both near-term intensity and long-term absolute targets.

Competing Frameworks

EPRI SMARTargets vs. SBTi: The Battle Over How to Set Utility Climate Targets

The power sector is now caught between two fundamentally different philosophies for setting climate targets. SBTi’s top-down, sector-wide approach faces a challenge from EPRI’s bottom-up, company-specific SMARTargets methodology. The outcome of this competition will shape how the 15 largest generators are evaluated for the next decade.

SBTi: Uniform Standards, Sector-Wide Accountability

SBTi’s Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA) derives company-level intensity pathways from global carbon budgets. Every power generator converges toward the same endpoint (<5 gCO₂/kWh by 2040). The Power Sector v2 draft (September 2025) introduces several changes:

  • Shifts from “renewable energy” to “zero-carbon energy” — nuclear now counts explicitly
  • Phased hourly matching requirement: 50% by 2030, 90% by 2040 for qualifying companies (≥10 GWh in a single region)
  • Expanded scope to cover the full value chain (Scope 1 + 2 + 3)
  • Offsets cannot count toward near-term targets; CCS at the facility level may count if verified

Strength: Universal, comparable, investor-trusted. One framework, one yardstick. Prevents cherry-picking favorable metrics.

Weakness: The SDA’s percentage-reduction approach creates the cost asymmetry documented above — already-clean generators face 5–10× higher per-tonne costs than coal-heavy ones.

EPRI SMARTargets: Company-Specific Pathways

EPRI’s Sustainability Metrics, Alignment, and Reporting Targets initiative, announced at COP28 (December 2023) and published as a 107-page draft framework in July 2025, proposes a fundamentally different approach:

Aspirational Targets (ATs)

Reference trajectories grounded in least-cost integrated energy modeling (NEMS, ReEDS, GenX). Not company-specific but scenario-specific. Cover the “what would it take?” question across a range of policy, technology, and demand scenarios. Each AT includes resource mix, cost, and reliability implications.

Qualified Targets (QTs)

Company-specific targets that account for the utility’s actual service territory, existing fleet, regulatory environment, resource availability, and customer affordability constraints. A QT may be less aggressive than an AT if local constraints make faster decarbonization impractical or unaffordable. Requires disclosure of gap between AT and QT with justification.

The credibility debate: SMARTargets has drawn sharp criticism from institutional investors and NGOs. Ceres (representing $45T+ in AUM) has publicly cautioned that “industry-led frameworks risk weakening climate ambition” and could provide “cover for delayed action.” NYC Comptroller Brad Lander (overseeing $250B+ in pension funds) warned that SMARTargets could enable “greenwashing at scale.” EPRI counters that SBTi’s one-size-fits-all approach ignores the practical reality that a utility in the Southeast with weak renewable resources and coal-dependent communities faces fundamentally different constraints than a Pacific Northwest hydro utility.

What This Means for the 15 Generators

Generators that benefit from SMARTargets:

  • Nuclear-led generators — QTs would recognize that their remaining emissions are from efficient gas CCGTs with high marginal abatement costs
  • Regulated utilities in conservative states (Southern, Entergy, DTE) — QTs can incorporate rate impact constraints and regulatory approval timelines
  • Generators with reliability obligations — QTs can account for resource adequacy mandates that prevent premature fossil retirement

Risk of SMARTargets without guardrails:

  • Delay justification — companies can cite “local constraints” to set weaker QTs indefinitely
  • Comparability loss — investors can’t compare generators if each has a bespoke pathway
  • Accountability gap — no external validation body equivalent to SBTi’s review process
  • Lobbying incentive — utilities may advocate for weak regulation knowing SMARTargets will accept it as a “constraint”

The resolution may be “both/and”: The most credible approach combines SBTi’s sector-level accountability with EPRI’s company-specific realism. A generator would commit to SBTi’s sector trajectory and disclose a QT-style pathway showing how it plans to get there given real-world constraints. The gap between the sector trajectory and the company pathway becomes a transparency metric — visible to investors, regulators, and the public. Neither framework alone captures both “what science requires” and “what this company can deliver.”

Demand-Side Accounting

GHG Protocol Scope 2 Revision: Hourly Matching Changes Everything

The GHG Protocol’s Scope 2 revision — upgrading from “Guidance” to binding “Standard” — may be the single most consequential development for power generator economics. The first consultation ran October 2025 through January 2026, with final publication expected in late 2027. The proposed changes would fundamentally restructure how corporate clean energy claims are verified.

Four Quality Criteria That Reshape Clean Energy Markets

1. Temporal Matching

EACs must match consumption on an hourly basis. Annual matching — buying cheap daytime solar RECs to “cover” nighttime fossil consumption — will no longer be sufficient for credible Scope 2 claims. This creates massive new demand for generation that produces during evenings, nights, and low-wind periods: nuclear, geothermal, CCS-CCGT, and storage-backed renewables.

2. Deliverability

Clean energy claims must be sourced from the same ISO/RTO region where load is located. A data center in PJM cannot claim credit for wind generation in ERCOT. This creates region-specific EAC markets with vastly different supply-demand dynamics — some ISOs face potential EAC scarcity while others have abundant surplus.

3. Incrementality

Under discussion: whether EACs should require proof that the clean generation is additional (would not have been built without the buyer’s procurement). This is the most contentious quality criterion. If adopted, it would prevent buyers from claiming existing nuclear or hydropower and require contracts for new-build clean resources only — dramatically increasing costs and deployment timelines.

4. Standard Supply Service (SSS)

The most innovative proposal: allocate existing clean resources (state nuclear programs, RPS-driven renewables, publicly owned hydro, rate-base clean generation) pro-rata to all consumers as a baseline “standard supply service.” Corporate buyers then only need to procure incremental clean energy above this baseline to make additional hourly matching claims. This avoids double-counting and ensures existing clean assets benefit everyone.

How SSS + Hourly Matching Affects Generator Economics

The Standard Supply Service concept transforms how we think about EAC markets. Instead of every MWh of clean energy being “available” for corporate claims, existing clean resources are allocated to the entire grid first. Only the surplus above socialized baseline allocation enters the voluntary market.

Example — PJM: PJM generates ~800 TWh/year. Existing clean resources (nuclear ~275 TWh, wind ~35 TWh, solar ~25 TWh, hydro ~10 TWh) total ~345 TWh, giving a standard supply service baseline of ~43% clean. Every consumer in PJM gets credited with 43% hourly-matched clean energy automatically. A corporate buyer targeting 90% hourly matching only needs to procure the incremental 47 percentage points — but those incremental MWh must come from generation that produces during the specific hours when the SSS baseline doesn’t cover their load.

Implications for Generator Target-Setting

If the GHG Protocol adopts hourly matching with SSS baselines, the demand signal for generator decarbonization shifts dramatically:

  • Nuclear generators see their existing fleet valued not just for energy but for its hourly clean attribute — producing 24/7 clean energy that anchors the SSS baseline. Nuclear uprates and license extensions become valuable not just for energy revenue but for growing the hourly EAC supply during night/winter hours when other clean sources are scarce.
  • Coal-heavy generators face dual pressure: (1) their fossil generation becomes a liability as corporate buyers avoid regions with low SSS baselines, and (2) replacing coal with wind/solar alone won’t satisfy hourly demand during evening/night hours — they need storage or clean firm too.
  • Gas-dominant generators face the most interesting calculus: CCS-CCGT could convert a liability (unmatched fossil hours) into an asset (hourly-matched clean firm that earns premium EAC revenue during the hours corporate buyers need most).

The hourly premium: Under annual accounting, a REC is a REC — daytime solar and nighttime nuclear are fungible. Under hourly accounting, nighttime clean energy commands a structural premium because supply is scarce and corporate demand is concentrated during these deficit hours. Our hourly CFE optimizer quantifies this premium: at 90%+ hourly matching targets, clean firm generation during evening/night hours can be worth 2–5× the daytime solar EAC price because it fills the hardest-to-match hours. This premium is the market signal that makes CCS, new nuclear, and LDES investable.

Policy Frontier

Hourly RPS: Could State Policy Catalyze Generator Deep Decarbonization?

Today, all 29 state RPS/CES policies use annual compliance — utilities procure enough RECs over 12 months to meet their percentage target. This allows temporal arbitrage: cheap daytime solar RECs satisfy the mandate even when the grid runs on gas at night. An hourly RPS would eliminate this loophole and create fundamentally different incentives for generator investment.

How an Hourly RPS Would Work

Instead of demonstrating that X% of annual retail sales were matched with RECs, utilities would demonstrate that X% of every hour’s retail sales were matched with clean generation produced during that hour within the same ISO. This creates:

  • Evening/night clean energy demand — utilities can’t satisfy requirements with daytime solar alone
  • Storage value — battery and LDES become compliance tools, shifting solar/wind generation to deficit hours
  • Clean firm premium — nuclear, geothermal, and CCS-CCGT earn premium value for reliable hourly production
  • Regional price signals — hours with low clean supply see high compliance costs, directing investment where it’s most needed

The deep decarb catalyst: Annual accounting lets generators and states declare “80% clean” while running gas at night for half the hours of the year. Hourly accounting exposes this gap and creates a market signal for the specific technologies — clean firm, LDES, CCS — needed to get from 80% to 95%+. No state has adopted hourly RPS yet, but California’s SB 100 implementation discussions, Illinois’s CEJA implementation, and New York’s Climate Act proceedings are all considering hourly elements. An hourly RPS in even one major state would catalyze technology deployment and cost reduction that benefits all regions.

Convergence: Hourly RPS + GHG Protocol + SBTi

The three frameworks are converging on the same conclusion: annual clean energy accounting masks real emissions and fails to drive the last-mile investments needed for deep decarbonization. If state hourly RPS mandates, GHG Protocol Scope 2 hourly requirements, and SBTi phased hourly matching all take effect by 2030, generators face a unified market signal:
