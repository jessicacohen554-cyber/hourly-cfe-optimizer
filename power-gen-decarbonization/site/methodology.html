Methodology & Sources

How we built this analysis: data sources, processing methods, scenario modeling approach, and limitations.

1. Analytical Approach

This analysis examines the 15 largest U.S. power generators by annual electricity generation (TWh) to assess the feasibility, cost, and enabling conditions for their decarbonization. We combine plant-level emissions data, corporate disclosures, clean energy cost modeling, and climate science frameworks to evaluate whether stated and implied targets are achievable.

Core principle: We co-optimize affordability and decarbonization. The analysis does not assume unlimited capital or regulatory mandate — it asks what pathways are simultaneously ambitious, feasible, and affordable given each generator's actual fleet composition and market position.

The analysis draws on a sister project — the Hourly CFE Optimizer — which models the cost of achieving various levels of 24/7 carbon-free energy matching across five major U.S. ISOs (CAISO, ERCOT, PJM, NYISO, ISO-NE). Cost curves and optimal resource mixes from that optimizer directly inform the pathway feasibility assessments here.7

2. Data Sources

2.1 Emissions & Generation Data

EPA eGRID 2023 (Emissions & Generation Resource Integrated Database) is the primary source for plant-level emissions, generation, and fuel type data. eGRID2023 was released January 15, 2025 and contains 2023 calendar year data. EPA eGRID1

eGRID provides data at the unit, generator, plant, state, balancing authority, eGRID subregion, NERC region, and US levels. Importantly, eGRID discontinued parent company-level aggregation after the 2009 data year. We reconstruct parent company totals by aggregating plant-level data using operator name matching and corporate structure research.1

EIA Form 923 provides monthly generation by plant and fuel type. EIA Form 860 provides plant characteristics, ownership, and nameplate capacity. Both supplement eGRID for cases where plant operator names require disambiguation. EIA-923, EIA-8602

2.2 Corporate Data

Company 10-K Annual Reports (SEC EDGAR) provide generation statistics, fleet capacity, and operating data directly from corporate filings. We cross-reference eGRID plant-level aggregations against 10-K reported figures to validate our parent company mapping.3

MJ Bradley / ERM Benchmarking Air Emissions Report provides annual rankings of approximately 100 U.S. power producers by CO₂, NOₓ, SO₂, and mercury emissions. The report uses EPA Continuous Emissions Monitoring System (CEMS) data and EIA generation data. This analysis complements our eGRID-based aggregation with an independent benchmark. MJ Bradley/ERM4

CDP (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project) provides corporate climate disclosures including Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions, targets, and governance. We reference CDP scores and self-reported emissions for the companies that participate. CDP16

Company sustainability reports provide stated decarbonization targets, strategy details, retirement schedules, and progress metrics. We cite specific reports in the company profiles.17

2.3 Cost & Technology Data

Lazard Levelized Cost of Energy Analysis (v17.0, 2024) provides LCOE ranges for new generation technologies. We use unsubsidized and subsidized LCOE for comparisons. Lazard LCOE+5

NREL Annual Technology Baseline (ATB) 2024 provides technology cost and performance projections through 2050. We use ATB mid-case projections for scenario modeling. NREL ATB6

Hourly CFE Optimizer (sister project) provides regionally-specific cost curves for achieving 75–100% hourly carbon-free energy matching, including optimal resource mixes (solar, wind, nuclear, CCS-CCGT, battery, LDES) and marginal abatement costs. The optimizer uses 2025 cost data with Low/Medium/High sensitivity toggles. 7

LBNL Utility-Scale Solar & Wind Reports provide empirical cost and performance data for deployed projects. LBNL Solar, LBNL Wind8

2.4 Climate Target Frameworks

IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (AR6), Working Group III (2022) provides the scientific basis for power sector decarbonization timelines. Chapter 6 specifically addresses energy systems and includes pathways for 1.5°C and well-below 2°C scenarios. IPCC AR6 WG39

IEA Net Zero by 2050 Roadmap (2023 update) establishes the Net Zero Emissions (NZE) scenario milestones for the power sector: net-zero electricity for advanced economies by 2035, global by 2040. IEA NZE10

SBTi Power Sector Guidance v2 (in development) establishes science-based targets for power generators using a sectoral decarbonization approach. We reference the draft methodology including intensity pathway requirements. SBTi Power11

U.S. Federal policy: IRA tax credit provisions (45Y, 48E, 45Q), EPA Clean Power Plan 2.0 / power plant GHG rules, and state-level RPS/CES mandates. IRA12

2.5 Grid & Market Research

NREL 100% Clean Electricity by 2035 Study (2022) — Examines pathways and costs for fully decarbonizing the U.S. power sector. NREL 100%18

Princeton Net-Zero America (2021) — Comprehensive modeling of U.S. economy-wide net-zero pathways including detailed power sector analysis. NZA19

Rhodium Group U.S. Climate & Power Sector Trackers — Real-time tracking of U.S. power sector emissions and clean energy deployment. Rhodium20

NERC Reliability Assessments — Long-term and seasonal reliability assessments identifying resource adequacy risks during the clean energy transition. NERC21

3. Parent Company Aggregation

Since eGRID 2023 does not provide parent company-level totals, we aggregate plant-level data using the following approach:

  1. Operator name matching: eGRID plant operator names (OPRNAME field) are matched to the 15 target parent companies using substring matching with a curated mapping table. Example: "Georgia Power" → Southern Company, "Luminant" → Vistra Energy.
  2. Corporate structure validation: Matches are validated against SEC filings, S&P Global corporate trees, and EIA-860 ownership data to ensure accuracy, especially for recently merged/acquired entities.
  3. Constellation + Calpine treatment: Constellation Energy completed its acquisition of Calpine Corporation in January 2026. We treat the combined entity as one company, aggregating both Constellation and Calpine plant data.22
  4. Metric calculation: For each parent company, we sum annual net generation (MWh), annual CO₂ emissions (short tons), and compute fleet-weighted average emissions intensity (lbs CO₂/MWh = total CO₂ tons × 2000 / total MWh).
  5. Fuel mix: Plant-level generation by primary fuel category is summed and converted to percentage shares.

Limitation: Operator name matching may miss plants where the eGRID operator name differs from the expected corporate name (e.g., legacy names from pre-merger entities). We cross-reference against 10-K reported generation totals and flag discrepancies >5%. The research-based profiles in this initial analysis use best estimates from company reports pending full eGRID plant-level processing.

4. Scenario Analysis Framework

For each generator, we model four decarbonization scenarios:

Scenario A: Business as Usual

Current approved IRPs, announced retirements, and planned additions. Existing tax credits. No carbon price. Reflects what companies are already committed to doing.

Scenario B: Policy-Enabled

Full IRA tax credit utilization, potential carbon price ($40–60/ton), accelerated coal retirement where economic, enhanced renewable buildout. Reflects what's achievable with supportive but realistic policy.

Scenario C: SBTi-Aligned

Meets SBTi Power Sector v2 intensity pathway. Identifies the resource additions, retirements, and costs needed to achieve science-based targets on each company's specific timeline.

Scenario D: IEA NZE / 1.5°C

Net-zero electricity by 2035. Maximum ambition scenario. Identifies what's physically impossible vs. merely expensive for each fleet type. Uses CFE optimizer cost curves for the "last mile."7,10

Cost Modeling Approach

Scenario costs are estimated using:

  • LCOE of replacement technologies (Lazard 2024, NREL ATB 2024)5,6
  • Regional cost adjustments from CFE optimizer sensitivity analysis7
  • IRA tax credit impacts on project economics12
  • Stranded asset exposure using remaining book value and depreciation schedules
  • Transmission and interconnection cost adders where applicable14

5. Limitations & Caveats

  • Snapshot analysis: This uses 2023 data as the base year. Fleet changes in 2024–2025 (retirements, additions) are noted where known but not fully captured in eGRID.
  • Scope 1 only: We focus on direct (Scope 1) CO₂ emissions from electricity generation. Upstream methane emissions (Scope 3), purchased power emissions (Scope 2), and lifecycle emissions are not included in the primary analysis.
  • No demand-side modeling: We do not model changes in electricity demand, load growth from electrification, or demand response. Load growth could significantly affect both total emissions and the feasibility of decarbonization pathways.
  • Simplified cost modeling: LCOE comparisons do not fully capture system integration costs, capacity value differences, or locational pricing. The CFE optimizer provides more rigorous system cost analysis for the 5 ISOs it covers.
  • Regulatory uncertainty: Federal and state policy environments are subject to change. IRA tax credit availability, EPA regulations, and state RPS mandates may be modified, expanded, or repealed.
  • Corporate structure changes: Mergers, spin-offs, and divestitures can change company rankings and fleet composition. The Constellation-Calpine merger (Jan 2026) is the most recent major change reflected here.22
