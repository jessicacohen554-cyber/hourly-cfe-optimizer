Four Archetypes

Every Generator Falls Into One of Four Fleet Archetypes

The decarbonization pathway for a power generator is fundamentally determined by its starting fleet composition. We identify four distinct archetypes among the top 15 generators, each with different economics, opportunities, and constraints.

Coal-Heavy

Evergy, AEP, BH Energy, Vistra, PPL

Coal ≥25% of generation. Highest current emissions intensity (454–572 kg/MWh). But coal retirement is the cheapest form of abatement: new renewables now undercut coal operating costs in most markets.5

Gas-Dominant

Entergy, Dominion

$80–150

Diversified / Transitioning

Duke, Southern, Xcel, DTE, WEC, AES

Mix of gas, coal, nuclear, and growing renewables. These generators have the most flexible pathway options: retire remaining coal first (cheap), then build renewables, then address gas (harder).

$25–80
$/tonne CO₂ abatement (blended pathway)

Nuclear-Led / Renewable-Led

Constellation, NextEra

Already below US average intensity. Nuclear or renewable backbone. Challenge is not reducing—it's maintaining low-carbon status while growing to meet surging demand (data centers, electrification).

$120+
$/tonne CO₂ for marginal reductions

The Paradox

Why the Dirtiest Generators May Have the Easiest Economics

Consider two generators trying to reduce emissions by 50%:

Evergy (Coal-Heavy): 519 → 260 kg/MWh

  • Strategy: Replace coal fleet with Kansas wind + battery storage
  • Cost: New wind at $25–35/MWh undercuts coal operating costs ($35–50/MWh)
  • Abatement: ~$15–25/tonne CO₂
  • Barrier: Coal community transition, stranded assets
  • Net bill impact: Potentially negative (lower system costs)

Verdict: 50% reduction is economically rational even without a carbon price. IRA credits make it a no-brainer.

Constellation (Nuclear-Led): 177 → 88 kg/MWh

  • Strategy: Must decarbonize ~130 TWh of gas CCGT generation
  • Options: CCS on gas ($90–130/MWh), new renewables+LDES ($80–120/MWh for last-mile matching)
  • Abatement: ~$80–150/tonne CO₂
  • Barrier: CCS at scale unproven; LDES still expensive
  • Net bill impact: Significant cost increase

Verdict: 50% reduction requires either CCS breakthrough, massive storage deployment, or carbon price >$80/tonne to be economic.

Regional Dimension

Geography Is Destiny: How Region Shapes the Decarbonization Playbook

The hourly CFE optimizer shows dramatic regional variation in decarbonization costs. A generator's location in the grid determines access to solar and wind resources, transmission capacity, wholesale market structure, and state policy environment.7

ERCOT (Texas)

Generators: Constellation, Vistra, AEP (partial)

Excellent solar+wind resources. Competitive wholesale market. No capacity market (energy-only). Battery+solar economics are strongest here. But: reliability concerns post-Winter Storm Uri drive gas retention.

PJM (Mid-Atlantic / Midwest)

Generators: Constellation, AEP, Dominion, PPL, Vistra

Largest US wholesale market. Nuclear backbone (Constellation). Capacity market values reliability. Coal-to-gas-to-clean transition underway. But: interconnection queue is massive (800+ GW waiting).

Southeast (SERC, non-ISO)

Generators: Duke, Southern, Entergy, Dominion (partial)

Vertically integrated, regulated monopolies. No wholesale market competition. IRP-driven planning. Excellent solar resource. But: vertically integrated structure means ratepayer impact is direct and politically sensitive.

MISO / SPP (Central US)

Generators: Xcel, Evergy, DTE, WEC, Entergy (partial), AES (Indiana)

World-class wind resource (Great Plains). Growing solar. Strong utility RPS compliance activity. But: transmission constraints limit exports from wind-rich regions to load centers. Coal retirement pace varies by state politics.

Western US (WECC)

Generators: BH Energy (PacifiCorp, NV Energy)

Exceptional solar and wind. Emerging Western energy market (EDAM). Aggressive state clean energy mandates (CA, OR, WA). But: PacifiCorp has massive legacy coal + wildfire liability. Transmission across the West is constrained.

CAISO (California)

Generators: Constellation (Geysers + gas), NextEra, AES

Most aggressive clean energy policy in the nation. SB 100 mandates 100% clean by 2045. Excellent solar but evening ramp challenge. But: natural gas still critical for reliability. Constellation’s Geysers geothermal is a clean firm anchor.

Optimizer Insights

Three Decarbonization Strategies: What the Hourly CFE Optimizer Reveals

The hourly CFE optimizer — our sister project modeling 24/7 clean energy matching across five US ISOs — identifies three distinct strategy zones. Each applies differently depending on a generator’s fleet archetype and regional market.7

Strategy 1: Renewable-First

75–85%
Clean Energy Target Zone

Solar + wind + 4-hour battery storage. Cost premium: $10–25/MWh above fossil baseline. Economic even without carbon pricing in most regions. This is the sweet spot for coal-heavy generators: replace coal with renewables+storage and system costs may actually decrease.

Best for: Evergy, AEP, BH Energy, Vistra, PPL — coal retirement + renewable build

Strategy 2: Nuclear-Plus

85–95%
Clean Energy Target Zone

Renewables + nuclear life-extension + CCS on select gas plants. Cost premium: $25–50/MWh. Clean firm (nuclear, geothermal, CCS-CCGT) becomes essential for reliability during low renewable hours. Economic with IRA tax credits and modest carbon pricing ($40–60/ton).

Best for: Duke, Southern, Dominion, Xcel, DTE, WEC — diversified fleets with nuclear anchors

Strategy 3: Regional Portfolio

95–100%
Clean Energy Target Zone

Full portfolio: renewables + nuclear + CCS + LDES (iron-air) + DAC offsets. Cost premium: $50–78/MWh. The last 5% costs as much as the first 50%. Long-duration storage and clean firm are non-negotiable at this level. Requires carbon pricing >$100/tonne or strong mandates.

The challenge for: Constellation, NextEra, Entergy — already-clean fleets pursuing the expensive last mile

Key Findings from Fleet Analysis

1. Coal Retirement Is Low-Hanging Fruit

New solar+wind+storage is now cheaper than operating existing coal in most markets. The 5 coal-heavy generators (Evergy, AEP, BHE, Vistra, PPL) account for ~211 Mt CO₂/year. Retiring their coal fleets could reduce total emissions by ~100+ MT CO₂/year at negative or minimal cost.5,8

2. Gas Is the Next Frontier — and Much Harder

After coal, natural gas is the dominant emitter (~45% of generation across these 15). But gas plants are more efficient, more flexible, and more critical for reliability. Decarbonizing gas requires CCS, clean firm alternatives, or very high storage deployment — all at 3–5x the $/tonne of coal retirement.6,7

3. Nuclear Is the Cheapest Clean MWh

Existing nuclear plants produce electricity at $25–35/MWh — cheaper than any new clean generation source. Extending nuclear life and pursuing uprates is the single most cost-effective decarbonization strategy. Constellation’s 23 GW nuclear fleet is an extraordinary clean energy asset.5,6

4. The Last 10–20% Is Exponentially Expensive

The CFE optimizer shows that going from 90% to 100% clean energy doubles or triples system cost in most regions. This last-mile problem is where long-duration storage, CCS, or new nuclear become necessary — and where policy support is most critical. Going from 99% to 100% alone costs an additional $18–31/MWh for just 1% more clean energy.7

5. Storage Alone Can’t Solve the Last Mile

Even at 100% clean energy, battery and LDES storage accounts for less than 10% of total system cost in the optimizer. The bottleneck isn’t storage — it’s the availability and cost of clean firm generation (nuclear, geothermal, CCS-CCGT) to fill multi-day low-renewable periods.7

6. Regional Variation Is Massive

The cost of achieving 95% clean energy varies by 2–3x across regions. ERCOT and CAISO benefit from excellent solar resources. PJM and ISO-NE face higher costs due to lower solar capacity factors and greater seasonal variability. Generators’ regional exposure directly determines their decarbonization economics.7
