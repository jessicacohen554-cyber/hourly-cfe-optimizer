The Fleet Composition Paradox

How Fleet Composition Determines Decarbonization Pathway

Coal-heavy generators face different economics than efficient gas+nuclear fleets. Understanding this paradox is essential for realistic target-setting and policy design.

Four Archetypes

Every Generator Falls Into One of Four Fleet Archetypes

The decarbonization pathway for a power generator is fundamentally determined by its starting fleet composition. We identify four distinct archetypes among the top 15 generators, each with different economics, opportunities, and constraints.

Coal-Heavy

Evergy, AEP, BH Energy, Vistra, PPL

Coal ≥25% of generation. Highest current emissions intensity (1,000–1,260 lbs/MWh). But coal retirement is the cheapest form of abatement: new renewables now undercut coal operating costs in most markets.5

Gas-Dominant

Entergy, Dominion

Gas ≥45% with nuclear. Moderate intensity (735–770 lbs/MWh). Already past the easy wins of coal retirement. Reducing gas emissions requires CCS, clean firm, or massive renewable overbuild—all more expensive per ton.6

$80–150
$/ton CO₂ abatement (gas → clean)

Diversified / Transitioning

Duke, Southern, Xcel, DTE, WEC, AES

Mix of gas, coal, nuclear, and growing renewables. These generators have the most flexible pathway options: retire remaining coal first (cheap), then build renewables, then address gas (harder).

$25–80
$/ton CO₂ abatement (blended pathway)

Nuclear-Led / Renewable-Led

Constellation, NextEra

Already below US average intensity. Nuclear or renewable backbone. Challenge is not reducing—it's maintaining low-carbon status while growing to meet surging demand (data centers, electrification).

$120+
$/ton CO₂ for marginal reductions
The Paradox

Why the Dirtiest Generators May Have the Easiest Economics

Consider two generators trying to reduce emissions by 50%:

Evergy (Coal-Heavy): 1,145 → 573 lbs/MWh

  • Strategy: Replace coal fleet with Kansas wind + battery storage
  • Cost: New wind at $25–35/MWh undercuts coal operating costs ($35–50/MWh)
  • Abatement: ~$15–25/ton CO₂
  • Barrier: Coal community transition, stranded assets
  • Net bill impact: Potentially negative (lower system costs)

Verdict: 50% reduction is economically rational even without a carbon price. IRA credits make it a no-brainer.

Constellation (Nuclear-Led): 390 → 195 lbs/MWh

  • Strategy: Must decarbonize ~130 TWh of gas generation (from Calpine fleet)
  • Options: CCS on gas ($90–130/MWh), new renewables+LDES ($80–120/MWh for last-mile matching)
  • Abatement: ~$80–150/ton CO₂
  • Barrier: CCS at scale unproven; LDES still expensive
  • Net bill impact: Significant cost increase

Verdict: 50% reduction requires either CCS breakthrough, massive storage deployment, or carbon price >$80/ton to be economic.

Regional Dimension

Geography Is Destiny: How Region Shapes the Decarbonization Playbook

The hourly CFE optimizer shows dramatic regional variation in decarbonization costs. A generator's location in the grid determines access to solar and wind resources, transmission capacity, wholesale market structure, and state policy environment.7

ERCOT (Texas)

Generators: Constellation/Calpine, Vistra, AEP (partial)

Excellent solar+wind resources. Competitive wholesale market. No capacity market (energy-only). Battery+solar economics are strongest here. But: reliability concerns post-Winter Storm Uri drive gas retention.

PJM (Mid-Atlantic / Midwest)

Generators: Constellation, AEP, Dominion, PPL, Vistra

Largest US wholesale market. Nuclear backbone (Constellation). Capacity market values reliability. Coal-to-gas-to-clean transition underway. But: interconnection queue is massive (800+ GW waiting).

Southeast (SERC, non-ISO)

Generators: Duke, Southern, Entergy, Dominion (partial)

Vertically integrated, regulated monopolies. No wholesale market competition. IRP-driven planning. Excellent solar resource. But: vertically integrated structure means ratepayer impact is direct and politically sensitive.

MISO / SPP (Central US)

Generators: Xcel, Evergy, DTE, WEC, Entergy (partial), AES (Indiana)

World-class wind resource (Great Plains). Growing solar. Strong utility RPS compliance activity. But: transmission constraints limit exports from wind-rich regions to load centers. Coal retirement pace varies by state politics.

Western US (WECC)

Generators: BH Energy (PacifiCorp, NV Energy)

Exceptional solar and wind. Emerging Western energy market (EDAM). Aggressive state clean energy mandates (CA, OR, WA). But: PacifiCorp has massive legacy coal + wildfire liability. Transmission across the West is constrained.

CAISO (California)

Generators: Constellation/Calpine (Geysers + gas), NextEra, AES

Most aggressive clean energy policy in the nation. SB 100 mandates 100% clean by 2045. Excellent solar but evening ramp challenge. But: natural gas still critical for reliability. Calpine's Geysers geothermal is a clean firm anchor.

Key Findings from Fleet Analysis

1. Coal Retirement Is Low-Hanging Fruit

New solar+wind+storage is now cheaper than operating existing coal in most markets. The 5 coal-heavy generators (Evergy, AEP, BHE, Vistra, PPL) account for ~211 MT CO₂/year. Retiring their coal fleets could reduce total emissions by ~100+ MT CO₂/year at negative or minimal cost.5,8

2. Gas Is the Next Frontier — and Much Harder

After coal, natural gas is the dominant emitter (~45% of generation across these 15). But gas plants are more efficient, more flexible, and more critical for reliability. Decarbonizing gas requires CCS, clean firm alternatives, or very high storage deployment — all at 3–5x the $/ton of coal retirement.6,7

3. Nuclear Is the Cheapest Clean MWh

Existing nuclear plants produce electricity at $25–35/MWh — cheaper than any new clean generation source. Extending nuclear life and pursuing uprates is the single most cost-effective decarbonization strategy. Constellation's 23 GW nuclear fleet is an extraordinary clean energy asset.5,6

4. The Last 10–20% Is Exponentially Expensive

The CFE optimizer shows that going from 90% to 100% clean energy doubles or triples system cost in most regions. This last-mile problem is where long-duration storage, CCS, or new nuclear become necessary — and where policy support is most critical.7
