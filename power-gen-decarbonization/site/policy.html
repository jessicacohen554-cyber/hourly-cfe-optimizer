Core Principle

Affordability and Decarbonization Must Be Co-Optimized

The most durable decarbonization pathways are those that don't require customers to choose between clean energy and affordable bills. The IRA's clean energy tax credits have fundamentally changed this equation: in many markets, clean energy is now the cheapest new generation option, not just the cleanest.5,12

But "cheapest new build" doesn't mean "free transition." Stranded assets, grid integration costs, reliability investments, and community transition all carry real price tags. The policy question isn't whether to decarbonize — it's how to structure the transition so costs are manageable and fairly distributed.

Enabling Conditions: What Unlocks Affordable Ambition

1. IRA Tax Credits: The Single Largest Enabler

The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) provides an estimated $369 billion in clean energy tax incentives over 10 years. For power generators, three credits are transformative:12

  • Section 45Y (Clean Electricity PTC): ~$28/MWh for qualifying clean generation (wind, solar, nuclear, geothermal). Reduces LCOE by 30–40%.
  • Section 48E (Clean Electricity ITC): 30% investment tax credit for clean energy projects + 10% bonus for domestic content + 10% for energy communities.
  • Section 45Q (CCS Credit): $85/tonne CO₂ for geologic storage. Makes CCS economically viable on large CCGTs at ~50–60% capture rate.

Impact of IRA Credits on LCOE

Who benefits most: Coal-heavy generators gain the most because replacement renewables receive the full credit, making coal-to-clean substitution economically compelling. Gas+nuclear fleets benefit from 45Q (CCS) and 45Y (nuclear PTC) but face higher marginal costs.

Demand-Side Pull

Corporate Buyers as a Catalyst for CCS and Clean Firm Deployment

The emerging hourly clean energy accounting framework creates a new demand signal that conventional annual REC procurement never did: demand for dispatchable, carbon-free generation that can fill hours when wind and solar are insufficient. This demand-side pull could be the catalyst that makes CCS-equipped natural gas plants commercially viable at scale.

How Hourly Accounting Changes the CCS Business Case

Under annual matching, corporate buyers have no incentive to procure CCS-equipped generation. They can meet "100% renewable" targets by purchasing cheap daytime solar RECs to offset nighttime fossil consumption. The economics are simple: buy the cheapest REC available, regardless of when or where it was generated.

Hourly matching eliminates this arbitrage. When every hour of consumption must be matched with carbon-free generation produced in that same hour on the same grid, corporate buyers face a structural deficit during:

  • Evening/night hours — after solar sets and before wind picks up
  • Multi-day low-wind events — 3–7 day doldrums that exhaust battery storage
  • Winter mornings — high heating demand, low solar, variable wind
  • Seasonal troughs — particularly in northern ISOs with weak winter solar

These gaps can only be filled by dispatchable carbon-free generation: nuclear, geothermal, or CCS-equipped natural gas. For large loads in regions like PJM, ERCOT, and CAISO, CCS-CCGT may be the fastest-deployable option since it leverages existing gas infrastructure and workforce.

The demand-side pull mechanism: When SBTi requires 50% hourly matching by 2030 and 90% by 2040, companies with loads in the tens or hundreds of GWh will need to contract for nighttime clean firm generation. CCS-CCGT plants with 45Q tax credits ($85/tonne CO₂ stored) can offer clean firm power at $90–130/MWh — expensive, but potentially the only option for hourly matching during multi-day low-renewable periods in regions without geothermal or nuclear expansion options.

Who Drives This Demand?

Hyperscale Data Centers

Google (24/7 CFE by 2030), Microsoft (100% CFE by 2030), and Amazon (net-zero operations by 2040) collectively consume >50 TWh/year in the US — roughly equivalent to a mid-sized utility. Their hourly matching commitments create direct demand for clean firm resources in PJM (Virginia data center corridor), ERCOT (Texas), and CAISO. Multi-decade nuclear PPAs and data center-driven plant restarts demonstrate the scale of corporate demand-side pull for existing and new clean baseload generation.

Large Industrial Loads

SBTi’s phased hourly matching requirement (≥10 GWh in a single region) will initially capture approximately 500–1,000 large C&I consumers across the US. These companies have steady baseload demand profiles that can’t be fully matched with variable renewables alone. Their procurement of hourly-matched clean energy creates a market for CCS, advanced geothermal, and long-duration storage that doesn’t exist under annual accounting.

Federal Facilities

The US government joined the UN 24/7 CFE Compact at COP28 (Dec 2023). Federal facilities consume ~55 TWh/year. As the largest single buyer of electricity in the country, a federal shift to hourly matching would create immediate demand for clean firm generation across multiple ISOs — particularly in PJM (DoD installations in Virginia/Maryland) and ERCOT (military bases in Texas).

CCS deployment scale needed: If hourly matching targets drive even 10–15 GW of CCS-CCGT deployment by 2035, this would represent a transformative scaling of carbon capture technology. The learning-rate effects alone could reduce CCS costs by 20–30% (based on analogous 15% learning rates for solar and wind), making CCS competitive for broader grid applications beyond corporate procurement.7,15

How RPS/CES Creates EAC Market Value

State RPS mandates force load-serving entities (utilities, competitive suppliers) to procure Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) or similar EACs to demonstrate compliance. This creates a competitive market where clean generators earn revenue not just from energy sales, but from the environmental attribute value of their generation. In regions with aggressive state targets and limited supply, EAC prices can reach $20–50/MWh — transforming the economics of clean energy projects.

ISO-NE (New England)

Generators: Limited presence among top 15 (Vistra, partial Constellation)

Some of the most aggressive state RPS mandates in the country. Massachusetts (50% by 2030, 100% clean by 2050), Connecticut (zero-carbon by 2040), Rhode Island (100% renewable by 2033), Vermont (100% by 2035). These overlapping mandates create strong EAC demand in a transmission-constrained region with limited land for new renewables.

REC prices: Massachusetts Class I RECs traded at $30–55/MWh in recent years — among the highest in the nation. This premium makes offshore wind, new solar, and existing clean generation highly competitive.

Implication: Clean generators in New England earn substantial REC revenue that improves project economics. Nuclear (Millstone, Seabrook) receives state-level zero-emission credits in CT and NH that keep these plants economically viable.

PJM (Mid-Atlantic / Midwest)

Generators: Constellation, AEP, Dominion, PPL, Vistra

The largest US wholesale market spans 13 states with varying RPS ambitions. Virginia (VCEA: 100% by 2045), New Jersey (50% by 2030, moving to 100%), Maryland (50% RPS by 2030), and Illinois (100% by 2045) create strong clean energy demand. Pennsylvania has no binding RPS target beyond 2020.

EAC dynamics: PJM RECs trade at lower prices ($5–20/MWh) than New England because the region has more diverse supply. However, state-specific carve-outs (IL zero-emission credits, NJ offshore wind RECs) create segmented premium markets.

The PJM interconnection queue crisis: PJM’s queue is the most congested in the nation, with >800 GW of capacity waiting for interconnection studies — over 3x the region’s installed capacity. Median time from application to commercial operation exceeds 4 years, with a 77% withdrawal rate for projects submitted 2000–2019. In 2024, gas capacity in the PJM queue rose 72% year-over-year while solar and storage applications actually declined.

Impact on generators: Existing nuclear and clean generation in PJM has a structural advantage — it’s already interconnected and producing clean energy that earns EAC value. New competitors face years-long queues and uncertain interconnection costs (averaging $240/kW for PJM completed projects). This advantages incumbent clean generators but throttles new entrants, potentially constraining the pace at which PJM can achieve state clean energy targets.

FERC Order 1920 (2024) requires proactive long-range transmission planning, and PJM is transitioning to a “first-ready, first-served” cluster study model. But implementation will take years. In the interim, the queue bottleneck is the single largest constraint on PJM decarbonization.

CAISO (California)

Generators: Constellation (Geysers geothermal + gas), NextEra, AES

California’s SB 100 mandates 100% carbon-free retail electricity by 2045, with interim targets of 60% RPS by 2030. The state also has a cap-and-trade carbon market ($25–35/tonne) and aggressive procurement mandates for storage, offshore wind, and clean firm resources.

EAC dynamics: CA RPS compliance costs average ~$15–25/MWh. The state’s “portfolio content categories” tiering system creates premium value for in-state or directly-delivered clean energy (Category 1) vs. unbundled RECs (Category 3, which CPUC is phasing out).

CCS opportunity: California’s ~6 GW of merchant gas generation could be a candidate for CCS retrofit, particularly with 45Q credits and the state’s own CCS incentives (SB 905, 2022). The 725 MW Geysers geothermal complex — the world’s largest — provides a clean firm anchor that demonstrates what dispatchable zero-carbon generation looks like at scale.

ERCOT (Texas) & MISO/SPP (Central US)

Generators: Constellation, Vistra, AEP (ERCOT); Xcel, Evergy, DTE, WEC, Entergy (MISO/SPP)

ERCOT: No state RPS (Texas repealed its 2005 RPS after exceeding targets). Competitive energy-only market. Clean energy deployment is driven entirely by economics and voluntary corporate procurement — making Texas the purest test of demand-side pull. Corporate PPAs (Google, Amazon, Meta) account for a large share of recent solar+storage buildout.

MISO/SPP: Mixed RPS landscape. Minnesota (100% by 2040), Colorado (100% by 2040), Illinois (100% by 2045) create strong demand. Kansas, Missouri, and Indiana have weaker or no targets. Transmission constraints limit export of Great Plains wind to load centers — a bottleneck for wind-rich generators like Evergy and Xcel.

The Interaction: Hourly Matching + RPS/CES

Currently, all state RPS/CES policies use annual REC compliance. No state requires hourly matching. But the convergence of three forces will reshape EAC markets:

  1. GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision (expected 2027) will likely require hourly matching for corporate emissions reporting, creating a parallel demand channel for hourly EACs that runs alongside state RPS compliance.
  2. SBTi hourly matching requirements (starting 2030) will force ~8,000 companies with validated targets to procure time-matched clean energy — generating massive new demand for hourly EACs, particularly during high-demand, low-renewable hours.
  3. EU CBAM (binding from Jan 2026) requires time-matched clean electricity documentation for embedded emissions in goods exported to the EU. US manufacturers in energy-intensive industries will need hourly clean energy certification to maintain EU market access.

The dual-market future: Generators will increasingly earn revenue from two overlapping EAC streams: (1) annual RECs for state RPS compliance, and (2) hourly granular certificates (GCs) for corporate/international hourly matching requirements. Clean firm generators — nuclear, geothermal, CCS-CCGT — earn premium hourly EAC revenue because they produce during hours when variable renewables cannot. This creates a natural revenue premium for the very technologies needed for deep decarbonization.

EAC Market Dynamics

EAC Scarcity Under Hourly Matching: Which ISOs Face a Clean Premium?

If the GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision mandates hourly matching with ISO-level deliverability — and corporate buyers must procure within their load’s ISO — the critical question becomes: do any ISOs face EAC scarcity that drives up the clean energy premium? The answer varies dramatically by region.

The analysis below assumes corporate procurement occurs within the ISO where load is located, no incrementality constraint on voluntary claims (buyers can use existing clean resources), and a Standard Supply Service (SSS) baseline where state programs, nuclear, RPS, rate-base clean generation, and publicly owned assets are allocated pro-rata before voluntary procurement.

The Scarcity Framework

After state RPS mandates claim their share of regional EACs and existing clean resources are allocated to the SSS baseline, the remaining “uncommitted” clean energy supply becomes the pool available for voluntary corporate procurement. If corporate demand at a given participation rate exceeds this uncommitted supply, the region experiences EAC scarcity — prices rise, and corporate buyers face a “clean premium” above wholesale energy costs.

PJM: Likely Scarcity — The Tightest Market

Clean supply: ~345 TWh/yr clean generation (nuclear ~275, wind ~35, solar ~25, hydro ~10) against ~800 TWh total load.

RPS demand: State mandates in NJ, VA, MD, IL, PA collectively require ~120–150 TWh of RECs by 2030.

After SSS + RPS: Once SSS baseline allocation (~43% of load = ~345 TWh) and state RPS claims are netted out, virtually no uncommitted clean supply remains for voluntary corporate procurement. The entire existing clean fleet is already spoken for.

Nuclear squeeze: Excess nuclear after state programs is being rapidly consumed by data center PPAs. Constellation, Talen, and other nuclear operators have signed multi-GW long-term contracts with hyperscalers. If even 50% of announced data center demand materializes in PJM, the region faces genuine EAC scarcity at any hourly matching target above 50%.

PJM clean premium projection: At 10–20% voluntary corporate participation, PJM already faces tight supply. At 30%+ participation, EAC scarcity drives hourly clean premiums to $15–40/MWh during evening/night hours. At 50%+ participation, new-build clean resources are required — and PJM’s interconnection queue crisis means 3–5 year lead times before new capacity can relieve scarcity. PJM is the ISO where hourly matching creates the strongest price signal for CCS, new nuclear, and LDES.

ISO-NE: Moderate Scarcity — Transmission-Constrained

Clean supply: ~55 TWh/yr (nuclear ~27, hydro ~8, wind ~10, solar ~10) against ~120 TWh total load.

RPS demand: MA, CT, RI, VT mandates collectively require ~50–60 TWh of RECs by 2030 — consuming nearly the entire existing clean supply.

After SSS + RPS: Minimal surplus. Millstone and Seabrook nuclear (~27 TWh) are partially committed to state zero-emission credit programs. New England’s aggressive state mandates leave little room for voluntary corporate procurement without new build.

Key constraint: Transmission limitations restrict import of Canadian hydro (HQ) and new offshore wind faces permitting and interconnection delays. The Vineyard Wind 1 experience illustrates the challenge of scaling offshore delivery.

CAISO: Moderate Scarcity — The Evening Gap

Clean supply: ~130 TWh/yr clean (solar ~55, wind ~20, geothermal ~12, hydro ~25, nuclear ~18 [Diablo until 2030]) against ~280 TWh total load.

RPS demand: SB 100 mandates 60% RPS by 2030 = ~170 TWh. Current clean supply already falls short of the RPS mandate, driving California’s aggressive procurement of new solar+storage.

The duck curve problem: California has abundant daytime clean energy but faces a structural deficit from 5 PM to 8 AM. Under hourly matching, this evening/night gap becomes the binding constraint. Even with 12+ GW of battery storage, multi-day low-wind events create hours that require clean firm (geothermal, CCS) or imported clean energy.

CCS opportunity: California’s ~6 GW gas fleet could provide hourly-matched clean firm if equipped with CCS. With 45Q credits + state CCS incentives (SB 905), this could be the fastest path to filling the evening gap and creating a new revenue stream for gas generators.

ERCOT: Unlikely Scarcity — Abundant and Growing Supply

Clean supply: ~150 TWh/yr clean (wind ~95, solar ~35, nuclear ~20) against ~470 TWh total load. But supply is growing rapidly: ERCOT interconnected ~15 GW of solar in 2024 alone.

RPS demand: Texas has no binding RPS. All corporate procurement is voluntary — driven by Google, Amazon, Meta, and other hyperscalers with major Texas operations.

After SSS baseline: Even after pro-rata allocation of existing clean resources (~32% clean supply ratio), ERCOT’s explosive wind+solar growth means corporate buyers face abundant daytime EAC supply at low cost. The constraint is evening/night hours, but South Plains wind produces heavily at night, partially filling this gap.

ERCOT may never see EAC scarcity for corporate buyers, even without an incrementality constraint. The combination of no state RPS (so no mandatory REC demand competing with voluntary buyers), rapid renewable deployment driven by favorable economics, and abundant wind+solar resources means EAC supply is likely to grow faster than corporate demand for the foreseeable future. ERCOT is the market where hourly matching is most achievable at the lowest clean premium — potentially $0–5/MWh for daytime hours and $5–15/MWh for nighttime. This makes ERCOT attractive for corporate load placement decisions.

NYISO: Moderate Scarcity — Hydro-Anchored but Constrained

Clean supply: ~75 TWh/yr clean (hydro ~30, nuclear ~30, wind ~8, solar ~7) against ~155 TWh total load.

RPS demand: CLCPA mandates 70% renewable by 2030, 100% zero-emission by 2040. Current trajectory: ~60% clean by 2030.

Key dynamics: Champlain Hudson Power Express (CHPE) will deliver ~10 TWh/yr of Canadian hydro to NYC by 2026. Indian Point closure (2021) removed ~17 TWh of zero-emission nuclear. NYC load zone (Zone J) faces the tightest supply-demand balance. Offshore wind pipeline (Empire Wind, Sunrise Wind) is critical but faces cost escalation and permitting uncertainty.

Voluntary Markets

Voluntary Procurement Under Hourly Matching: The Standard Supply Service Framework

The GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision introduces a concept that resolves a fundamental tension in clean energy accounting: Standard Supply Service (SSS). This framework determines how existing clean resources are credited before voluntary corporate procurement begins.

How Standard Supply Service Works

Under the proposed SSS framework, every electricity consumer in a region automatically receives a pro-rata share of the region’s existing clean energy baseline. This baseline includes:

  • State nuclear programs — zero-emission credits in IL, NY, CT, NJ, OH
  • RPS-driven generation — renewables built to satisfy state mandates
  • Rate-base clean generation — utility-owned clean assets in regulated territories
  • Publicly owned assets — federal hydropower (BPA, TVA, WAPA), municipal utilities

This means a corporate buyer in PJM with a 90% hourly matching target doesn’t start from zero. They start from the ~43% SSS baseline and need to procure the additional 47 percentage points through voluntary contracts. The SSS baseline is allocated hourly — reflecting the actual production profile of existing clean resources.

The Clean Premium by ISO

EAC scarcity translates directly into a “clean premium” — the additional cost above wholesale energy prices that corporate buyers pay for hourly-matched clean energy. This premium varies by ISO, time of day, and participation level:

PJM: $15–40/MWh

Highest clean premium in the US. Evening/night hours face structural deficit. Nuclear PPAs with data centers are removing existing supply from the voluntary market. At 30%+ corporate participation, PJM buyers will need to contract for new-build clean firm at premium prices. PJM is where CCS-CCGT and LDES earn their highest revenue.

ISO-NE: $20–55/MWh

Already-high Massachusetts Class I REC prices ($30–55/MWh) indicate existing scarcity. Adding hourly matching on top of aggressive state mandates pushes premiums even higher. Limited land area and transmission constraints mean new supply is expensive and slow to build.

CAISO: $10–25/MWh

Daytime surplus keeps average premium low. Evening/night hours (5 PM–8 AM) face $20–40/MWh premiums. Battery storage partially mitigates but cannot cover multi-day low-wind events. Geothermal expansion could moderate nighttime premiums.

ERCOT: $0–15/MWh

Lowest clean premium. Abundant and rapidly growing wind+solar. No RPS competing for EACs. Nighttime wind fills much of the evening gap. Even at 50% corporate participation, ERCOT’s supply growth likely outpaces demand. The most cost-effective ISO for corporate hourly matching.

The investment signal: The regional variation in clean premiums creates a powerful location signal for both corporate load placement and generator investment. Companies choosing where to build new data centers or manufacturing plants will factor in hourly matching costs — favoring ERCOT and CAISO over PJM and ISO-NE for cost-sensitive loads. Generators in PJM that invest in CCS or clean firm capacity now will capture the premium as corporate demand grows. The clean premium is the market mechanism that connects voluntary corporate ambition to physical generator decarbonization investment.

Barriers: What Makes Targets Difficult or Too Costly

Stranded Asset Risk

Coal and gas plants with remaining book value create financial pressure to continue operating. Regulated utilities recover capital through rate base — early retirement means writing off undepreciated assets, which requires regulatory approval and may increase rates. Estimated stranded asset exposure for the 15 generators: $50–80 billion.

Reliability Mandates

NERC’s 2025 Long-Term Reliability Assessment finds 13 of 23 assessment areas face elevated or high resource adequacy risk over 5 years, including MISO, PJM, and ERCOT. Summer peak demand could surge by 224 GW (69% more than prior projection). Meanwhile, 122,000 MW of dispatchable generation is retiring over the next decade. Winter Storm Uri (2021) and Elliott (2022) reinforce the political case for retaining gas plants.21

Load Growth

After two decades of flat electricity demand, the US is experiencing unprecedented load growth from data centers, AI computing, EVs, and industrial electrification. Some utilities project 4–6% annual growth. Growth means generators must decarbonize a moving target — absolute reductions are harder when the denominator is growing.

Political & Regulatory Headwinds

Coal communities resist plant closures. State legislatures in fossil-fuel-dependent states pass laws restricting early retirement. EPA regulations face legal challenges. IRA tax credit repeal risk creates investment uncertainty. Permitting reform stalls in Congress.

Rate Impact Concerns

Regulated utilities must demonstrate that capital investments are prudent and in ratepayers' interest. Large-scale clean energy investment can increase rate base and, temporarily, customer bills. Affordability is a political constraint even when long-term economics favor transition.

Supply Chain & Workforce

Solar panel manufacturing is concentrated in China (with tariff and trade policy implications). Transformer and grid equipment lead times are 2–4 years. Skilled workforce for clean energy construction is constrained. These practical bottlenecks slow deployment even when economics are favorable.

The planning regression (RMI Q4 2025): Utility integrated resource plans are moving in the wrong direction. Planned wind+solar by 2035 has declined from 280 GW (end 2023) to 226 GW (end 2025). Planned new gas has risen from 60 GW to 100 GW. The projected 2035 CO₂ reduction (vs 2005) has deteriorated from −60% to −45%—a 15 percentage-point regression in just two years. Battery storage is the sole bright spot: 68 GW planned, up 7 GW year-over-year.

Strategic Recommendations

Catalytic Policy Positions: What Should Generators Advocate For?

If power generators want to take policy positions that accelerate least-cost decarbonization — benefiting both their bottom line and the climate — here's what the analysis suggests by fleet archetype:

Coal-Heavy Generators Should Advocate For:

  • Securitization legislation — Allows utilities to issue low-cost bonds to pay off stranded coal assets, reducing ratepayer impact of early retirement
  • Transmission buildout — Connects wind/solar-rich regions to load centers, enabling coal replacement
  • Community transition funding — Reduces political opposition to coal plant closures
  • IRA credit preservation — 45Y/48E credits make replacement renewables cheaper than coal operations

Gas-Dominant Generators Should Advocate For:

  • 45Q CCS credit enhancement — Higher or extended CCS credits make gas+capture competitive
  • Clean firm technology R&D — Geothermal, advanced nuclear (SMR), long-duration storage development
  • Dispatchable clean energy recognition — Capacity market reforms that value clean dispatchable resources
  • Methane reduction rules — Reduces upstream Scope 3 and demonstrates credibility

Nuclear-Led Generators Should Advocate For:

  • 45U nuclear PTC preservation — Ensures existing fleet economics remain strong
  • License extension beyond 80 years — NRC framework for extended operation
  • Nuclear in clean energy standards — Recognition as zero-emission baseload in CES/RPS
  • SMR permitting reform — Streamlined NRC review for standardized designs

All Generators Should Advocate For:

  • Permitting reform — Faster NEPA review, transmission siting authority for federal corridors
  • Interconnection queue reform — FERC action on serial study process, "first-ready, first-served" models
  • Technology-neutral clean energy standard — Rewards all zero-emission generation equally
  • Long-term policy certainty — 10–15 year credit horizons to de-risk capital deployment
